**Thesis.** Part III opens with the general concept: bad faith is calculated obstruction disguised as sincerity — distinguishable from honest disagreement by intent — with a recognizable tactic repertoire: motivated misinterpretation, double standards, strategic outrage, goalpost shifting, bait-and-switch, feigned curiosity, deflection. Its systemic cost is the currency of discourse itself (trust erodes, sincere participants exit, communities fragment), and the counter-protocol is naming the tactic for observers, disengaging, and redirecting to sincere audiences. Two case studies at two scales: the personal (the Rowling treatment — nuanced sex-based-rights positions deliberately distorted into "anti-trans" caricature, aimed at deterring bystanders, not persuading the target) and the institutional (the NPR/Marist political-violence poll — an engineered ambiguity whose three linguistic tricks convert a moral question into a sentimental one: "pollsters don't measure beliefs; they design them").

**Sources.** bad-faith (Aug 2025): the taxonomy anchor. silencing-through-stigma: the motivated-misinterpretation case; three chilling consequences (including the erosion of genuine trans advocacy). the-semantics-of-violence (Oct 2025): diffused agency, implied necessity, normative framing; the falsifiable prediction (swap "Americans" for "people like me" and support collapses); the neutral counter-formulation.
