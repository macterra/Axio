**Thesis.** Part IV opens with the mechanism kit. The Boghossian Principle is the chapter's law: "A system that forbids open conversation about its own existential problems is a system unworthy of preservation, because it has already guaranteed its own failure" — and captured institutions become cargo cults, scholarship's rituals without its error-correcting substance. The engine is Kuran's preference falsification: motivated minorities seize gatekeeping positions while a passive majority publicly endorses what it privately rejects — capture without genuine support, hence unstable. The demand side: the epistemic vacuum — relativism creates the craving for moral certainty that authoritarian frames supply ("if you believe everything is about power, you're going to want power" — held to correlation-caution, with six rival hypotheses on the table). And the psychology: ideology as coping — a rare public de-conversion from Marxism read carefully for what it gets right (Moloch not conspiracy; classes as bad predictors; political belief as self-esteem maintenance, true of all camps) and the fatal error it retains (treating poverty as caused rather than as humanity's default).
