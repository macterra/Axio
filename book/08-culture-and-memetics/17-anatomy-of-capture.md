You can measure an institution by the questions it will not let you ask. A physics department that punished skepticism about its foundational assumptions would not be a physics department; it would be a shrine that happened to own a particle accelerator. Yet whole faculties now operate exactly this way. Ask openly whether a diversity initiative achieves what it claims, or whether a theoretical commitment to multiculturalism has ever been tested against a result, and you learn quickly that the question is not permitted. The reaction is not counter-argument. It is the machinery of heresy — the raised eyebrow, the quiet exclusion, the note in your file.

Peter Boghossian named the law that governs such places. In its distilled form it reads:

> A system that forbids open conversation about its own existential problems is a system unworthy of preservation, because it has already guaranteed its own failure.

I take this as the founding principle of the theory of capture, and Part IV is its unfolding. Notice what the principle does not say. It does not say that a captured institution holds false beliefs, or corrupt people, or bad values — those may or may not be true, and are hard to establish from outside. It says something stronger and structurally verifiable: that a system which has closed off the conversation about its own failure modes has removed the only mechanism by which a system corrects error. Whether or not it is wrong today, it can no longer discover that it is wrong tomorrow. It has traded its immune system for the comfort of never feeling ill. The prognosis follows from the anatomy.

This chapter names the parts of that anatomy — the law, the engine, the demand, and the psychology. It is the opening of Part IV, and it is deliberately a kit rather than a machine. The full mechanism of capture, the account of *how attention itself becomes the terrain that captured institutions fight over*, is [The Forbidden Pattern](21-the-forbidden-pattern.md), the capstone this part builds toward. Here I am laying out the components and showing that each one already appeared, in isolation, before anyone had assembled them.

## Cargo Cults

Start with what a captured institution looks like from the inside, because it does not look captured. It looks busy. It holds conferences. It runs peer review, awards grants, mints citations, and generates specialized vocabulary at an impressive rate. Every visible ritual of scholarship is present and correct.

This is precisely the tell. The Pacific cargo cults built runways, control towers of bamboo, and headsets of carved wood, and then waited for the planes full of goods to land — reproducing every observable feature of the airfields they had seen, in the belief that the ritual was the cause. They had copied the form and lost the function. The wartime airfields had produced cargo because they were nodes in a vast productive system; the ritual replicas produced nothing because the system was the point and the ritual was only its shadow.

A captured faculty is a cargo cult in exactly this sense. Genuine scholarship has an outward form — the peer review, the jargon, the citations — and an inward function, which is error correction: relentless self-examination, the active hunt for one's own mistakes, the willingness to let a result overturn a commitment. The rituals exist to serve that function. A cargo-cult discipline keeps the rituals and discards the function. Its peer review filters for conformity rather than for error. Its complexity obscures rather than illuminates — sophisticated-seeming methodology deployed to protect a conclusion instead of to test it. Its dogmatism punishes the dissenter, which is the one move a truth-seeking enterprise can never afford, because the dissenter is the error-correcting signal arriving in person.

This is why the Boghossian Principle is not moralizing. Scholarship is one of the [truth machines](../02-conditionalism/05-truth-machines.md) Volume 2 described — an institution whose entire value lies in its capacity to be wrong out loud and fix it. Strip out that capacity and you have not damaged the machine; you have replaced it with a costume of itself. The runway is immaculate. No planes are coming.

## The Engine

If capture guaranteed its own failure, and everyone could see it, it would be a curiosity rather than a crisis. The hard question is how these systems arise and persist while so few of the people inside them actually believe in them. Here the mechanism is not conviction. It is silence.

Jonathan Kay, writing about the rise and expected fall of social-justice ideology in Canadian institutions, sketched the recipe in three parts. First, a highly motivated minority — a small number of ideologically driven people willing to spend real effort — captures the key positions: the committees, the accreditation bodies, the professional associations, the offices that write the rules. Second, a passive majority acquiesces, mostly because it mistakes the early policies for harmless symbolism, something to nod through rather than fight. Third, a collective-action problem locks the arrangement in place. Kay's example was nursing students who privately regard the mandated ideological content as nonsense and say nothing, because each of them correctly calculates that speaking up carries a real personal cost — a grade, a placement, a reference — while the benefit of dislodging the policy is diffuse and shared with everyone who stayed quiet.

The name for this is Timur Kuran's *preference falsification*: people publicly endorse what they privately reject, to avoid the penalties attached to honesty. And the decisive feature of a system built on preference falsification is that it can command near-total public compliance while resting on almost no genuine support. The gap between the private distribution of belief and the public distribution is the whole of the structure. Everyone complies; hardly anyone is convinced; and because each person sees only the compliance and not the private dissent, each concludes that they are the lonely exception, which makes them comply harder.

Two things follow. The first is that such a regime is far weaker than it appears. Its strength is a statistical illusion assembled out of individual fears, and it is brittle in a specific way — Kuran's own point was that these equilibria can collapse with startling speed once the private distribution becomes visible, because the moment enough people learn they are the majority, the cost calculation inverts and the falsified preferences unfalsify all at once. Capture without genuine support is capture living on borrowed time. The second is grimmer and is why the collapse is not automatic: as long as the falsification holds, the motivated minority governs, and the passive majority's belief that the policies are "purely symbolic" is exactly the anesthetic that lets the capture proceed. Symbolism is how capture enters. It is rarely how it stays.

## The Vacuum

The engine explains how capture spreads through a population once an ideology is present. It does not explain the demand — why the ideologies that get captured tend to be the ones they are, or why they attract the temperament they attract. For that we have to look at what the captured belief supplies to the person who holds it.

There is a suggestive finding here, and it needs to be handled with care. Deverson et al. (2025) reported a statistically significant correlation between postmodern beliefs — skepticism toward objective truth, toward reason, toward science, and the view of reality as socially constructed and structured by power — and higher scores on measures of left-wing authoritarianism. Claire Lehmann compressed the intuition to a sentence: *if you believe everything is about power, you're going to want power.* The logic has a certain awful cleanness. If truth is not a thing that can arbitrate between us, then persuasion is a fiction and only leverage remains; a person who has genuinely absorbed that view has no reason left not to reach for the lever.

But a correlation is not a mechanism, and this one admits at least six readings, only one of which is the causal story Lehmann's sentence implies. It could be shared psychological traits — a temperament high in the need for cognitive and emotional security drawn independently to both postmodernism and authoritarianism. It could be environment — an institutional culture that rewards conformity to both at once, so that the correlation lives in the water supply rather than in any individual head. It could be the epistemic vacuum: relativism dissolves the ground under a person's feet, the dissonance is intolerable, and an authoritarian frame is attractive precisely because it hands back the moral certainty that the relativism took away. It could be identity dynamics — a focus on power and marginalized identity that makes coercive methods feel like justified self-defense rather than aggression. It could be a methodological artifact — overlapping survey constructs, or respondents smoothing themselves toward ideological consistency. Or it could run backward entirely: an authoritarian disposition that already wanted power, reaching for postmodernism afterward as the philosophy that most conveniently licenses it.

I do not know which of these dominates, and neither does anyone else yet; the honest verdict waits on longitudinal work and finer instruments. Volume 2's [discipline of holding a claim to its evidence](../02-conditionalism/05-truth-machines.md) applies here to my own side of the argument as much as to anyone's — the Lehmann sentence is a hypothesis with a real correlation behind it, not a demonstrated cause, and I will not launder the one into the other. What survives the caution is the third reading, the epistemic vacuum, because it names a demand that any of the others would still have to satisfy. A person cannot live indefinitely in "nothing is true." The relativism is not a resting state; it is a wound, and wounds recruit whatever will close them. This is why the frames that thrive in a relativized culture are the ones offering total moral certainty — they are supplying to a demand the culture manufactured. The political form this takes, the way an epistemic vacuum resolves into an [authoritarian default](../07-liberty-and-governance/23-the-authoritarian-default.md), is Volume 7's subject. Here the point is cultural: capture finds its readiest hosts in minds that have been hollowed out and left craving.

## The Coping

There is a last part to name, and it is the most human. Underneath the law, the engine, and the vacuum sits a fact about what ideology is *for* in the individual who carries it — and the clearest window onto it is a document in which someone lost their ideology in public.

The essay *Of Marx and Moloch* is a rare artifact: a committed Marxist setting out to prove to a skeptical audience that socialism was the real root-cause solution to global poverty, and instead, methodically and honestly, dismantling their own case in real time. It is worth reading for the intellectual courage alone. It is worth studying for what the de-conversion reveals, and for the one error it does not manage to shed.

What it gets right is a compact education. Knowing the root cause of a problem is not the same as having a feasible plan to fix it — most utopian arguments stop at diagnosis and never survive contact with implementation. Academic sociology, the author found, was either too abstract to use or too unfalsifiable to trust. Social outcomes are the work of Moloch, not of conspiracy: misaligned incentives, competition, and coordination failure produce the pattern that conspiratorial thinking wrongly attributes to a coordinating cabal — and there is no cabal, which is worse, because you cannot depose a coordination failure. Classes turn out to be poor predictors of behavior, since variation within any class swamps the average and the working class never was the coherent revolutionary agent the theory needed it to be. And — the observation that opens onto everything else — political belief functions largely as psychological maintenance, a way of protecting self-esteem and reducing dissonance rather than a truth-seeking process.

That last insight is the load-bearing one, and its power is that it is symmetrical. The author saw it about socialism, but it is not true only of socialists. It is true of libertarians, of conservatives, of Effective Altruists, of me, of anyone whose sense of being a good and intelligent person is bound up with the political picture they hold. This is the psychological substrate the whole of Part IV rests on. Ideology is comfort before it is analysis, and the [black-hole geometry](06-the-geometry-of-culture.md) of a captured worldview — the way it curves the intellectual space around it so that no evidence ever quite reaches escape velocity — is possible only because the belief is doing a job that has nothing to do with being true. You do not defend your identity's foundations the way you defend a hypothesis. The [pathogens](07-memetic-pathogens.md) that colonize such minds are exploiting a real service the host is grateful to receive.

And then the error the de-conversion could not shed, because it was too deep to see: the author still treated poverty as something capitalism *causes*. That is the definitional mistake, and it survives the loss of every other Marxist commitment because it was never really an economic claim — it was the moral shape of the whole worldview. Poverty is not created by a system. It is the default state of humanity; it is what you get when nothing is done. Wealth is the anomaly that must be generated, coordinated, and preserved by ongoing effort, and a system can either foster that generation or fail to. The reframing changes what there is to fix. If you believe capitalism causes poverty, dismantling it feels like the solution; if you understand that poverty is the baseline and wealth the built thing, dismantling the mechanism that builds wealth without a better one to replace it is just scuttling your own lifeboat. The full economic case — that [poverty is the baseline, not the injury](../06-markets-and-money/07-the-poverty-myth.md), and that [wealth is a process rather than a pile](../06-markets-and-money/09-wealth-is-not-a-pile.md) to be redistributed — belongs to Volume 6, and I will not re-argue it here. What matters for the anatomy of capture is the pattern the case exhibits: a person can shed almost every conclusion of an ideology and still carry its founding error, because the error was doing the psychological work, and the conclusions were only its decorations.

## The Kit

Four parts, then, laid on the table. The *law* — a system that forbids the conversation about its own failure has already failed, and captured institutions become cargo cults, all the ritual of scholarship with the error correction cut out. The *engine* — preference falsification, motivated minorities holding the gates while a passive majority publicly endorses what it privately rejects, which yields command without conviction and a strength that is real until the day it isn't. The *vacuum* — the relativized mind, hollowed of certainty and craving its return, supplying the demand that authoritarian frames rush to meet. And the *coping* — ideology as self-esteem maintenance, comfort mistaken for analysis, an error kept long after its conclusions are gone because the error was the thing that felt like home.

None of these is capture. Capture is what happens when all four engage the same institution at once, and the account of that engagement — the terrain they fight over, the reason the fight is invisible to the people losing it — is still ahead. What this chapter establishes is that the components are not exotic. Each was visible on its own, in ordinary institutions, before anyone assembled them into a mechanism. That is the unsettling part. The parts of capture are lying around in plain sight, in every faculty that has stopped asking the forbidden question, waiting to be fitted together.
