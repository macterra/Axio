The previous chapter dismantled a discrimination claim. [Parity Where It Pays](23-parity-where-it-pays.md) took the demand for gender balance in prestigious fields and showed that the outcomes it treats as injustice are mostly the arithmetic of freedom: give people more latitude and sex-typical sorting widens rather than closes, which is why nobody marches for equality in the morgue. That is the kind of result that makes a reader nervous. A framework that keeps arriving at *no injustice here* starts to look less like a method and more like a verdict it reached before the evidence. If every claim of discrimination dissolves under examination, the examination is rigged.

So it matters that the same framework, run honestly, sometimes returns the opposite answer. The test of an adjudicating instrument is not that it acquits — it is that it convicts when the facts convict. Here is a case where they do. Rosalind Franklin was genuinely wronged in the discovery of DNA's structure, and saying so is not a concession to the grievance economy the last chapter criticized. It is the same discipline producing a different result because the facts are different. What makes the two chapters a pair is not that they agree; it is that they were decided by one procedure. This is the [grey-zone discipline](../07-liberty-and-governance/05-the-grey-zone.md) — read the easy ends off directly, and adjudicate the middle by asking what would have to be true — turned from political theory onto a historical record.

Before the facts, the standard. What would have to be true for a discrimination claim to earn a conviction rather than a dismissal? Three conditions, and they are the same ones the previous chapter applied and found unmet. There must be a genuine deprivation, not merely an unequal outcome that voluntary sorting explains. The deprivation must trace to the person's group membership, not to a difference in what they did or produced. And the mechanism must be identifiable — a concealment, an exclusion, a consent that was overridden — rather than left as an inference from the disparity itself, which is where most weak claims live and die. In the parity case, no condition held: the outcomes were sorting, not deprivation; they tracked interests, not exclusion; and no mechanism survived inspection. The Franklin case is worth the chapter precisely because all three conditions are met and can be shown to be met, which is what distinguishes an adjudicated wrong from an asserted one.

## The Facts, Read Off Directly

Franklin's X-ray diffraction images gave the double helix its empirical spine. The most famous of them, Photo 51 — taken by her PhD student Raymond Gosling — showed the helical form with a clarity that made the structure legible to anyone who understood the crystallography. Maurice Wilkins showed that photograph to James Watson without Franklin's knowledge or consent. At nearly the same time, Max Perutz passed an internal Medical Research Council report, one that summarized Franklin's unpublished measurements, to Francis Crick. The data were not stamped confidential, but they were hers, and they were not yet published.

Watson and Crick built their model from that material. When the model appeared in *Nature* in 1953, the paper thanked Franklin and Wilkins in a single perfunctory line, crediting "the general nature of the unpublished results." The vagueness was not an accident of style. It gestured at propriety while concealing dependence — acknowledging that unpublished work existed without conceding that the model could not have been built without it.

Four features of the record are worth stating plainly, because the verdict rests on their accumulation rather than on any one of them.

First, consent and attribution. Franklin did not agree to the use of her data, and the model could not have been assembled without her diffraction parameters. By any standard of collaboration we would now recognize, that is an ethical lapse. Circulating internal reports may have been ordinary practice; ordinary practice does not discharge the duty to credit an originator transparently, and the studied vagueness of the acknowledgment shows that the authors themselves felt the duty they were evading.

Second, power asymmetry. Franklin worked inside a male-dominated hierarchy at King's College, where Wilkins treated her as a subordinate rather than the peer she was. The Cambridge laboratory that Watson and Crick occupied carried higher status, easier publication channels, and the freedom to chase theory over experiment. The system rewarded charisma and connection — resources the two men had in abundance and Franklin was structurally denied.

Third, gender and reputation. Watson's later memoir, *The Double Helix*, compounded the wrong by casting Franklin as a dour, difficult woman — "Rosy," a nickname she never used — and offering her personality as the explanation for her exclusion. That portrait shaped decades of public memory. The sexism of the 1950s was not incidental background; it was a structural bias that lowered her standing in life and in legacy.

Fourth, credit and consequence. When the Nobel Prize went to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins in 1962, Franklin was already dead, and the prize is not awarded posthumously. Even alive, she would have faced a committee that reliably favored model-builders over data-gatherers. But precedent is not a moral defense; that the committee had always slighted data-gatherers describes the bias, it does not excuse it. Without her data there is no double helix — only conjecture waiting for a photograph.

## Granting the Defense in Full

An honest adjudication does not stack the record. The case for Watson and Crick has real content, and I will state it at full strength rather than the strawman strength that makes refutation easy.

Franklin had presented much of her data in seminars that Watson attended, so the argument that he stole a secret overstates the secrecy — some of what he used, she had shown to a room that included him. The MRC report was an internal document, not a sealed one; sharing it violated no explicit prohibition, because none had been posted. And all the parties published in the same 1953 issue of *Nature*: Franklin's own paper followed the Watson–Crick model, laying out the experimental foundation under her name, so she was not written out of the printed record entirely.

Each of these is true, and each does real work. Together they demolish the maximalist story — the one in which two men crept into a locked drawer, lifted a stolen photograph, and buried the woman they robbed. That story is false, and a framework that cannot tell it apart from the true one is worthless. This is the same move the previous chapter made in the other direction: grant the steelman completely, then measure exactly how much of the claim survives it.

What survives is this. Permission once given in a lecture is not carte blanche for appropriation; showing data to colleagues is not the same as licensing them to build your unpublished result into their own priority claim. An internal report being technically shareable does not make routing it to a competitor without the author's knowledge a neutral act. And citation without specificity is not acknowledgment — a line vague enough to hide the dependence it names is closer to concealment than to credit. The counterarguments soften the case. They do not erase it. They move the verdict from theft to something more diffuse and, in a way, more damning, because it cannot be laid at the feet of two bad men and dismissed with them.

## The Verdict, Calibrated

Watson and Crick did not steal Franklin's data. They exploited a permissive culture that made the borrowing possible and the concealment comfortable. Their conduct was unfair because it relied on information not freely offered, hid the dependency behind a vague line, and let systemic bias do the work of suppressing her recognition without anyone having to intend that outcome. The injustice lives less in a single act of theft than in the seamless fit between individual ambition and institutional sexism — a fit so smooth that each man could have believed, sincerely, that he had done nothing wrong.

That is the calibrated verdict, and calibration is the whole point. The maximalist reading — deliberate villainy, a robbery — is refuted by the defense. The dismissive reading — ordinary practice, no harm, a woman who published her own paper after all — is refuted by the consent violation, the concealment, and the structural bias that turned an ambiguous act into a decades-long erasure. The truth is the interlaced middle, and a framework worth trusting has to be able to name it without collapsing toward either pole to make the story simpler.

Notice what the honest verdict costs. It denies you the satisfaction of a villain and the comfort of an acquittal in the same breath. There is no one to cancel and no one to exonerate — only a system that made good outcomes for some depend on the quiet dispossession of others, and men who prospered inside it without ever having to choose cruelty. That is harder to hold than either myth, and holding it is what adjudication is for.

History cannot reassign the prize, but it can rebalance the narrative. Franklin was not a supporting character in the discovery of DNA. She was one of its authors, and that it took decades to say so is itself part of the record. There is a temptation, especially once the principals are dead, to separate the science from the men and sanitize the legacy until the textbooks read clean. That would be another distortion. The discovery was not a moment of pure reason; it was a human event, tangled in ambition, hierarchy, and prejudice, and to remember it honestly is to remember all of it — the light and the shadow interlaced. Watson's own life supplied the caption for the whole episode: the presumption that insight excuses empathy, the belief that grasping a pattern others missed licenses overlooking the people whose work made the pattern visible.

**History does not owe forgiveness, only clarity.** The molecule is indifferent to fairness; we are not, and the whole reason to run the procedure is that our judgments are supposed to track something the molecule cannot. This is where the pairing with the next chapter turns. [The Rituals of Absolution](25-the-rituals-of-absolution.md) examines the machinery that grows up when a culture wants the feeling of having addressed a historical wrong without the labor of adjudicating one — the acknowledgment that gestures at propriety while changing nothing, which is exactly what the *Nature* line did in miniature. Adjudication and ritual are the two things a society can do with an inherited injustice. One of them asks what actually happened and says so. The other performs having asked. The Franklin case is what the first one looks like when it convicts, and holding it up is the surest way to see what the second one is missing.
