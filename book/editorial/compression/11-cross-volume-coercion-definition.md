# Cross-Volume Coercion Definition Pass

Date: July 21, 2026

## Selection and scope

The first cross-volume Phase 7 inventory searched for repeated canonical definitions, signature examples, conclusions, and transition formulas. Its strongest exact match was the coercion definition: after normalizing the optional article in “a material setback,” the complete formula appeared fourteen times across seven volumes and the glossary.

Frequency alone did not decide the edit. The test was whether a full recital performed local classificatory work or merely interrupted an argument whose reader had already been given the definition.

## Protected-material ledger

- Preserve Volume 5 Chapter 9's complete definition, four elements, mugger and bluff cases, Credence analysis, and force/violence/speech boundaries.
- Preserve every full formulation that introduces a volume-local taxonomy or compares coercion with adjacent categories.
- Preserve the edited chapters' front matter, headings, cross-reference targets, examples, substantive claims, objections, and qualifications.
- Preserve the mechanism in every shortened use; a bare synonym or unexplained label is not an adequate bridge.
- Do not weaken the Phase 5 taxonomy: harm, coercion, force, violence, consent, wrongfulness, and legitimacy remain distinct.

## Line decisions

- Volume 3 Chapter 34 now states what coercion does in the intelligence/power comparison instead of restating all four defining elements one chapter after Volume 3's full boundary.
- Volume 5 Chapter 12 explicitly inherits classification from Chapter 9 and moves directly to its own question: possible grounds and safeguards for justification.
- Volume 5 Chapter 14 links the term and keeps the reason it matters to the utilitarianism critique: anticipated setback is used to control agency.
- Volume 7 Chapter 5 inherits the immediately preceding EFO application and turns directly from classification to its assigned legitimacy question.
- Volume 7 Chapter 23 links a credible coercive threat directly to the psychology of submission rather than pausing for another definition.
