# Phase 7 Cross-Volume Conditionalism Recap Audit

Baseline: July 22, 2026, after the calibrated boldness-recovery review.

## Scope

This audit tests the repetition-ledger instruction to compress later defenses of “all truth is conditional.” The canonical argument remains in Volume 2, Chapters 2–4. The audit examined direct repetitions of that formula, claims about truth or interpretation requiring conditions, “view from nowhere” language, and compact invocations of Conditionalism outside the canonical sequence.

The ownership test was stricter than phrase matching:

1. Does the later passage repeat the canonical defense, or does it identify conditions specific to a new domain?
2. Does an independently readable volume need a short bridge before applying the framework?
3. Would replacing the passage with a bare link remove a premise required by the local argument?
4. Does repeated wording perform a different structural job within its chapter?

## Inventory

### Reader and volume orientation

The book introduction, Volume 1's genealogy, Volume 3's cybernetic lineage, and the Volume 6 introduction each use one compact formulation to locate a later volume in the book-wide architecture. The glossary routes readers to the canonical chapters. These are orientation bridges, not repeated defenses.

**Decision:** Retain.

### Physics and metaphysics

Volume 1 Chapter 20 uses conditionality to connect structured claims to the proposed regress of frameworks beneath the Chaos construction. Chapter 25 states the conditions under which structural regularities and observer-relative laws hold. Chapter 26 applies the principle to the CTMU's claim of tautological necessity. Chapter 28 makes its proposed dissolution of absolute nothingness explicitly conditional on a structured modal space. Each passage identifies the local burden being tested rather than replaying Volume 2's absolutism and relativism arguments.

**Decision:** Retain.

### Models, minds, and alignment

Volume 3 Chapter 2 specializes truth's conditionality to model adequacy for a purpose, while Chapter 3 uses conditional interpretation to separate a thermostat's physical control model from intentional attribution. Volume 4 Chapters 2–3 use it to delimit the formal framework and specify the semantic conditions under which a goal can be interpreted. Removing those conditions would remove the applications themselves.

**Decision:** Retain.

### Value and ethics

Volume 5 uses the framework at several distinct joints: the transition from truth conditions to normative standpoints in Chapter 1; condition-binding and agent-binding in Chapter 2; the additional need for normative standards in Chapter 3; situated comparison in Chapter 4; rival value frames in Chapter 6; socially interpreted property in Chapter 10; the hidden obligation in the moral-extortion argument in Chapter 15; the common constraints behind moral convergence in Chapter 16; the explicit premise behind viability in Chapter 19; and the volume's compact final formalization of agent-bound judgment in Chapter 26. These passages inherit the canonical epistemology and add a domain-specific variable or inference.

**Decision:** Retain.

### Governance

Volume 7 Chapter 5 names the conditions of a Grey Zone judgment; Chapter 14 names the evidential assumptions inside a guilt claim; and Chapter 21 makes scale and incentive alignment the conditions of the communist case. Chapter 23 is the closest apparent duplication because Conditionalism appears three times. Continuous reading assigns the appearances different jobs:

- “Certainty is a consumer good” identifies the psychological attraction of dogma.
- “Dogmatic certainty versus conditional epistemology” defines one of the chapter's three political axes.
- The closing formulation derives the institutional requirement to remain corrigible.

The third appearance depends on the first two but does not re-argue them. Compressing any one would blur the chapter's psychology → definition → institutional-consequence sequence. The prior continuous-reading decision to leave Chapter 23 unchanged therefore stands.

Chapter 27 then derives a different application: political corrigibility under changing institutional conditions. It is the synthesis the chapter needs, not another defense of the underlying epistemology.

**Decision:** Retain.

### Culture and meaning

Volume 8 Chapter 6 invokes the absence of a neutral vantage while distinguishing perspectival evaluation from empirical relativism. Chapter 23 distinguishes the truth conditions of descriptive and constitutive categories in its gender case. Volume 9 Chapter 7 applies Conditionalism to the Stoic claim that virtue is objectively good, while Chapter 12 separates the conditions of interpreted truth, agent-relative value, and enacted existential meaning. These passages prevent local category errors and cannot be replaced by the slogan alone.

**Decision:** Retain.

## Result

The direct recurrences outside Volume 2 are compact bridges or applications that bind new local conditions. None repeats the canonical argument solely to reassure the reader that Conditionalism is not absolutism, skepticism, or relativism. No manuscript cut is warranted.

This is a no-cut decision, not a failed compression pass. The Phase 7 method uses demonstrated redundancy rather than a quota. Recording the negative result prevents a later phrase search from mistaking thematic recurrence for duplicated argumentative work.

The repetition ledger's earlier generic instruction to “compress later defenses” is now closed: no later full defense remains in the audited family. Future line editing may still improve an individual sentence, but it must do so for local reasons rather than because the Conditionalist premise recurs.
