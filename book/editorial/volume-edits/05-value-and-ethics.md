# Volume 5 edit record — Value and Agency-Centered Ethics

Status: **In author review**

## Editorial outcome

The pass preserves all 27 chapters and makes the volume's six-part normative architecture visible. The opening subjectivism and closing Sapient Agency Realism now form a declared progression rather than an apparent reversal: facts can constrain action within a specified domain, while reciprocal protection of sapient agency remains an explicit value commitment rather than an ought derived from physics.

The central editorial decision is to separate four layers the draft sometimes fused: physical or strategic facts, evidence of an agent's values, moral classification, and institutional authority. The volume still argues for agency-centered ethics, but no longer lets sacrifice prove preference, branch weight become a moral currency, viability become a universal optimizer, or procedure legitimate itself.

## Principal decisions

- Treat choice, opportunity cost, and sacrifice as defeasible evidence of value under conditions of information, voluntariness, capacity, and meaningful alternatives. Addiction, compulsion, misinformation, poverty, and adaptive preference remain live distortions.
- Make agent-binding operational by specifying the agent, model of the act, commitments, rules, and conflict-resolution conditions. Binding repairs under-specification but does not make a commitment coherent or worthy.
- Define coercion as a credible conditional threat of a material, non-consensual setback used to obtain compliance. Directed incitement can be delegated violence where intent, specificity, causal role, and connection support that classification.
- Preserve valid advance authorization through later incapacity and distinguish prospective revocation from retroactive invalidation. Capacity and surrogate authority remain decision-sensitive.
- Present property as a proposed institutional model rather than a metaphysical or universal lexical test. Territorial control, possession, enforceable title, and legitimacy are separate questions.
- Define harm as material degradation of functional capacity or viable options relative to an explicit baseline. Authorization does not erase injury; wrongfulness also requires analysis of consent, causation, foreseeability, and justification.
- Restrict prospective risk-harm to material, foreseeable, attributable worsening. Ordinary reciprocal risk, negligence, predation, rescue, and due care do not collapse into one category.
- Treat Sapientism as an explicit substrate-neutral standing commitment under graded uncertainty, not as a discontinuity proved by an administrative threshold.
- Mark the QBU ethics as conditional. Measure can weight modeled continuations but supplies neither value, interpersonal aggregation, nor permission to trade away protected claims.
- Treat “navigating Measure” as counterfactual decision language, not a physical mechanism. Ethical comparisons remain indexed to policies, Vantages, evidence, and protected claims.
- Present viability convergence as a conjecture under stated selection conditions. Persistence supplies reasons to agents who value continued agency; it does not derive a universal duty or explain every durable strategy.
- Index viability to a unit, preserved identity or function, environment, perturbations, baseline, and horizon. Treat metagame and scoreboard language as analytic metaphor rather than a universal optimizer.
- State the Ethics of Viability's agency-protection premise explicitly. Coercive orders can persist, reciprocal respect is not the only equilibrium, and defensive permission or loss of standing still requires normative and procedural premises.
- Distinguish reciprocal protection of agency from maximization of total agency. Restitution is a relational remedy, not a term in a global objective function.
- Require the coexistence protocol's institutions to earn legitimacy through public rules, evidence, due process, appeal, reciprocity, and safeguards against capture. Distributed procedure is not self-authorizing.
- Preserve Chapter 25's aggressor-contaminated-field contradiction. Chapter 26 acknowledges rather than solves it, and the coda claims only that zero-harm guarantees conflict with robust agency—not that any one institutional boundary is optimal.

## Structural changes

The volume now renders six parts and a coda:

Chapter 13 remains a deliberate one-chapter hinge between the operational definitions and rival-framework tests. Chapters 18–26 accumulate the viability argument without treating the final chapter as a hidden solution to the contradiction in Chapter 25.

## Open technical and author decisions

- **Agent-binding:** specify a reusable procedure for conflicts among an agent's avowed commitments, revealed priorities, standing rules, and later reflection.
- **Consent:** define standards for decision-specific capacity, advance directives, surrogate authority, irreversible reliance, and the point at which exit costs defeat voluntariness.
- **Property:** decide whether transferability is constitutive or typical, and identify the public conditions that make enforceable exclusion legitimate rather than merely effective.
- **Harm baseline:** select institutional baselines and materiality thresholds for recurring cases; neither physics nor the definition chooses them automatically.
- **Standing:** develop graded evidentiary protections for animals, developing humans, impaired adults, and artificial systems without treating an administrative line as a cognitive phase transition.
- **QBU ethics:** decide whether the branching module belongs in the normative core or should remain an optional interpretation-dependent extension.
- **Viability:** specify the conditions under which cooperative convergence is predicted and the conditions under which domination, deception, or lock-in can persist instead.
- **Coexistence institutions:** supply jurisdiction, selection, funding, review, appeal, capture resistance, enforcement limits, and legitimacy conditions without recreating unaccountable central authority.
- **Contaminated fields:** decide how defensive action should treat innocent risk when every available policy imposes some, or retain the contradiction as a permanent limit of the framework.
- **Realism label:** decide whether *Sapient Agency Realism* communicates the position better than *conditional constructivism*, given the explicit normative premise.

## Repetition and voice

Chapter 1 owns the rejection of valuer-independent value; Chapter 2 owns agent-binding; Chapter 4 owns sacrifice as evidence; Chapters 9–12 own the operational definitions; Chapters 18–22 own the descriptive-to-normative viability sequence; Chapter 25 owns the unresolved contradiction; Chapter 26 owns the qualified realism claim. Later volumes should cross-link these treatments instead of re-deriving them.

The pass preserves the book's direct, combative voice while removing false numerical precision, universal convergence language, and claims that physical vocabulary settles moral classification. Polemical passages remain where their target is a stated premise rather than a caricature or a substitute for institutional analysis.

## Phase 5 continuity update

The final meaning cluster fixes the downstream use of commitment. A commitment is an endorsed policy, priority, or evaluative stance that can be inherited, encountered, revised, or newly formulated. Reflective authorship means understanding, endorsing, revising, and answering for it, not creating it without causes. A terminal commitment ends the instrumental chain in the current account without becoming arbitrary, unrevisable, singularly ranked, or universally binding.


The standing argument now assigns different work to two axes: sentience can ground patienthood and welfare concern, while sapient authorship grounds the framework's stronger sovereign protections. Sapientism no longer treats substrate-neutrality as automatic machine coronation or current animal uncertainty as proof of absence. Sapient Agency Realism keeps reciprocal authorship as its explicit normative premise without claiming that value, suffering, or moral concern begin only at the sovereignty threshold.

The value and conditional-objectivity pass now separates empirical attribution from normative application. Agent-binding specifies the standard, agent or class, act model, scope, baseline, evidence, horizon, and conflict rules; it does not prove endorsement, authority, motivation, or enforceability. “Conditional objectivity” is reserved for public assessability under fixed inputs. Sapient Agency Realism may apply its reciprocal-authorship premise to non-adopters within its declared domain, but that coverage does not convert the chosen premise into agent-independent value or legitimate coercive enforcement by itself.

The harm, coercion, consent, and force pass now gives the operational sequence one stable taxonomy. Harm is a material welfare or capacity setback against an explicit appropriate baseline; prospective exposure, realized injury, and wrongful responsibility remain distinct. Coercion uses a credible conditional threat to obtain compliance, force directly compels, and violence is injurious or destructive physical force. Consent changes authorization and responsibility without erasing impact. Prior authorization, protection, defense, rescue, and remedy only open the justification inquiry; evidence, authority, necessity, proportionality, minimally harmful means, process, time limits, accountability, and review constrain it. The coexistence protocol no longer turns classification into enforcement legitimacy or Domain Exit into wholesale standing loss. Chapter 25's contaminated-field contradiction remains an explicit author decision.
