# Claim-Calibration Standard

This document governs how claims are described and edited across *Axio*. Its purpose is not to weaken the voice. It is to make the strength of the voice match the strength of the support.

## The six classes

### E — Established result

A result already established within the relevant external discipline or proved in a formal treatment the chapter accurately invokes.

Use direct declarative language when the result is represented within its accepted scope. State material assumptions, idealizations, and domain limits where their omission could change the inference.

Examples include standard thermodynamic constraints, decoherence, Bayes' theorem, and a theorem proved in a linked Axionic paper. Calling something a theorem is an E-class claim only for what the theorem actually proves.

### I — Interpretation of a result

A philosophical, ontological, or practical reading of an established result.

Signal the interpretive step when reasonable alternatives remain. “On the QBU interpretation,” “this suggests,” and “the framework reads this as” are appropriate where the manuscript currently risks presenting an interpretation as raw physics or mathematics.

Everettian ontology, the agency interpretation of thermodynamic work, and philosophical implications drawn from experimental results commonly belong here.

### A — Argument advanced by the book

A conclusion for which the chapter supplies explicit premises and reasoning, but which is neither disciplinary consensus nor a formal theorem.

State the premises, answer the strongest live objection, and allow confident conclusions when the argument earns them. Conditionalism, agent-binding, the Agency Criterion, and the political consequences of the Agency Protection Principle are normally A-class claims.

### C — Conjecture

A proposed explanation, extrapolation, unverified mechanism, or possibility claim whose support is incomplete.

Use language such as “I propose,” “the conjecture is,” “one possibility is,” or “if this model is right.” Identify what evidence or derivation would discriminate the conjecture from rivals. Chaos as an ontological foundation, Modal Realization at full generality, future machine succession, and broad civilizational predictions require particular attention here.

### V — Value commitment

A chosen evaluative commitment, priority, or creed rather than a result forced by description alone.

Own it in the first person or bind it explicitly to its valuing agents. Do not disguise it as physics, evolutionary success, logical necessity, or universal moral fact. Phosphorism and the Credo are canonical V-class material; the viability bridge must show exactly where description ends and commitment enters.

### R — Rhetorical illustration

An analogy, story, toy case, historical vignette, polemical label, or compressed example used to make structure visible.

An illustration may clarify an argument but cannot carry more evidentiary weight than its correspondence warrants. Mark fictional cases where ambiguity is plausible, verify historical cases, and avoid letting a vivid example substitute for a general derivation.

## Editing rules

1. Classify the load-bearing claim, not every sentence.
2. A paragraph may move between classes, but the transition must be visible.
3. Mathematical notation does not promote A or C to E.
4. A linked paper promotes only the claims actually established in that paper.
5. “Structural” is not a calibration class. A structural claim may be E, I, A, or C.
6. Conditional phrasing does not excuse a hidden premise; state conditions that materially control the result.
7. A stipulative definition may be exact without being uniquely correct. Separate usefulness and coherence from inevitability.
8. Empirical claims in the applied volumes must be verified, qualified, or recast as illustrations.
9. Polemical confidence is welcome after the reasoning, not in place of it.
10. Where the correct class is disputed, preserve the dispute in the editorial record until the author decides.

## High-risk transitions

The following transitions require an explicit calibration check:

- Thermodynamics → a measurable theory of agency.
- Everettian quantum mechanics → the QBU's specific ontology.
- Branch structure → causality, identity, and free will.
- Formal model or simulation → claim about realizable agents.
- Persistence or viability → ethical obligation.
- Chosen invariant → conditional moral realism.
- Market mechanism → historical or policy conclusion.
- Memetic model → judgment about a live political group.
- Naturalized experience → metaphysical conclusion.

## Chapter review notation

Editorial notes may annotate a claim as `[E]`, `[I]`, `[A]`, `[C]`, `[V]`, or `[R]`. These marks belong only in `book/editorial/`; they must never appear in published prose.

A chapter is ready for `review` when its load-bearing claims have identifiable classes, the prose matches those classes, and no inference crosses between classes without an argued bridge.
