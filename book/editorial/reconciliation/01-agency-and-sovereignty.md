# Agency and sovereignty reconciliation

Status: **Complete**

## Scope and method

This pass reconciles the first Phase 5 concept cluster across the complete manuscript. A case-insensitive inventory found agency or sovereignty vocabulary in 233 manuscript files, including 66 files with definition-bearing constructions and 61 files using *sovereign* or *sovereignty*. The pass reviewed the canonical chapters, every definition-bearing location, and the remaining uses by volume for layer changes, category substitutions, and institutional ambiguity.

Ordinary uses do not need a link every time. They remain valid when the intended layer is clear from context. Local passages that offered a rival definition, collapsed descriptive control into moral legitimacy, or treated institutional power as a mind were revised.

## Canonical hierarchy

| Layer | Canonical meaning | Canonical home | Not sufficient by itself |
|---|---|---|---|
| Causal capacity | The ability of a system or event to make a difference to an outcome. | Vol. 1, ch. 2 as the contrast with drift and control | Modeling, evaluation, agency, authorship, or legitimacy |
| Basal agency | An embedded system models reachable futures, evaluates them internally, and makes behavior depend on that evaluation so as to steer outcomes relative to a baseline. | Vol. 1, chs. 2 and 5 | Consciousness, reflection, moral standing, or sovereignty |
| Reflective agency / authorship | A temporally continuous agent owns reasons and commitments, can revise them, and authors transitions through its evaluative structure. | Vol. 4, ch. 2, supported by chs. 4–5 | Benevolence, correct judgment, or ethical conduct |
| Sovereignty | A reflective agent's evaluative machinery remains causally authoritative and is not instrumentally subordinated to another optimizer. Its reasons can stop an otherwise feasible action. | Vol. 4, chs. 2 and 4 | Freedom from every constraint, competence, or moral legitimacy |
| Sapient or sovereign standing | The book's chosen moral status for minds that meet its authorship criteria, with graded caution for uncertain, developmental, latent, or residual cases. | Vol. 5, chs. 13 and 26; Vol. 4, ch. 11 | A consequence of physics or basal agency alone |
| Effective or political agency | The practical exercise of an existing agent's capacities under liberty, capability, opportunity, and institutional conditions. | Vol. 7, ch. 2 | A replacement definition of the underlying agent |
| Institutional agency | An organization-level attribution warranted when decisions remain evaluable, responsibility traceable, commitments revisable, and actions endorsable through an institutional process. | Vol. 7, ch. 33 | A literal group mind or automatic consequence of coordination |
| Institutional sovereignty | A claim to final, coercively enforceable authority within a jurisdiction. | Vol. 7, chs. 29–31 and 35 | Reflective sovereignty, unified agency, or legitimacy |
| Authority | A scoped permission to cause a transition, mechanically or institutionally recognized under stated constraints. | Vol. 4, ch. 12 | Sovereignty, wisdom, or moral warrant |

The terms are layered rather than interchangeable. Basal agency admits degrees of control effectiveness. Reflective sovereignty supplies a constitutive boundary, while evidence about whether a system meets it and resilience near the boundary remain graded. Political agency describes realized opportunity, not another kind of control loop.

## Reconciliation decisions

### Basal agency remains broad but not indiscriminate

- Volume 1 owns the book-wide definition. Embeddedness, modeling, evaluation, and steering jointly separate an agent from a merely causal process.
- *Intentional* at the basal layer is functional: internal evaluation changes behavior. It does not imply consciousness or reflective endorsement.
- A thermostat remains a regulator rather than an agent because it lacks the modeled-future criterion used by the book. Bacterial chemotaxis remains the proposed minimum case.
- The kybit, where applicable, measures distributional control. It does not measure every layer of agency, authorship, standing, or sovereignty.

### Descriptive agency is not ethical legitimacy

Volume 1, Chapter 7 previously fused three questions: control, coherent authorship, and legitimate coexistence. The revised chapter distinguishes:

1. **Basal control:** did the system model, evaluate, and steer?
2. **Constitutive admissibility:** did a coherent author persist through the transition?
3. **Ethical admissibility:** did the authored action respect other agents under the book's chosen normative framework?

A predator may gain control by consuming another agent's options. The ethics can condemn that gain without defining the predator out of agency. Conversely, path occupancy can grow while authorship collapses. These are different failure modes and now use different language.

### Authorship qualifies agency rather than replacing it

Volume 4's slogan “agency is authorship” now identifies that volume's reflective layer explicitly. Its four pillars—binding reasons, deliberative meaning, commitment revision, and temporal continuity—are proposed criteria for reflective authorship within the tested design family. They do not retroactively deny basal agency to simpler systems.

The type/degree seam is resolved as follows:

- control effectiveness can vary by degree;
- reflective sovereignty imposes a proposed constitutive boundary;
- observations and implemented systems can occupy an uncertain or pressure-sensitive region around that boundary;
- moral caution may remain graded even where a governance system requires a threshold decision.

### Sovereign standing is the ethical threshold, not agency simpliciter

Volumes 4 and 5 now say *sovereign agency*, *sovereign authorship*, or *sapient agency* where meta-preference revision, standing, or jurisdiction is at issue. The changes preserve the substrate-neutral moral claim while avoiding the false inference that bacteria, animals, current AI systems, and reflective persons either share identical standing or are not agents at all.

The normative step remains disclosed: structure can identify an author and possible violations of authorship; reciprocal protection of that author is a value commitment, not a theorem of physics.

### Effective agency is the political target

Volume 7 uses *effective agency* for the practical field shaped by liberty, capability, and opportunity. This prevents poverty, education, resources, and access from becoming extra components of the basal definition while preserving their political importance.

Rights may track profiles of capacity, dependence, vulnerability, and experience. That graded profile does not erase the separate distinction between basal agency and the book's proposed sovereignty threshold.

### Institutional authority is not a group mind

The institutional-authority seam is resolved in both directions:

- Volume 4 no longer treats a nation as automatically one agent. Alignment with an organization is meaningful only to the extent that commitments, responsibility, revision, and authorship remain attributable.
- Volume 7's Leviathan argument now concerns the loss of attributable institutional authorship, not the disappearance of the individual agents inside the institution.
- Political sovereignty names a claim of coercive supremacy. Reflective sovereignty names self-authorship. One does not entail the other.
- Scoped, delegated, revocable authority can exist without either form of sovereignty.

## Exit assessment

This concept cluster meets the Phase 5 gate. Agency has one basal canonical definition; authorship, sovereignty, standing, effective agency, institutional agency, institutional sovereignty, and authority are explicit qualifications or contrasts. The known type/degree, descriptive/normative, thermostat, moral-threshold, and institutional-authority contradictions are resolved or acknowledged in the manuscript.
