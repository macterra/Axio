# Harm, coercion, consent, and justified force reconciliation

Status: **Complete**

## Scope and method

This pass reconciles the sixth Phase 5 concept cluster across the complete manuscript. A broad inventory found 3,135 uses of the harm, coercion, consent, force, violence, defense, threat, duress, and innocence families in 210 manuscript files. Most uses were ordinary or locally sound. Close review concentrated on Volume 5's canonical Chapters 9–12 and 20–25, Volume 3's AI-power applications, Volume 7's coercion, rights, speech, justice, and governance sequence, and Volume 8's enforcement cases.

The main defect was a collapsed inference chain. The canonical chapter defined coercion narrowly as compliance obtained by conditional threat, while later chapters used *coercion* for direct force, injury, risk, institutional power, and any constrained choice. Consent sometimes erased harm rather than authorizing an act; harm sometimes included wrongfulness in its definition and sometimes did not. Rights, defensive permission, Domain Exit, and institutional authority could then appear to follow directly from a classification.

## Canonical hierarchy

| Layer | Canonical meaning | Does not imply |
|---|---|---|
| Harm | A material setback to a sentient subject's welfare or an agent's functional capacity and viable options against an explicit appropriate baseline | Wrongfulness, coercion, or liability by itself |
| Prospective harm | A material, foreseeable, attributable worsening of exposure against an appropriate baseline | Realized injury, strict liability, or a zero-risk rule |
| Wrongful harm | Harm joined to a failure of authorization, duty, due care, or justification for which responsibility is attributable | That every remedy or enforcer is legitimate |
| Coercion | Deliberate use of a credible conditional threat of material setback to obtain compliance | Direct force, violence, persuasion, every costly refusal, or automatic wrongfulness |
| Force | Direct compulsion, restraint, seizure, or movement | A conditional threat or an automatic moral verdict |
| Violence | Physical force causing bodily injury, destruction, or material damage | Offense, influence, every harm, or every coercive threat |
| Consent | Scoped, decision-specific authorization under sufficient capacity, material understanding, intention, and voluntariness | Omniscience, costless refusal, absence of harm, or unlimited future permission |
| Moral right | A defensible protection or claim whose justification survives reciprocal application | Legal recognition or an enforcement mechanism |
| Legal right | An institutionally recognized claim | Moral legitimacy merely because law recognizes it |
| Enforcement | A mechanism used to secure a claim | That the claim, actor, jurisdiction, or remedy is legitimate |
| Justification | A valid ground—prior authorization, protection/defense/rescue, or remedy—plus applicable safeguards | Permission from classification or aggregate benefit alone |
| Domain Exit | A limited, reviewable protective status tied to an established serious violation or continuing threat | Character condemnation, an outlaw caste, revenge, or wholesale standing loss |

## Reconciliation decisions

### Impact, wrongfulness, and responsibility are separate

Harm is descriptive at the first layer. Valid consent to surgery does not make tissue damage disappear; it changes authorization, responsibility, and the act's moral classification. Natural misfortune, unavoidable accident, negligence, intentional predation, attempted harm, prospective exposure, and realized injury therefore remain distinguishable even when they affect the same protected interest.

The relevant baseline must be explicit and appropriate to the case. It can involve due care, a legitimate entitlement, a valid agreement, an authorized practice, or the trajectory absent an intervention. The instant before an act is not automatically the correct comparison. Materiality, attribution, foreseeability, and responsibility prevent the prospective account from becoming a prohibition on every non-zero risk.

### Coercion is a conditional-threat mechanism

Coercion works by making the target anticipate a material setback if they refuse. The target's warranted credence establishes practical credibility; a failed bluff can remain attempted manipulation or fraud without successfully controlling the target through anticipation. The threat/offer distinction depends on legitimate baselines, duties, dependencies, and authority. A refusal to begin a trade is ordinarily an offer withheld; a threat to breach an existing duty or exploit monopoly control of an essential dependency is different.

Force directly compels or restrains. Violence physically injures or destroys. One episode can contain all three, but a shove, seizure, cyberattack, or killing without a conditional demand is not coercion merely because it forces an outcome in ordinary language. This separation also stabilizes the speech boundary: advocacy and preparatory propaganda are not coercion, while intentional operational participation in a specific credible harm process can function as delegated violence under a high evidentiary burden.

### Consent authorizes within a scope

Consent requires decision-specific capacity, material understanding, intention, and voluntariness. It does not require complete disclosure of immaterial facts or an option with no cost. Pressure, asymmetry, poverty, dependency, and limited alternatives are evidence, not automatic invalidators of every agreement. The analysis must ask who created the threatened setback, which entitlement or duty supplies the baseline, what alternatives were viable, and what scope was authorized.

Advance authorization can persist through later incapacity; revocation is prospective where possible and must remain meaningful for ongoing arrangements. Guardianship and surrogate choice are fiduciary authorities bounded by the protected person's welfare and developing or prior agency, not substitutes for ownership.

### Grounds and safeguards jointly justify force

The manuscript now uses three families of possible grounds: valid prior authorization; protection, defense, or rescue; and remedy or restitution. Meeting a ground only opens the inquiry. Evidence, legitimate authority and jurisdiction, necessity, proportionality, least harmful feasible means, time limits, notice and opportunity to answer where feasible, appeal, accountability, and review limit the permission. Emergency timing can postpone some procedure but creates a later review and repair burden.

Aggregate benefit can inform proportionality inside a valid ground; it is not sufficient by itself to authorize using an innocent person as an instrument. Classification likewise cannot grant authority. A moral right can lack effective protection, a legal right can be unjust, and a powerful enforcement mechanism can secure a claim no one had the authority to impose.

### Innocence and Domain Exit remain act-relative

Innocence is a procedural presumption about responsibility for the matter at issue, not moral purity. Domain Exit requires an established serious violation or continuing threat and refusal of legitimate protective or remedial terms. It remains proportionate, reviewable, terminable, and tied to the violation. Accusation, inability to pay, forum refusal under capture, or one classification cannot erase standing or create a person whom anyone may attack.

The strict invariant in the Ethics of Viability is therefore stated without redefining all instrumental harm as coercion: do not use innocents as instruments by imposing material harm or exposure for another's ends. Chapter 25 retains the unresolved question of defensive action in aggressor-contaminated fields. This pass clarifies the contradiction; it does not choose the author's answer.

## Exit assessment

This concept cluster meets the Phase 5 gate. The manuscript now separates welfare or capacity setback, prospective exposure, realized injury, wrongfulness, coercive threat, direct force, violence, consent, rights, defensive permission, remedy, and institutional authority. Later applications link to the canonical definitions rather than silently replacing them. Incitement remains a distinct operational-participation inquiry, and neither moral classification nor Domain Exit creates enforcement legitimacy by definition.
