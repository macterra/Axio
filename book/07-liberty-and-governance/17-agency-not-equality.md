Consider two societies. In the first, everyone commands the same meager resources: the same thin diet, the same absent medicine, the same foreclosed futures. Perfect equality, universal misery. In the second, incomes span orders of magnitude — but the poorest eat well, see doctors, educate their children, and can realistically build a life different from the one they were born into. By the egalitarian metric, the first society is just and the second is a scandal. By any measure a human being actually cares about, the first is a catastrophe and the second is a triumph.

That inversion is the tell. When a moral metric ranks universal misery above universal flourishing because the flourishing is unevenly distributed, the metric is measuring the wrong variable. This chapter is about what the right variable is, and about the three most durable arguments for the wrong one: that inequality itself is a harm, that the role of luck in success licenses correcting it by force, and that even those of us who reject equal outcomes should still pursue equal opportunity. All three fail, and they fail in instructive ways — not at the edges, where a better version might survive, but at the root, where no version can.

## The Wrong Variable

Poverty and inequality are different kinds of things, and almost everything wrong with egalitarian politics begins by conflating them.

Poverty is a condition. It describes one person's relation to his own possible futures: the man in poverty cannot act on most of what he values. Food, shelter, medicine, learning, movement, the raising of his children — the choices that constitute a human life are foreclosed to him, not because they are difficult but because they are out of reach. In the terms this book has used throughout, poverty is an absolute deprivation of agency: a contraction of reachable futures down toward bare survival. That is harm in the exact sense that matters, and a person suffering it is harmed regardless of what anyone else has. A subsistence farmer in an economy of subsistence farmers, perfectly equal to all his neighbors, is still poor, and his poverty is still the problem.

Inequality is not a condition; it is a relation — a comparison between two people's holdings. And a relation, by itself, has no victim. To find harm in a disparity you must find a mechanism: theft, fraud, coercion, the capture of law by wealth. Where such a mechanism exists, the mechanism is the wrong, and it would be just as wrong if it produced equality. Where no mechanism exists — where the gap arose from voluntary production and exchange — the gap itself has harmed no one. My neighbor's fortune removes no food from my table, no years from my life, no options from my future. If everyone's agency is expanding while the gaps between us widen, no one is being wronged; something is merely being counted that was never the point.

The economic half of this argument — why wealth is not a fixed pile from which the rich subtract, and why the trillionaire's fortune is deployed in other people's paychecks rather than hoarded against them — is made in [Wealth Is Not a Pile](../06-markets-and-money/09-wealth-is-not-a-pile.md), and I will not repeat it here. The ethical half is what this chapter needs: harm is the contraction of someone's agency, and a disparity contracts nothing. It follows that a serious anti-poverty ethic targets the floor, not the gap. It asks whether the worst-off can act on their values, not how far behind the best-off they stand. The two questions come apart in both directions, and history has run both experiments. You can eliminate inequality while deepening poverty — several regimes achieved near-perfect equality of immiseration — and you can watch poverty collapse while measured inequality grows, which is a fair summary of the last two centuries. A politics that cannot tell these outcomes apart is not confused about economics. It is confused about what harm is.

The confusion is not innocent in its effects, either. Every unit of moral attention spent on the gap is a unit withdrawn from the floor. A government that measures its justice by the Gini coefficient can score victories that help no poor person at all — leveling down counts as progress by that metric — while a government that measures by agency has only one way to improve its score: make the worst-off lives more livable. Getting the variable right is not a philosophical nicety. It determines what a society bothers to fix.

## Luck Is Real — and Irrelevant

The strongest egalitarian rejoinder does not dispute any of this directly. It changes the subject to desert. The fortunate did not earn their advantages, the argument runs — success flows from genes, parents, upbringing, the century and country of one's birth — and what is unearned may be reclaimed. James Surowiecki has put the position with admirable bluntness, replying to a writer who argued the mirror image — that if success flows from randomness or exploitation redistribution makes sense, but if it flows from inherited traits it doesn't: if success is due to inherited traits that people have no control over, rather than individual effort and dedication, redistribution obviously makes *more* sense, not less.

Two claims are tangled here, and the argument survives only while they stay tangled.

The first claim is that success depends significantly on luck. This is true, and I concede it without reservation. Nobody chose his genome, his parents, his temperament, or his birthplace; the compounding advantages of a fortunate start are real and large. The dice are loaded before the first roll — [the loaded dice of parenthood](../06-markets-and-money/23-the-loaded-dice-of-parenthood.md) examines just how loaded. Anyone who thinks his position in life is entirely his own doing has not thought about it.

The second claim is that luck-based disparities justify coercive redistribution. This does not follow from the first, and no accumulation of evidence for the first can make it follow, because the two claims answer different questions. Coercion — [the credible threat of actual harm to gain compliance](../05-value-and-ethics/09-what-counts-as-coercion.md) — is not a free action awaiting a good enough occasion. It carries a burden of justification that scales with its severity, and [The Grey Zone](05-the-grey-zone.md) sets out what discharging that burden looks like: coercion answers, when it answers at all, to the prevention and remedy of agency violations. Run the question honestly. What would justify threatening a peaceful person with prison to make him surrender his holdings? That he violated someone's agency in acquiring them — perhaps. That he *didn't earn them* appears nowhere on the list, and cannot appear on it, because it is an answer to a different question entirely: the question of merit. The origin of a man's advantages — effort, inheritance, dumb luck — changes what we may admire about him. It changes nothing about what we may do to him.

Notice what the Surowiecki exchange actually reveals. The two sides reach opposite conclusions, but they share a premise: both assume the legitimacy of redistribution hangs on whether success is deserved. One says success is inherited, therefore redistribution loses its warrant; the other says success is inherited, therefore redistribution gains its warrant. Both have made desert the hinge. And desert cannot be the hinge, because the justification of coercion runs through agency, not merit. The self-made man has no more right to threaten his neighbors than the heir does; the heir has no less protection against threats than the self-made man. Once that premise falls, the entire luck literature — heritability estimates, twin studies, the arithmetic of the birth lottery — becomes ethically inert for this question. Interesting, largely true, and irrelevant.

What remains is worth stating, because the luck argument is not wrong about everything. The role of luck in success is an excellent reason for *voluntary* redistribution. If the unearned character of your own advantages moves you — and it should — then give, organize, endow, fund. Gratitude for one's own luck is a reason to open one's own wallet, not a license to reach into someone else's. Voluntary redistribution respects agency at both ends of the transfer, and it addresses the thing that actually needs addressing — poverty — rather than the relation that doesn't.

## The Equal-Opportunity Regress

So much for the egalitarianism of outcomes. But there is a second egalitarianism, and it infects my own allies. Ask a libertarian about equality and you will usually get a formula: equal outcomes, no; equal *opportunity*, yes. It sounds like the best of both worlds — fairness without coercion, a level starting line followed by an honest race. It is an illusion, and the reason is structural.

Opportunity, defined practically, is the set of choices realistically available to a person. Now ask where that set comes from. The child's schooling is an outcome — of her parents' income, choices, and neighborhood. The parents' income is an outcome of their own education, health, and networks, each of which is an outcome in turn. The confidence, the connections, the capital, the second chances: all of it is the sediment of prior outcomes, deposited by earlier rounds of effort and luck. There is no layer of "opportunity" that floats free of the outcomes that constitute it. Today's opportunities simply *are* yesterday's outcomes, viewed from the starting line.

The consequence is immediate. To genuinely equalize opportunity, you must equalize the prior outcomes that produce it — confiscate the differential wealth, negate the inheritance, neutralize the advantages that parents labor precisely in order to confer. That is equalization of outcomes, pursued by coercion, which is exactly what the libertarian formula claimed to renounce. And the program cannot stop at wealth, because the family is the primary transmitter of advantage: the read-to child, the well-fed child, the child with two attentive parents starts ahead in ways no estate tax can touch. Equalizing opportunity in earnest means reaching into the household itself. Taken seriously, the ideal devours the principle that endorsed it; each generation's finish line must be bulldozed flat to build the next generation's starting line.

The standard escape is to shrink the definition: equal opportunity just means no legally enforced discrimination — careers open to talents, no caste bars, no exclusionary statutes. That principle is sound, and I hold it. But it is not equality of opportunity; it is the *absence of coercive exclusion*, and honesty requires calling it that, because under it two children's actual chances remain wildly, systematically unequal. So the ideal faces a fork. At full strength it requires the coercive leveling of outcomes; diluted to coherence it is no longer about equality at all. There is no dose at which "equal opportunity" is both coherent and non-coercive — which means the ideal should be dropped, not moderated.

And dropping it costs nothing. Everything that was ever genuinely attractive in the phrase — that no one be barred by force from fields he could serve in, that the law not rig the race — is already secured by the demand that agency be protected from coercion. What the phrase adds beyond that is precisely the incoherent part: the fantasy of identical starting lines in a world where every starting line is someone's finish line. Libertarians who wave the banner of equal opportunity are borrowing prestige from an ideal their own principles refute. Better to say plainly what the position is: not equal chances, but unblocked ones.

## The Target Is Agency

Abandon equality as a goal, then — not just equal outcomes but the whole family: opportunity and outcome, full-strength and diluted. Not because fairness doesn't matter, but because equality was never the thing fairness was tracking. What fairness was tracking, all along, is agency: whether people can act on their own values, pursue their own goals, and reach futures they have reason to want. Poverty attacks agency; that is why poverty is evil. Coercion attacks agency; that is why coercion bears the burden of justification. A neighbor's fortune attacks nothing; that is why inequality, as such, is not a wrong. The whole argument of this chapter is contained in that asymmetry.

Measured by agency rather than equality, the political questions reorganize themselves. Instead of asking whether a policy narrows the gap, ask whether it expands or contracts what people can actually do — and whether it works by consent or by threat. The previous chapter already ran this test on one application: [Borders and Movement](16-borders-and-movement.md) found that immigration restrictions fail it twice over, coercively blocking the migrant's chosen future and the host's freedom of association at a single stroke. That is what the agency standard looks like in use — indifferent to whether an arrangement produces disparity, ruthless about whether it produces force.

This chapter frames the part; the five that follow are applications, and they ascend in ambition. [The Corruption of Compassion](18-the-corruption-of-compassion.md) takes the modest version: redistribution by taxation, basic income, foreign aid — the claim that good intentions launder coercive means. [Against Envy](19-against-envy.md) takes the psychology beneath the metric: the invalid inference from disparity to injury, and why a society that preserves mobility can tolerate inequality while a society that suppresses mobility cannot tolerate even perfect equality. [Against Socialism](20-against-socialism.md) takes the program: need mistaken for title, and the conditions under which collective provision turns predatory. [Against Communism](21-against-communism.md) takes the program at maximum dose, where the egalitarian promise becomes a machine for immiseration. And [Against Utopia](22-against-utopia.md) takes the temptation in its final form: the perfected society, worth any sacrifice, closed against correction. The ambition grows at every step. The answer does not change. Equality is not the measure of a just society. Agency is.
