**Thesis.** Coercive extraction is a continuum, not a binary: from total self-sovereignty (0%) to chattel slavery (100%), with every tax regime somewhere between. Statism's structural feature — a central authority's claimed license to initiate coercion over peaceful non-consenters — is identical across regimes; democracies and dictatorships differ in how sparingly the license is used, not in its existence. Under the technical definition of ownership (use, exclude, dispose), the state holds enforceable partial claims over its citizens: partial enslavement and partial ownership are the same fact in two vocabularies.

**Sources.** from-sovereignty-to-slavery: the quantified spectrum and the future-moral-scrutiny argument (today's taxation as yesterday's serfdom); consent requires viable refusal. statism-is-always-authoritarian: the structural definition (four features: monopoly of force, presumed consent, unilateral rule-making, coercive enforcement) and the Colin Wright rebuttal. invisible-chains: the ownership-bundle mapping (licensing→use, borders/zoning→exclude, taxation/estate law→dispose). against-slavery: the rhetorical maximum — structural isomorphism of slaveowner and state claims, coercion laundered through moral vocabulary.
