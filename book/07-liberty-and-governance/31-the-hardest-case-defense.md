**Thesis.** Defense is the state's one serious case, and this chapter takes it at full strength. "X is necessary, therefore state X" is the central statist fallacy — a real need never proves one preferred implementation — but the concessions are real: war rewards command (centralized coercion genuinely wins narrow speed-and-coordination contests), free-riding is real if abused as a concept, and capital may collaborate under invasion, demanding bonded mutual-defense compacts, precommitted command triggers, and automatic sanctions for defection. The structural danger runs the other way too: defense shell → fiscal machine → political order → ruler; military necessity is the great engine of state expansion. The burden of proof cuts both ways — statists must show defense requires sovereignty; anti-statists must show organized force can avoid hardening into rule — and the unresolved task is designing protection strong enough to deter without consuming the thing it exists to preserve.
