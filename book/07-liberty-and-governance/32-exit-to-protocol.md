**Thesis.** The coercive state is 17th-century technology: centralized, opaque, tax-funded, non-forkable. Its successor is protocolized governance — voluntary, modular, competitive, cryptographically enforceable — which out-competes rather than overthrows politics: arbitration networks for law, insurance consortia for defense, federated cooperatives for infrastructure, mutual-aid pools for safety nets, cryptographic money for finance. Governance becomes measurable, comparable, and forkable; the transition begins where coercion is weakest (digital, borderless commerce), and the state withers "not through revolution but through redundancy." The evidence of legitimacy migration is already visible: classical liberalism collapsing geographically while being reborn on the Internet — and the old knowledge class's authority evaporating with it, scholarly declarations reduced from thunderclap to press release.
