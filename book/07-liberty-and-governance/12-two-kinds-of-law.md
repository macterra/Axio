**Thesis.** Law divides in two: common law — bottom-up, reactive, a decentralized discovery process that resolves conflicts over person and property — and command law: top-down legislation that criminalizes peaceful behavior and manufactures conflict between citizen and legislator. The test of any law is whether it reduces or increases the credible threat of harm used to force compliance; a legal system's legitimacy is not its statute count but whether it upholds the agency of the governed. The archist rejoinder — centralized authority delivers superior stability — is conditionally valid but cooks the books: the "order" is extracted through institutionalized coercion whose costs (economic, ethical, psychological) are hidden, and much of what is called stability is suppression.
