> There are two kinds of laws: traditional "common" law is there to resolve conflicts that may occur with respect to person or property; the other kind is instituted to create conflict between people and the legislator by disallowing the former from taking certain actions.
>
> — Per Bylund

Consider two farmers. The first has a neighbor whose livestock broke through a fence and trampled his crops. There is a genuine dispute here — real damage, two parties with incompatible claims, and a question that must be answered: who bears the loss? The second farmer sells raw milk to a customer who drove out to the farm specifically to buy it. Both parties are satisfied. No one has been harmed, no one has complained, and no dispute exists between anyone. Yet in most jurisdictions the second farmer, not the first, is the one facing criminal penalties.

Both situations are governed by law, and we use the same word for both. That shared word conceals the deepest divide in jurisprudence. The law that gives the first farmer recourse exists to resolve a conflict that arose between people. The law that criminalizes the second farmer's sale *creates* a conflict where none existed — between two peaceful traders on one side and the legislator on the other. Bylund's distinction is not a classification of legal instruments; it is a classification of what law is *for*. And once you see it, you cannot unsee it in any statute book.

## Law as Discovery

Common law is built from precedent, custom, and the outcomes of real disputes. Its purpose is practical: when people come into conflict, it provides a mechanism for settlement — damages, restitution, injunction — but the aim is always to resolve a problem that has already happened. It is reactive by design, and the reactivity is not a defect. It is the feature that keeps the law tethered to reality. A rule enters the common law only because an actual conflict forced the question, and it survives only as long as it keeps resolving conflicts of that kind better than the alternatives.

This makes common law a decentralized discovery process. No one designed the law of trespass, bailment, or nuisance; thousands of adjudicated disputes gradually refined rules that align with what people in a given society actually expect of one another — where the fence obligations lie, who bears the risk of straying animals, what counts as reasonable care. The rules that reduced future conflict propagated as precedent; the rules that generated new grievances got distinguished, narrowed, and abandoned. The parallel to markets is exact, and it is not a metaphor. Prices emerge from voluntary exchanges nobody scripted and encode information no central authority possesses; common-law rules emerge from adjudicated disputes nobody scripted and encode norms no legislator could enumerate. [Mechanisms for honest values](../06-markets-and-money/17-mechanisms-for-honest-values.md) shows how markets discover what people actually value; common law is the same epistemic engine pointed at a different question — what people actually owe each other. Both are discovery procedures, and the generalization of that principle to governance itself is where this volume ends, in [Axiocracy](35-axiocracy.md).

The typical common-law case involves a clear harm: theft, assault, fraud, damage to property. The court's role is not to dictate how people live but to provide a predictable framework within which they can interact freely, knowing that genuine violations will be adjudicated. When the livestock tramples the crops, the law supplies compensation or restraint — and then gets out of the way. Because it acts only where real disputes arise, it tends structurally to limit coercion: it has no mechanism for reaching conduct that harms no one, because harmless conduct generates no plaintiffs.

## Law as Command

Legislation inverts every one of these properties. Instead of waiting for disputes, legislators set rules that dictate behavior in advance. Instead of emerging from the bottom up, the rules are imposed from the top down. And instead of encoding the accumulated expectations of the people the law governs, they encode the priorities of whoever captured the drafting process — political bargaining, lobbying, ideological agendas. The raw-milk ban did not emerge from farmers and customers suing each other over spoiled milk; it emerged from a legislature, shaped by whichever coalition of regulators, incumbent dairies, and public-health lobbies happened to prevail.

The structural consequence is the one Bylund names: command law manufactures conflict. Citizens who would peacefully transact are placed in opposition to the state itself. The farmer and his customer had no quarrel until the statute created one — and note who the new quarrel is with. Not with each other; with the legislator. Command law does not referee disputes between people. It generates a standing dispute between the people and the authority, one that the authority then polices, prosecutes, and punishes at its own expense and everyone else's. This is why such laws are *experienced* as arbitrary and coercive even by people who could not articulate the distinction: the felt difference between a law that would protect you from your neighbor's trespass and a law that makes you a criminal for a sale both parties wanted is not confusion. It is accurate perception of two different kinds of thing wearing the same name.

## The Honest Middle

Not every law sorts cleanly into one bin, and the argument gains nothing from pretending otherwise. Some rules attempt to prevent harms before they occur — public-health measures, environmental regulation — and prevention is not automatically illegitimate. Pollution controls limit emissions that would otherwise generate real conflicts over health and property; a rule that pre-empts a thousand nuisance suits by stopping the nuisance at the smokestack can genuinely reduce future disputes rather than manufacture new ones. In theory, such rules are protective, and the theory is sometimes true.

The ambiguity lies in execution, because preventative law slips into overreach with almost gravitational reliability. "Prevention" is the universal solvent of legislative justification: every product ban, licensing mandate, and market regulation can be dressed as pre-empting some conceivable future harm. The question that separates the honest cases from the rest is whether the restriction truly prevents harm to others or merely enforces conformity with a political agenda. The pollution control answers to identifiable victims and traceable damage. The raw-milk ban answers to a hypothetical harm that the supposed victim — the customer — has knowingly and voluntarily accepted, which is to say it protects no one from anything except their own choices. A law that pre-empts trespass is still conflict-resolution law, run in advance. A law that pre-empts consent is command law, whatever its preamble says. What a genuine justification for the preventative middle would have to look like — the burden of proof, who carries it, and how the answer stays provisional — is the procedure set out in [The Grey Zone](05-the-grey-zone.md); the point here is that the honest middle exists, and that its honesty is exactly as good as its answer to that question.

## Coercion as the Measure

The test, then, is not whether a rule was made by judges or legislators — that is the historical correlate, not the criterion. The test is whether the law reduces or increases coercion: the [credible threat of actual harm used to gain compliance](../05-value-and-ethics/09-what-counts-as-coercion.md). Conflict-resolution law reduces the total coercion in a society. It protects rights, provides restitution, and makes voluntary agreements dependable, which means people can deal with strangers without arming themselves against them. Control-based law increases it: conduct that was consensual and peaceful becomes conduct performed under threat, and every statute of that kind adds a new tripwire between ordinary life and state violence.

This distinction matters because coercion is what undermines agency. A society whose laws primarily resolve disputes leaves individuals free to act and answerable for their choices; a society dominated by laws that create conflicts treats individuals as subjects to be managed rather than agents to be respected — it contracts the futures they can reach, not by making choices costly but by making them forbidden. And this yields the criterion that everything else in this chapter has been building toward: the legitimacy of a legal system is not measured by how many statutes it enforces, or how vigorously. It is measured by whether it upholds the agency of the governed. A jurisdiction with ten thousand laws and no freedom is not a triumph of order. It is a failure of law wearing order's uniform.

## The Order Objection

The strongest reply to all of this deserves stating in its strongest form. Centralized authority, the argument runs, is what makes law effective at all: a state empowered with special rights — coercion, a monopoly on violence — is necessary to maintain law, order, and stability, and the evidence is on its side. Centralized states pacified post-feudal Europe and drove down interpersonal violence. Authoritarian states like Singapore and Saudi Arabia have sustained decades of internal stability. And where central authority collapses — Somalia, Libya after 2011 — the result is not flowering common-law order but warlords. Central enforcement, the argument concludes, suppresses private retaliation and solves the coordination problems that voluntary arrangements cannot. Even granting that command law manufactures conflict at the margin, the state that issues it delivers the stability that makes everything else — including common-law adjudication — possible.

This is the archist position — support for centralized authority holding rights that individuals lack — and it gets its full examination in [The Archist Axiom](29-the-archist-axiom.md). Here I take only its empirical core, the stability claim, and the claim does not survive an honest audit.

First, misattribution. Much of what gets called stability is suppression: violence and conflict not resolved but sat upon, invisible until the lid comes off. A society where disputes are settled and a society where disputants are too afraid to press their claims produce similar homicide statistics and are not similar societies. Second, the correlation is real but runs through the wrong variable. Stability tracks the rule of law and the density of mutual cooperation, not the centralization of authority as such. The pacification of Europe coincided with centralizing states, but also with expanding trade networks, commercial law, and norms of reciprocity — and the authoritarian exemplars are stable precisely insofar as they maintain predictable rules, not insofar as they concentrate power. Concentrated power with unpredictable rules is not Singapore; it is every kleptocracy on the same continent as the failed states the argument cites. Third, the hidden violence. Archist stability is not the absence of force but its institutionalization: the coercion, imprisonment, and standing threats that produce the calm are themselves violence, running continuously beneath the surface — structural instability disguised as order, and priced as if it were free.

And the price is the part the stability argument most needs you not to examine. Economically, the machinery of centralized order consumes, in a typical liberal democracy, somewhere between 35 and 50 percent of what its citizens earn, extracted under threat of punishment — a protection racket distinguished from the criminal kind by scale and paperwork, a case made in full in [The Coercion Continuum](03-the-coercion-continuum.md) and [Extortion-Funded Organizations](04-extortion-funded-organizations.md). Ethically, it runs on a standing asymmetry: the state claims rights of coercion that it denies to everyone it governs, so the order is purchased by violating the very principle — no special moral privileges — that ordinary morality runs on. Psychologically, it normalizes coercion itself: generations raised under managed order come to treat permission as the natural precondition of action, which corrodes exactly the agency that a legal system exists to uphold.

Nor is the archist's implicit history — order or authority, choose one — actually the menu. Medieval Iceland maintained a functioning legal order for roughly three centuries with courts, binding arbitration, and enforceable judgments but nothing resembling an executive state; chieftaincies competed for adherents who could switch allegiance, which disciplined the adjudicators the way exit disciplines any service provider. Celtic Ireland ran on a comparable decentralized order for far longer. These societies were not utopias, and I am not offering them as blueprints. They are counterexamples, and against a claim of necessity a counterexample is enough: law without a command hierarchy has existed, at scale, for centuries at a stretch. Stability does not require archism. What requires archism is command law — because rules that manufacture conflict with the governed can only be sustained by an authority strong enough to win that conflict indefinitely.

So the stability argument, honestly appraised, is conditionally valid and ethically bankrupt as stated: centralized authority can produce a kind of order, but the order is extracted through institutionalized coercion whose costs — economic, ethical, psychological — are kept off the books. Put the costs back on the ledger and the two kinds of law come apart cleanly. Conflict-resolution law earns its authority by making peace cheaper; command law spends authority making obedience mandatory. When law emerges from real conflicts and resolves them fairly, it strengthens trust and preserves freedom; when it is used as a tool of control, it manufactures conflict, raises the ambient level of threat, and erodes the very respect for law that its enforcers demand. That is the divide. The next question is who the rules actually reach — because even the best law binds only where power permits it to, and that is the subject of [Law and Power](13-law-and-power.md).
