**Thesis.** Name the hidden assumption: an archist supports special moral privileges for the State; an anarchist asks the question archism must answer — why can the State ethically coerce where individuals cannot? The divide is ultimately axiomatic, but the load-bearing logical move is available to everyone: rights enforcement entails coercion, but coercion does not entail monopoly — liberty flourishes under decentralized, competitive, voluntary, accountable enforcement, while monopoly enforcement suffers exactly the pathologies (no accountability, misaligned incentives, funding by the coercion it exists to prevent) the state is supposed to cure. Then the hardest case, steelmanned: national defense, the strongest public-goods argument for the state — met with the knowledge problem, threat-inflation incentives, historical existence proofs (privateers, the Icelandic Commonwealth, the Hanseatic League), and the observation that free-rider concerns consistently provoke market innovation rather than market failure.
