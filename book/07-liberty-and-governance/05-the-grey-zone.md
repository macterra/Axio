**Thesis.** The Part I capstone and the volume's canonical position on coercion's legitimacy: no slogan — aggression, vulnerability, order, outcomes — settles it. Easy cases exist at both ends (defense and restitution; naked domination), but the grey zone cannot be abolished, only kept honest: coercion is at most provisionally justified under specified conditions — specific, proportional, evidence-bound, reversible, institutionally contained — with the burden of justification rising with severity, duration, uncertainty, and enforcer discretion. "Good coercion" is a dangerous phrase: coercion corrupts even when justified, so institutional drift belongs in the moral calculus from the start.
