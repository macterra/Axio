“Socialist” is asked to cover the regime that built Auschwitz, the regime that built the Gulag, and the regime that built Danish healthcare. Stretched across an ethno-nationalist death cult, a class-revolutionary police state, and parliamentary welfare capitalism, the word conveys almost nothing unqualified.

“Fascist” has similarly eroded from a package of ultranationalism, corporatist statism, militarism, and violent authoritarian control into “people I dislike on the right.” *Communist*, *liberal*, *neoliberal*, and *far right* increasingly operate as gestures rather than instruments.

This is not pedantry. Political vocabulary is an instrument panel; gauges that always read the same leave judgment blind. The repair is taxonomy plus discipline: separate the referents, then hold the line on usage.

## Three Socialisms

The word *socialism* has named three distinct systems.

**National Socialism** is ethno-nationalist authoritarianism. Nazi Germany used socialist language but subordinated freedom and economic rights to the nation-state and racial identity. Private ownership survived on paper under corporatist direction; oppression, war, and genocide define the legacy.

**International Socialism** — Marxist-Leninist communism — sought proletarian solidarity and abolition of private ownership. The Soviet and Maoist systems centralized economic and political command, crushed individual rights, and produced the record examined in [Against Marxist-Leninist Communism](21-against-communism.md).

**Liberal Socialism** — social democracy or welfare capitalism — keeps democratic institutions, individual rights, private ownership, and markets while adding regulation and social provision. Nordic countries, Canada, and much of Western Europe operate within this broad family.

| Aspect | National Socialism | International Socialism | Liberal Socialism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Central goal** | Ethnic-nationalist supremacy | International proletarian solidarity | Democratic flourishing and social provision |
| **Economic control** | Corporatist direction | Central planning | Regulated markets |
| **Individual freedom** | Severely restricted | Severely restricted historically | Protected and prioritized |
| **Democracy** | Explicitly anti-democratic | Explicitly anti-democratic historically | Explicitly democratic |

## The Axis That Matters

National and International Socialism sit at opposite ends of the conventional spectrum but converge in machinery: authoritarian government, suppressed rights, state control, blocked exit, and punished dissent. Their announced beneficiaries differ; their treatment of people converges.

Liberal Socialism shares some distributive aims with International Socialism but rejects its machinery. The more informative axis is therefore authoritarian versus liberal — how power is held and constrained — rather than what rulers say power is for.

This structural taxonomy complements [Against Socialism](20-against-socialism.md), which maps dose, domain, monopoly, exit, and feedback. One distinguishes regime types; the other measures mechanisms and constraints. Neither substitutes for the other.

## Why Precision Matters

A word binding distinct referents lets a conclusion proved about one travel silently to another. Calling public healthcare “socialism” can imply a road to gulags or, before a different audience, the innocence of Denmark. The defender of welfare capitalism inherits Stalin's body count; the central planner hides behind pension funds.

The same equivocation powers slogans claiming that the left are the real fascists or that fascism is merely the right's endpoint. Terms loose enough to contain any opponent prevent convergence because participants no longer discuss the same object. Precision is the precondition for disagreement that can learn.

## Doyle's Razor

*Fascist* is the most inflated label. Overuse drains its diagnostic force and gives genuine fascists cover among the falsely accused. Andrew Doyle offers a sharp sufficient test:

> If you condone the murder of your political opponents, you're a fascist.

This is not a complete historical definition, but it restores a bright line at the legitimization of political murder. The test is cheap, symmetric, and indifferent to faction.

After an assassination, a fringe often celebrates while screenshots make it look representative of half a country. Precision cuts both ways: the fringe is not the mainstream, but those celebrating bloodshed have crossed the line. Fascism is not a neighbor who votes wrongly; it is political violence made legitimate.

Two distortions reinforce each other:

1. **Overuse:** ordinary opponents are slandered and the term becomes noise.
2. **Under-focus:** those condoning violence go unnamed inside that noise.

The word is moral currency. Debasement makes every accusation easier and every valid accusation weaker. The next volume examines deliberate label weaponization; this chapter supplies the definitional hygiene needed to detect it.

## A LARPitarian Interlude

Semantic hygiene has a temperamental cousin: hold political identity lightly. Erving Goffman described social life as contextual performance; political flags, vocabularies, and denunciations are often costume. LARPitarianism gives the honest version a one-line constitution: *everybody should be free to LARP; nobody should be forced to LARP.*

The first clause protects voluntary identities and roles. The second rejects compulsory identities, rituals, and demands that strangers play villains in someone else's drama. Label inflation is forced LARP: a casting director nobody hired conscripts the public as extras.

## Retiring the Compressed Axes

“Socialist,” “fascist,” and left–right all project a high-dimensional political space onto a single epithet or line. The three-socialisms table shows what gets destroyed: structural twins are filed at opposite ends and structural opposites side by side.

Taxonomy can sharpen the old tools, but the honest endpoint is to retire the compressed axes. Political arrangements require dimensions for how power is held, whether exit exists, what feedback constrains rulers, and how much of life the system claims. [The Admissible Region](34-the-admissible-region.md) builds that space. The relevant question is not “how far left?” but whether an arrangement preserves viable agency.
