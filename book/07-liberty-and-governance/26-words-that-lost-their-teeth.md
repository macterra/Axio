Consider what the word "socialist" is asked to cover. It names the regime that built Auschwitz. It names the regime that built the Gulag. And it names the regime that built the Danish healthcare system. One word, stretched across an ethno-nationalist death cult, a class-revolutionary police state, and a prosperous parliamentary democracy where the trains run on time and nobody disappears in the night. A word that covers all three of those things conveys almost nothing. Whoever uses it unqualified is not making a claim; he is making a noise.

"Fascist" has fared no better. It once referred to a specific ideological package — ultranationalism, corporatist statism, militarism, violent authoritarian control. Decades of rhetorical service have worn it down to a generic insult for "people I dislike on the right." The same erosion has claimed "communist," "liberal," "neoliberal," "far right," and most of the rest of the working vocabulary of politics. These words were once instruments; now they are gestures.

This matters more than a pedant's complaint about usage. Political vocabulary is the instrument panel of collective decision-making. When the gauges all read the same regardless of conditions, the vehicle is being steered blind. A society that cannot distinguish a Nazi from a social democrat in its language will eventually fail to distinguish them in its judgments — condemning the harmless and excusing the monstrous, because the words that should sort them have lost their teeth.

The remedy is not new words. It is taxonomy plus discipline: split the inflated terms into the distinct things they actually name, then hold the line on usage. This chapter does that for the two worst offenders.

## Three Socialisms

Socialism is treated as a monolithic ideology, and almost every argument about it founders on that mistake. In truth the word has historically named three radically distinct systems, each with its own ideals, methods, and outcomes.

**National Socialism** is ethno-nationalist authoritarianism. Nazi Germany, its infamous embodiment, appropriated socialist language — claiming to represent "workers," posturing against capitalism — but had no interest in redistributing power or wealth democratically. It subordinated individual freedom and economic rights to the perceived needs of the nation-state and its racial identity. Its economics were corporatist: private ownership on paper, state direction in fact. Its legacy is universal condemnation, earned through oppression, violence, and genocide.

**International Socialism** — Marxist-Leninist communism — championed global working-class solidarity and the abolition of private ownership. In practice it produced authoritarian regimes in which individual rights were crushed in the name of class struggle: the Soviet Union, Maoist China. Economic decision-making was centralized into planned economies characterized by inefficiency, oppression, and eventual collapse. Its legacy is broad discredit — though paradoxically it retains admirers among Western intellectual and political elites who never had to live under it.

**Liberal Socialism** — social democracy, welfare capitalism — balances democratic governance, individual rights, and market economies with robust safety nets and regulated industries. Liberal socialists do not seek to abolish capitalism or private ownership; they aim to moderate capitalism's excesses through public investment in health, education, and welfare. The post-war Nordic countries, Canada, and much of Western Europe run on this model, and it has proven economically stable and durably popular.

Set the three side by side and the differences stop being subtle:

| Aspect | National Socialism | International Socialism | Liberal Socialism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Central goal** | Ethnic-nationalist supremacy | International proletarian solidarity | Democratic individual and collective flourishing |
| **Economic control** | Corporatist authoritarianism | Centrally planned economy | Regulated market economy |
| **Individual freedom** | Severely restricted | Severely restricted historically | Protected and prioritized |
| **Democracy** | Explicitly anti-democratic | Explicitly anti-democratic historically | Explicitly democratic |

## The Axis That Matters

Read the table by columns and something jumps out. On the conventional left–right axis, National and International Socialism are supposed to be opposites — the far right and the far left, mortal ideological rivals who fought each other in the streets of Weimar Germany and then across the Eastern Front. Yet on every row that measures how the system treats the people living under it, they converge: authoritarian governance, suppressed individual rights, state control at the expense of personal freedom. The rivalry was real; the resemblance is realer. Two prison camps do not become opposites because their flags differ.

Liberal Socialism, meanwhile, sits closer on the left–right axis to International Socialism — both invoke solidarity, redistribution, the welfare of workers — and yet on every structural row it is the odd one out, in the good direction: rights protected, democracy explicit, markets retained.

The lesson is that the structural axis — authoritarian versus liberal, or how power is held and constrained — matters more than the left–right axis of what the power is nominally for. The first two socialisms differ in their announced beneficiaries (the race, the class) and agree in their machinery (total state, no exit, no dissent). The third shares some of the second's announced goals and rejects the machinery — it employs socialist-inspired policies pragmatically rather than dogmatically, inside institutions that individuals can vote against and walk away from. Content divides National from International Socialism; structure unites them. Structure divides both from Liberal Socialism; content only partially does.

This is one of two complementary lenses this book applies to socialism: this chapter's taxonomy sorts regime types by structure, while the dose taxonomy of [Against Socialism](20-against-socialism.md) — dose, domain, monopoly, exit, feedback — measures how much of the socialist mechanism a system administers and how it is constrained; the two lenses answer different questions and neither substitutes for the other. And it is the same insight that the political triangle in [Why Liberalism Loses](24-why-liberalism-loses.md) encodes geometrically: the interesting divide in politics is not left versus right but the axis running orthogonal to it.

## Why Precision Matters

Someone might object that this is all pedantry — everybody knows Sweden is not the Soviet Union, whatever words get used. But the confusion is not harmless, because arguments run on words, and a word that binds three referents lets a conclusion proved about one referent travel silently to the others. That is the mechanics of a category error, and political discourse is full of the wreckage.

Call a national healthcare proposal "socialism" and you have implied — without ever having to argue it — that it sits on a continuum ending in gulags. Call it that in front of an audience that associates the word with Denmark and you have implied it is obviously benign. Both moves are fraudulent, and both are ubiquitous. The defender of welfare capitalism gets saddled with Stalin's body count; the apologist for central planning hides behind Norwegian pension funds. Each equivocation is only possible because the word refuses to distinguish.

The confusion compounds when the inflated words collide. "The Nazis were socialists, so the left are the real fascists." "Fascism is capitalism in decay, so the right are the real fascists." Both slogans exploit the same defect: terms so baggy that any opponent can be stuffed inside. Labeling welfare policies "fascist" or "communist" doesn't misdescribe them by degree; it misdescribes them by kind — and debates conducted in miscategorized kinds cannot converge, because the parties are not disagreeing about the same object. Distinguishing National, International, and Liberal Socialism is not academic tidiness. It is the precondition for arguing about any of them.

## Doyle's Razor

Which brings us to "fascist," the most inflated word in the modern political lexicon. Its semantic inflation has robbed it of diagnostic utility: when everyone is a fascist, the label means nothing. And a label that means nothing protects the people it should indict, because genuine fascists can hide in the crowd of the falsely accused.

The writer Andrew Doyle has proposed a simple intervention, which I will call Doyle's razor:

> If you condone the murder of your political opponents, you're a fascist.

This is not an exhaustive definition — the historical package of fascism includes more than political murder — but it is a sharp one, and sharpness is what the word has lost. The razor draws a bright line at the practice that made fascism what it was: the treatment of political opponents as legitimate targets of violence. It is a sufficient condition, cheap to check, and indifferent to which team the accused plays for.

The test earns its keep whenever political violence actually occurs. After a political assassination in a polarized society, the pattern is depressingly reliable: the great majority across the spectrum condemn the killing, while a fringe openly celebrates it — and screenshots of the celebrants circulate until they create the impression that a large fraction of the other side was cheering. That impression is exaggeration, and the exaggeration is itself a form of the inflation this chapter is against. What actually exists is a noisy fringe: individuals who crossed the line into condoning political murder.

Doyle's razor handles this case with precision that the inflated word cannot manage. The celebratory fringe are not the mainstream of their movement, and no honest observer should pretend they are. But the razor applies to them with full force: by cheering political bloodshed they disqualify themselves from the moral high ground and embrace the very authoritarian impulse they claim to resist. Fascism is not your neighbor who votes wrong, and it is not anyone with an unpopular opinion. It is crossing the line into legitimizing political murder — and the ones who cross it unmask themselves, whatever banner they marched in under.

Notice that the discourse around fascism suffers from two opposite distortions, and the razor slices through both:

1. **Overuse.** Calling every ordinary conservative — or every ordinary progressive — a fascist drains the term of meaning and slanders the innocent.
2. **Under-focus.** Ignoring the fringe that actually condones political violence leaves genuine fascism unnamed and uncontested.

These errors feed each other. The overuse makes every accusation sound like partisan noise, which gives the genuinely guilty cover; the under-focus lets the guilty operate unnamed, which makes the word seem safe to spend on trivia. A term of moral condemnation is a currency, and it obeys the logic of currency: debase it and it stops buying anything. Doyle's murder test preserves the word's moral weight by targeting it precisely where it belongs — and nowhere else.

I should be clear about what this chapter is and is not claiming. The inflation of political labels is not always innocent drift; often it is deliberate — a weapon in memetic conflict, wielded to destroy reputations and forbid positions rather than describe them. The mechanics of that weaponization — how [cancel culture](/posts/173950922.cancel-culture.html) deploys inflated labels as social artillery, and how [algorithmic media corrupt language itself](/posts/202203795.the-algorithmic-corruption-of-language.html) — belong to the next volume's treatment of narrative warfare. This chapter's business is the definitional hygiene that makes the weapon detectable: you cannot recognize a debased currency until you know what the coin was originally worth.

## A LARPitarian Interlude

Semantic hygiene has a temperamental cousin worth a brief aside: holding political identity itself a little more lightly. Erving Goffman observed that social life is theatrical performance — we all adopt personas strategically, by context and audience — and political identity is no exception. Much of what circulates as political conviction is costume: the flags in the bio, the team vocabulary, the ritual denunciations. Call the honest version of this LARPitarianism, after live-action role-play, and give it a one-line constitution: *everybody should be free to LARP; nobody should be forced to LARP.*

The mantra is playful; the content is not. Its two clauses are the voluntarist position on political identity. The first defends every person's right to adopt, perform, and discard social and political roles as they choose — including roles others find ridiculous. The second forbids conscripting anyone into a role: no compulsory identities, no mandatory rituals, no demand that your neighbor wear your team's costume or be treated as the enemy. Most political labeling malpractice violates the second clause — it is forced LARP, the insistence that people you have never met are already cast, wardrobe and all, in your drama's villain role. The label-inflator is a casting director nobody hired. You are free to play the antifascist crusader; you are not free to conscript the rest of us as extras.

## Retiring the Compressed Axes

The deeper conclusion of this chapter is not about any single word. "Socialist" and "fascist" fail for the same reason: they are compressions — attempts to project the high-dimensional space of political arrangements onto a single line, or a single epithet. The left–right axis is the grandest compression of all, and the three-socialisms table shows what it destroys: it files mortal structural twins at opposite ends and structural opposites side by side. Any compression that lossy will be exploited, and the equivocations, the motte-and-baileys, and the label warfare of contemporary politics are exactly that exploitation.

Taxonomy and discipline are the repair this chapter can offer: split the words, publish the table, apply the razor. But the honest endpoint of the argument is that the compressed axes should not be repaired so much as retired. What politics actually needs is more dimensions, not better epithets — a way of locating political arrangements in the full space of their structural properties: how power is held, whether exit exists, what feedback constrains the rulers, how much of life the system claims. In that space, "how far left?" dissolves as a question, and the question that replaces it — is this arrangement inside or outside the region where agency remains viable? — turns out to be answerable. Building that space is the work of [The Admissible Region](34-the-admissible-region.md). This chapter has only sharpened the old tools; that one replaces them.
