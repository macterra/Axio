> *"The 'realist' view of international affairs is false. People don't, and can't, act in their interests because they don't know what those are until they have a world view."*
> — David Deutsch

*National interest* appears just before wars, surveillance, secrecy, sacrifice, and coercion. It is spoken as a hard constraint rather than a contestable judgment. That cadence does most of the work.

[The Individualist Premise](01-the-individualist-premise.md) identified the category error of attributing mental states and moral standing to abstractions. Here two errors stack: interests are treated as objective facts, and nations as agents capable of possessing them. The phrases *national interest* and *collective will* convert those errors into authority to compel individuals.

## Interests Are Not Primitives

An interest presupposes a model of the world, causal theory, evaluation of outcomes, assessment of risk, and time horizon. Actors often fail not by irrationally pursuing known interests but by misidentifying them through a false worldview. Political realism treats interests as inputs; they are conclusions of fallible theories wearing the costume of premises.

Hard constraints remain real. Annihilation forecloses continued agency and suffocation forecloses life. But constraints rule out futures without selecting among those left open. Moving from “we cannot survive this” to “therefore we must do that” crosses from physics into interpretation. Physics closes doors; it does not issue policy.

Interests are therefore conjectures about which actions will produce futures an agent would endorse. Held as contestable and revisable hypotheses, the term is useful. Trouble begins when a conjecture becomes a command.

## Nations Are Not Agents

Even objective interests would need a subject. A nation has no unified belief state, value function, or vantage. It is a changing pattern of individuals, institutions, incentives, traditions, and power relations. Treating that aggregate as a coherent chooser is an analytical convenience.

Shorthand can be useful. “Britain sought to contain France” compresses decisions filtered through institutions. The error begins when compression becomes ontology and then justification: one faction's judgment is enforced as necessity in the name of a national subject that cannot choose.

## The Vanishing Intersection

Could aggregation produce a *collective will*? Sean Carroll calls government “the organized expression of our collective will.” The natural operational definition is the intersection of preferences shared by every member.

Small and intimate groups may have a substantial intersection. As group size and experience vary, it shrinks. At national scale it approaches emptiness. Abstract values such as fairness, freedom, prosperity, and security do not rescue it. Agreement fractures when fairness becomes a tax schedule or security a surveillance program, conscription order, or war. Consensus on a word is not consensus on anything a government can do.

Government thus expresses majority, elite, or institutional preferences under the moral cover of unanimity. “Collective will” borrows the standing of consent for decisions that lack it. This does not settle when governance is justified — [The Grey Zone](05-the-grey-zone.md) does that work — but it prevents a group fiction from making coercion disappear.

## How the Fallacy Licenses Harm

The phrases endure because indeterminacy is useful. Whoever holds power can fill an empty claim; necessity then shields it from reply.

Once a policy becomes “the national interest,” disagreement is moralized into disloyalty. A critic with a rival model becomes a traitor to a will already declared. Failure becomes unavoidable sacrifice, insufficient resolve, or a necessary cost. A framework in which no outcome counts against policy does not guide action; it insulates action.

Individuals become instruments, and compliance becomes duty. The strongest form appears in defense, where national interest supports the claim that protection requires sovereignty — a claim [The Hardest Case: Defense](31-the-hardest-case-defense.md) examines directly. It also appears in [AI-safety politics](../03-minds-and-machines/33-the-politics-of-safety.md) when “alignment” presupposes a collective target that does not exist.

## Catastrophe as Epistemic Failure

The most destructive twentieth-century regimes did not fail from insufficient commitment. They enforced false models and suppressed correction. Once dissent is criminalized, rational execution makes the error more coherent, optimized, and catastrophic.

Censorship, propaganda, and punishment are therefore symptoms of epistemic weakness: judgment defended by force because it cannot remain exposed to argument. Free speech, criticism, and contestable authority are a society's correction machinery. National-interest rhetoric switches that machinery off by presenting one model as necessity.

## Best Current Judgment, Not Destiny

Leaders still must act under pressure and uncertainty. Corrigibility is not paralysis. Planes fly and treatments proceed before knowledge is complete; successful practice keeps decisions open to criticism, reversal, and learning.

Political action shapes possible futures without knowing which will occur. National-interest claims collapse that uncertainty prematurely and enforce one account of which futures count. The defensible alternative is to name interests as fallible judgments, make them answerable to outcomes, and preserve correction. [Centrism as a Discipline](27-centrism-as-a-discipline.md) owns that governing method.

There is no national interest in the sense the rhetoric requires and no collective will for government to express. There are agents acting through imperfect models. Abandoning the fiction requires neither passivity nor consensus, only leaders willing to say: *this is our best current judgment, not our destiny* — and to remain answerable when it proves wrong.
