**Thesis.** The volume capstone. The deepest case for free institutions is not efficiency but epistemics: markets are the only large-scale mechanism that lets value emerge from agents' choices without an authority defining value in advance — and that principle, generalized, is axiocracy: governance by value discovery. Value means demonstrated exchange value under property, scarcity, purchasing power, and consent — not moral worth ("a market price is not an entry in the Book of Life"); voting compresses preferences into one outcome where the winner gets the policy and the loser gets ruled, while prices permit divergence without domination. The anti-axiocratic taxonomy names the corruptions: fiat inflation, regulatory capture, bailouts, protectionism — coercion as signal corruption, epistemic damage. The institutional layer is the Mars constitution: axiocratic constitutionalism rejecting both democratic confiscation and corporate sovereignty; enforcement without general sovereignty (force constrained, decomposed, audited, liable); command confined to physics; contestability where redundancy is unaffordable; engineered exit — portable assets, anti-retaliation, pre-funded repatriation — because "exit is not a slogan; it is an engineering requirement for liberty under dependency."
