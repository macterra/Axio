Were the Nazis socialists? The dispute never ends, and its endlessness is the interesting fact about it. One side points to the party's name, its anti-capitalist rhetoric, its subordination of industry to the state. The other points to the persistence of private ownership, the crushed unions, the murdered communists. Both sides hold real evidence. Neither is being stupid. And the argument cannot converge, because it is not actually an argument about Nazi Germany. It is an argument about where to place a three-dimensional object on a one-dimensional line — a question that has no answer because the question is malformed.

[Words That Lost Their Teeth](26-words-that-lost-their-teeth.md) diagnosed this as semantic inflation and prescribed taxonomy: split "socialism" into the three things it names, apply the razor, hold the line on usage. That chapter also admitted the limit of its own remedy. Sharper words are still words arranged along the old axis, and the old axis is the disease. The left–right spectrum compresses authority, economics, moral scope, and coercion into a single coordinate and then forces every political system to occupy a point along it. Once that compression occurs, confusion is no longer an accident. Systems that differ radically in structure become rhetorically interchangeable, while systems that share deep similarities are treated as moral opposites. The Nazi-"socialism" dispute is not a historical curiosity; it is a predictable artifact of a representational failure. The same collapse appears in contemporary arguments about taxation, welfare, regulation, markets, nationalism, and global governance. The spectrum cannot encode the distinctions people are implicitly arguing about, so disagreement degenerates into semantic noise.

Most political disagreement is misdiagnosed this way. What looks like a clash of values is usually a clash of coordinate systems: people argue as though they are disagreeing about outcomes, when in fact they are reasoning inside a model that has already erased the distinctions doing the real work. If political analysis is to regain traction, the spectrum has to be abandoned — not sharpened, not supplemented, abandoned. This chapter builds its replacement.

## The Questions That Actually Classify

Political systems are not defined by what they call themselves. They are defined by how power is arranged and enforced. Any classification that survives contact with reality must answer three questions independently.

The first concerns **command**: where authority ultimately resides and how far it reaches — whether it is distributed or centralized, reversible or absolute, grounded in delegation or treated as intrinsic. The second concerns **ownership**: who determines the use of productive resources, how contestable that control is, and whether economic power can be reassigned without coercion. The third concerns **beneficiaries**: who the system is for — whom it treats as morally salient, and whom it is willing to exclude or instrumentalize.

These questions vary independently in the real world. Authority can be centralized or distributed regardless of how ownership is allocated. Ownership can be private, collective, or conditional regardless of who counts morally. Moral scope can be universal, national, class-based, or exclusionary without fixing either of the others. Political rhetoric routinely collapses these distinctions because collapsing them is persuasive; analysis cannot afford that convenience.

So the simplest faithful representation of political systems is not a line but a phase space with at least three independent dimensions. This alone dissolves much of the standing confusion. The Nazi case stops being paradoxical the moment the axes are separated: centralized absolute command, ownership private on paper but conditional on political obedience in fact, beneficiaries explicitly exclusionary. Whether to call that "socialist" stops mattering, because the coordinates carry the information the label was failing to carry. Systems that were previously misclassified fall naturally into place once authority, ownership, and moral scope are allowed to vary separately.

Yet even this three-dimensional model is incomplete.

## The Missing Variable

Two regimes can occupy similar positions along the axes of command, ownership, and beneficiaries while producing radically different lived experiences. One allows planning, dissent, and refusal. The other induces opacity, caution, and fear. The difference does not lie in ideology. It lies in whether agents can model the system acting on them.

Call this variable **evaluability**. It measures whether rules are legible before they bind, whether obligations can be anticipated, whether authority can be challenged without violence, and whether participation can be refused without catastrophic loss. Evaluability explains why some systems feel navigable even when authority is strong, and why others feel suffocating even when they claim benevolence. It also explains why so many political theories fail to predict lived experience: it is rarely modeled explicitly. It is, though, no informal notion. It is the variable whose collapse under scale defines a Leviathan — the argument of [the previous chapter](33-the-limits-of-leviathan.md) — and its formal treatment, as authority reduced to traceable, revocable, expirable tokens whose lawful transformation *is* governance, belongs to [Governance Without Gods](../04-axionic-agency/12-governance-without-gods.md).

The operational test of evaluability is **exit**. A system whose rules you cannot model in advance is one you can only escape; a system you cannot escape is one whose rules you are forced to gamble on. And here a distinction must be drawn precisely, because everything downstream depends on it. Exit costs may be high. Leaving any social order forfeits local advantages, relationships, accumulated standing, and opportunities — that is the ordinary price of relocation and withdrawal, and no arrangement in history has been free of it. What disqualifies a system is not costly exit but *blocked* exit: departure prevented or punished by [credible threats of imposed harm](../05-value-and-ethics/09-what-counts-as-coercion.md). The emigrant who loses his neighborhood has paid a price. The emigrant who loses his family to reprisal, his savings to confiscation, or his life at the border wall has been imprisoned. The first is a fact about the world; the second is a fact about the regime.

## A Constraint, Not a Position

Where does Axio itself sit in this space? It doesn't. Axionic agents are not left, right, statist, anarchist, capitalist, or socialist. Axio operates at a different level of abstraction: it is a meta-framework that constrains political systems by agency-preservation, independent of their distributive, cultural, or moral goals. It does not specify which political order should be preferred. It specifies which political orders remain compatible with the continued existence of agents who can model, contest, and revise the structures acting upon them.

Two honesty clauses before proceeding. Axio is normative in the sense that it values agency; it does not claim moral objectivity, inevitability, or physical law. And the phase-space language is structural modeling, not a claim that politics reduces to physics. The point of the vocabulary is to make distinctions computable that the spectrum made invisible — nothing more, and nothing less.

## The Admissible Region

From an Axionic perspective, political systems carve out regions of the phase space. Some regions preserve agency under variation and stress; others reliably collapse it. The **admissible region** is defined by structural conditions rather than policy choices:

1. **Delegability.** Authority must be traceable to agent-level delegation rather than terminating in sacred or self-justifying power.
2. **Contestability.** Control relations must remain challengeable without violence.
3. **Voluntary participation.** Participation must not be enforced through credible threats of imposed harm that the agent did not endorse under non-manipulated conditions.
4. **Legibility.** Obligations must be knowable in advance rather than appearing retroactively or opaquely.
5. **Exit.** Leaving must remain possible without destroying the agent's capacity to act — where, per the distinction above, only threatened harm disqualifies, not relocation cost.

These conditions do not select a single political form. They license a range of arrangements: markets, cooperatives, federations, voluntary collectivities, even temporary states. What they exclude are systems that close off contestation, collapse evaluability, or convert participation into coercion. And the test is conjunctive: a system leaves the admissible region the moment any one condition fails.

Readers of [The Grey Zone](05-the-grey-zone.md) should recognize the shape of this instrument, because it is the same discipline at a different scale. The grey-zone procedure interrogates a single coercive act: name the agents, specify the violation, state the background conditions, scale the evidence to the severity, bound the response, check what the enforcement machinery will become. The admissible-region conditions interrogate an entire political order at once: trace the authority, test the contestation, audit the participation, read the obligations, price the exit. Both refuse to let a moralized label — *justice*, *safety*, *the people*, *socialism*, *freedom* — substitute for condition-checking. Both scale the burden of justification with the power being exercised. The grey zone is the microscope; the admissible region is the map. One discipline, two magnifications.

## Mapping Real Regimes

Once political systems are treated as points in a structured space, the framework's value becomes empirical rather than rhetorical. Regimes stop being moral archetypes and become structural cases.

**Nazi Germany** fails everything simultaneously. Authority terminated in personalist sovereignty; delegation existed rhetorically but not structurally — power could not be reconstructed back to agent endorsement in any meaningful sense. Ownership formally remained private, yet control was contingent on political obedience, eliminating contestability. Moral scope was explicitly exclusionary. Evaluability collapsed under retroactive law and discretionary enforcement. Exit was treated as betrayal. Far outside the region — and notice that the verdict required no ruling on whether it was "really" socialist. The question that consumed the old axis does not even arise on the map.

The **Soviet Union** occupied a different part of the phase space and exited the region for related reasons. Authority consolidated into party sovereignty without reversible delegation. Ownership centralized without challenge mechanisms. Class-framed moral scope was imposed rather than chosen. Evaluability existed at the level of ideology and formal rules while outcomes turned on discretionary enforcement and political alignment. Exit carried existential cost. Failure on delegability, contestability, and exit suffices, whatever the intent.

The **People's Republic of China** shows exactly why one-dimensional models flounder. Its economic coordinates are mixed and pragmatic: market mechanisms coexist with extensive state direction, and the left–right axis has no idea what to do with that. The decisive failures lie on the axes the spectrum doesn't have. Authority terminates in party supremacy without reconstructible delegation. Contestation is tightly bounded. Evaluability is selectively high for compliant agents and sharply degraded for dissenters. Exit remains conditional and politically constrained. Economic flexibility does not compensate for the structural treatment of agency — a sentence the spectrum cannot even express.

Liberal democracies require more care, because they sit near the boundary rather than clearly on either side. The **United States** is the instructive case. Authority is constitutionally delegated and formally reversible; ownership is largely private and contestable; moral scope is individualist in principle. Evaluability remains high enough to permit planning and dissent — but it is eroding through regulatory opacity and administrative sprawl, the drift this volume has tracked from [The Censorship Machine](10-the-censorship-machine.md) onward. Exit is possible but costly, and the costs fall unevenly. The verdict is conditional: the United States remains admissible so long as its coercion thresholds and exit penalties stay below agency-destroying levels, and its trajectory on those margins matters more than its founding documents.

**Switzerland** sits comfortably inside — and for reasons worth stating exactly, because they are unglamorous. Authority remains federated and traceable through delegation. Ownership is private and contestable. Moral scope is locally grounded rather than universalized. Evaluability is unusually high, sustained by transparency, decentralization, and frequent consent refresh. Exit exists formally and practically. Nothing in this list appeals to national character. Switzerland's admissibility rests on structure rather than virtue, which is precisely what makes it reproducible.

**Singapore** is admissible but brittle. Authority is centralized but predictable; ownership is private under clear rules; evaluability is high, which makes the system navigable despite limited political contestation; exit exists and is widely exercised. Singapore stays inside not because contestation is strong but because other agency-preserving mechanisms partially compensate for its weakness. That is a legitimate position in the space — the conditions are conjunctive, but compensation at the margins is real — and it is an exposed one. With one condition already thin, further erosion of contestability or exit would matter disproportionately.

**Estonia** and the post-authoritarian republics like it demonstrate the converse of the Leviathan argument: digital transparency and legible governance can preserve evaluability at scale. Authority remains reconstructible, ownership contestable, exit practical. Complexity alone does not destroy agency; opacity does.

Finally, the stateless and quasi-stateless systems — open markets, open-source ecosystems, voluntary protocols — clarify the lower bounds of command. They typically maximize evaluability and exit by default, which is why [Exit to Protocol](32-exit-to-protocol.md) casts them as the state's successors. But that chapter planted a caveat, and this is where it comes due. The primary risk of voluntary systems arises when network effects convert coordination into dependency. A platform, a settlement layer, or a protocol that everyone may formally leave — but that no one can leave without losing the capacity to transact, speak, or coordinate at all — has made exit nominal rather than real. When that happens, a formally voluntary system drifts out of the admissible region, and the Axionic test applies to it as strictly as to any state. Voluntariness is a property of the exit that actually exists, not of the signup page.

Across the sweep, one pattern: systems fail because authority becomes irreducible, contestation collapses, or exit turns into a credible threat of imposed harm. Systems remain admissible when those failure modes are structurally blocked — regardless of ideology, and regardless of what they call themselves.

## What the Map Explains

Several of this volume's running tensions resolve in this vocabulary.

Coercion justified by benevolent intent still fails when it destroys exit or evaluability — the grey zone said this of individual acts, and the region says it of whole orders. Inequality alone does not disqualify a system when agency remains intact, which is why the egalitarian chapters attacked coercive leveling rather than disparity itself. Prosperity does not imply legitimacy when participation is compulsory — Singapore earns its place by exit and legibility, not GDP. Moral universalism becomes suspect when it overrides consent in the name of aggregate outcomes: a universalized beneficiary axis cannot redeem a collapsed exit condition.

And the volume's hardest standing tension — between [Law and Power](13-law-and-power.md) and [Anarchy Is Not Chaos](30-anarchy-is-not-chaos.md) — finally gets its resolution vocabulary. One chapter showed that law binds only where power is balanced, and that hegemons operate outside the law's effective domain; the other showed that the international system, a working anarchy, produces real order without any sovereign. Both are true, and the spectrum's categories — lawful versus lawless, governed versus anarchic — cannot hold them simultaneously. The region's categories can. The international order is admissible where reciprocal constraint reaches: among roughly balanced powers, obligations are legible, contestation proceeds without violence, and the arrangement sits inside the region without any Leviathan above it. At the top of the power distribution, the conditions fail — authority answers to no delegation, contestation is impossible, and for the small state facing a hegemon there is no exit — and that portion of the order sits outside the region, whatever the lawbooks say. Anarchy is not disqualified by its statelessness, and hegemony is not redeemed by its legal apparatus. The question was never *is there a sovereign?* The question is whether every actor, at every altitude, remains inside the conditions.

Many political disputes persist because the participants are arguing across different dimensions without realizing it — one defending an ownership arrangement, the other attacking an exit condition, both convinced they disagree about "capitalism." The admissible-region framing forces the dimensions into view. That will not settle every dispute. It will, for the first time in a long while, let the parties disagree about the same object.

## Inside or Outside

Political evolution is unlikely to converge on a single ideology, and the map explains why it doesn't need to. Within the admissible region, pluralism is possible: markets and cooperatives, federations and city-states, thick communities and thin protocols, all licensed so long as the conditions hold. Outside it, domination emerges regardless of intent — the benevolent planner and the vicious one converge once contestation and exit are gone. What competition, scale, and stress select for is not a point in the space but a region of it: the arrangements that preserve agency under pressure are the ones agents keep choosing when choice remains structurally possible. Digital governance, modular citizenship, protocol-based coordination, exit-driven competition between systems — these are not utopian aspirations. They are structural responses to the selection pressure that evaluability and exit impose.

The conditions license a range; they do not select a point within it. But nothing forbids designing one. The final chapter of this volume describes [Axiocracy](35-axiocracy.md): not another boundary case to be audited, but a system engineered from the conditions outward — delegation, contestation, legibility, and exit built in as load-bearing structure — a designed point deep inside the region.

For two centuries, political identity has been an address on a line: how far left, how far right. The line was always a compression, and its artifacts — the interminable label wars, the structural twins filed as opposites, the oppositions that were never really there — were the price of the compression. Drop it, and the real question emerges with the answer built into its form. The future of politics is not left or right. It is inside or outside the region where agency remains viable.
