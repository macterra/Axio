Across cultures with little contact, political orders repeatedly converged on concentrated authority, expected submission, and punished dissent. Liberalism appeared late, locally, and incompletely. When a form recurs across eras and cultures, its causes cannot be only local villains or doctrines. Authoritarianism is humanity's psychological default; liberalism is a fragile achievement that must be maintained.

This chapter makes that case through the psychology of submission, the cycle by which factions abandon liberal principles in power, and the three axes separating authoritarian from liberal order.

## The Instinct for Submission

Six familiar forces converge on the same output.

**Hierarchy paid.** Small groups under scarcity and threat benefited from rapid coordination. Selection shaped followers as well as leaders: deference under threat is old machinery optimized for group survival, not individual agency.

**Thinking is expensive, and outsourcing it is cheap.** Authority precomputes beliefs and decisions. The savings grow with stress, precisely when authoritarians recruit.

**Conformity polices the group from inside.** When ostracism once threatened survival, alignment with the group was adaptive. Authoritarianism leverages that circuitry rather than installing it.

**Certainty is a consumer good.** Dogma offers fixed answers; [Conditionalism](../02-conditionalism/02-all-truth-is-conditional.md) demands permanent openness to error. Comfort usually outsells accurate uncertainty.

**Fear is easily weaponized.** A [credible conditional threat](../05-value-and-ethics/09-what-counts-as-coercion.md) makes submission first a calculation, then a habit, and finally an internalized loyalty that economizes on enforcement.

**Agency is a burden as well as a good.** Centralizing agency in a leader or institution also transfers responsibility. Abdication feels like acquittal.

Each force discounts the cost of being an agent: hierarchy discounts coordination, authority thought, conformity conflict, dogma doubt, submission fear, and abdication responsibility. The strongman is met halfway by followers whose instincts fit his offer. The same psychology supplies the ordinary personnel of [The Censorship Machine](10-the-censorship-machine.md) and made the regimes in [Against Marxist-Leninist Communism](21-against-communism.md) administrable.

The inversion matters: liberalism is not the natural condition from which tyranny deviates. Autonomous and critically rational life is a deliberate project that every generation must relearn against instincts that do not disappear.

## The Cycle

The pull appears inside liberal orders as a regular cycle: factions champion liberal principles out of power and abandon them after gaining it.

Out of power, free speech, tolerance, autonomy, and limited government build coalitions and constrain incumbents. In power, the same principles protect rivals and obstruct coalition maintenance. Values rented for the climb become liabilities at the top.

The pattern is symmetric. The left embraces censorship when it controls institutions and calls it protection from harmful ideas. The right adopts coercive measures against opponents and calls them stability or national security. Each faction documents the other's betrayal while treating its own as necessity. Fear supplies the emergency, certainty the justification, and conformity the applause.

The timing is diagnostic. Betrayals arrive at the transition into power across factions, the signature of structure rather than isolated character. A manifesto therefore forecasts less than incentives: ask what a principle will cost its advocates once it constrains their own coalition.

Unopposed, the cycle settles into competing authoritarianisms. Each overreach recruits for the other, and the contest becomes whose control rather than whether control. Only principled behavior interrupts it: observing the same limits in power and out, tolerating dissent one could silence, and accepting the cost of restraint. Those actors make liberal order more than a truce between factions awaiting their turn. [Centrism as a Discipline](27-centrism-as-a-discipline.md) develops that commitment as a method of governance.

## Three Axes

Andrew Doyle describes authoritarianism as humanity's default and liberalism as freedom from ideological dogmatism. The first claim is this chapter's thesis; the second becomes more exact along three axes.

**Authority versus autonomy.** Authoritarianism centralizes decisions and demands conformity. Liberalism treats individual self-determination as the end rather than an instrument of a collective project.

**Coercion versus consent.** Coercion is authoritarianism's ordinary instrument. Liberalism places coercion under a burden of justification. It does not pretend force disappears; [The Grey Zone](05-the-grey-zone.md) specifies the evidence and constraints required when it does not.

**Dogmatic certainty versus conditional epistemology.** If doctrine is infallible, dissent is mere error and coercion in truth's service becomes a favor. If knowledge is fallible, institutions must keep criticism legal, authority revocable, and cooperation voluntary. The politics follows from the epistemology.

Both orientations seek coordination. The distinction is how they produce it: imposed from above by overriding agency, or grown through consent, reciprocity, and rights. Law likewise divides into rule *by* law, which binds subjects but not rulers, and rule *of* law, which constrains those who write it.

Liberalism is therefore not an absence of conviction. It is the political embodiment of a conviction about fallibility, built to survive being wrong. That discipline must be learned and paid for in every generation. The next chapter asks why, even when established and productive, it so often loses.
