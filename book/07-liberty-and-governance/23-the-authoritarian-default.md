Consider the shape of human political history from a height. Sumer crowned god-kings. Egypt deified its pharaohs. China ruled by the mandate of heaven, Rome by the divinity of emperors, medieval Europe by the divine right of kings; the Aztecs, the Inca, the Khmer, the Ottomans, the politburos of the twentieth century — societies with no contact and nothing else in common converged, again and again, on the same design: authority concentrated above, submission expected below, dissent treated as disease. Authoritarianism needed no missionaries. It was invented independently, everywhere, from local materials.

Liberalism was not. The idea that the individual owns his own life — that authority must justify itself to the governed rather than the reverse, that truth is settled by argument rather than decree — appeared late, in a few places, incompletely, and it has been contested every day since. Where authoritarian orders regenerate spontaneously after every collapse, liberal orders have to be built, maintained, and defended, and they begin to decay the moment the maintenance stops.

An asymmetry that stark demands an explanation, and the explanation cannot be local. It cannot be a story about particular villains, particular ideologies, or a particular unlucky century, because the pattern is too universal for any of those to carry it. When a political form recurs in every era and every culture, its causes lie not in the circumstances but in the species. The uncomfortable conclusion is that authoritarianism comes naturally to us: it is humanity's psychological default, and liberalism is a precious, fragile, unnatural achievement. This chapter makes that case three ways — the psychology that makes submission attractive, the dynamics by which that psychology reclaims even liberal institutions, and the definition of what, exactly, the two orientations disagree about.

## The Instinct for Submission

Six forces, each well-documented on its own, converge on the same output. None of them is a defect in this or that population; all of them are standard equipment.

**Hierarchy paid.** Humans evolved in small, tightly-knit groups whose survival depended on coordinated action under scarcity and threat. In that environment, a clear authority structure was not oppression; it was engineering. Quick, unified responses to danger, reduced internal friction, decisive allocation of scarce resources — groups with efficient, stable hierarchies outlasted groups that deliberated while the raiders crossed the river. Selection accordingly shaped followers as thoroughly as leaders: the disposition to close ranks and defer under threat is not a malfunction of ancient machinery but the machinery working as built. What evolution optimized was group survival, not individual agency, and it shows.

**Thinking is expensive, and outsourcing it is cheap.** Autonomous agency demands constant vigilance, cognitive effort, and emotional resilience — every decision owned, every belief audited. Human cognition economizes with heuristics, and the grandest heuristic of all is authority: accept the prescribed beliefs and norms, and an enormous portion of life's decisions are precomputed. The savings are real, and they grow with stress. In uncertain or threatening environments, when cognitive resources are most taxed, the shortcut becomes most attractive — which is precisely when authoritarians come recruiting.

**Conformity polices the group from inside.** Conformity bias and in-group favoritism run deep, and for good ancestral reasons: for most of human history, ostracism was a death sentence, so aligning with majority opinion and authoritative dictate was elementary self-preservation. Authoritarianism does not have to install this circuitry; it merely leverages what is already there. Rigid conformity strengthens cohesion, makes internal disagreement costly enough to be rare, and simplifies every social interaction into a test with one right answer.

**Certainty is a consumer good.** Humans crave epistemic security — clear, comforting, unequivocal accounts of the world — and recoil from ambiguity. Authoritarian narratives sell exactly this: absolute truth, fixed moral clarity, an answer for everything. The honest alternative — that [all truth is conditional](../02-conditionalism/02-all-truth-is-conditional.md), that every claim holds only under background conditions that must be continually re-examined — is correct and demanding. It requires reflection, humility, and the permanent possibility of being wrong. Between comfortable certainty and accurate uncertainty, most people, most of the time, buy comfort. Dogma outsells doubt for the same reason candy outsells medicine.

**Fear is easily weaponized.** Coercion — [the credible threat of actual harm to gain compliance](../05-value-and-ethics/09-what-counts-as-coercion.md) — exploits the deepest aversion an organism has. Faced with a credible threat, submission is at first a calculation, then a habit, and finally something worse: internalized, so that the subject polices himself and calls it loyalty. This is the self-reinforcing dynamic that lets authoritarian systems economize on enforcement — the fear does most of the work, and after enough time the fear is no longer even experienced as fear.

**Agency is a burden as well as a good.** Autonomy entails continuous personal accountability, and the weight of it — every outcome traceable to one's own choices — can be crushing. By centralizing agency in a figure or an institution, authoritarianism relieves the individual of responsibility for his own life. Every abdication is also an acquittal: whatever happens next is no longer your fault. The trade is emotionally appealing and ethically ruinous, and it is offered fresh every day.

Put the six together and notice what they share: each is a discount on the cost of being an agent. Hierarchy discounts coordination, authority discounts thought, conformity discounts conflict, dogma discounts doubt, submission discounts fear, and abdication discounts responsibility. Authoritarianism is not an alien imposition on human nature; it is human nature's path of least resistance, and this is the crucial point missed by every theory that treats tyranny as something done *to* populations. The strongman does not conquer a nation of frustrated liberals. He is met halfway by followers whose instincts his offer fits like a key. Nor does the pattern require a strongman at all: the same psychology, operating at low intensity through ordinary institutions, supplies the willing personnel of [The Censorship Machine](10-the-censorship-machine.md) — no commissars needed, just people choosing certainty and safety one referral at a time. At full dose, it is what made the regimes anatomized in [Against Communism](21-against-communism.md) administrable: those systems ran on tanks, but also, and indispensably, on submission.

The inversion follows, and it reorganizes everything downstream of it. Liberalism is not the natural condition from which authoritarianism deviates; authoritarianism is the natural condition from which liberalism deviates. Liberal, autonomous, critically rational life is a deliberate and continuous struggle — a cultural and philosophical project that must be re-argued, re-taught, and re-defended in every generation, against six instincts that never go away. There is no point at which the work is finished, because the pull it works against is not historical but constitutional.

## The Cycle

If the pull is constant, it should be visible inside liberal orders too — not as coups, but as a repeating pattern in ordinary politics. It is, and the pattern is regular enough to state as a rule: political actors champion liberal principles when out of power and abandon them upon gaining it.

The mechanism is that the principles are held instrumentally. To a faction out of power, universal liberal values — free speech, individual autonomy, tolerance — are the best tools available. They build the broadest possible coalitions, win public sympathy, and indict incumbents by standards the incumbents profess. But the moment the faction gains authority, its priorities shift from coalition-building to coalition-maintenance, and the same values change sign. Free speech now protects the opposition. Tolerance now shelters the rival. Limited government now limits *us*. Principles that were load-bearing on the way up become obstacles at the top — because they were rented for the climb, never owned.

The cycle is symmetric, and the symmetry is diagnostic. The left, historically the champion of free expression, reliably embraces censorship once it holds institutional power, rationalizing it as necessary combat against harmful and dangerous ideas. The right, long professing fidelity to limited government, reliably adopts authoritarian measures against its opponents once dominant, citing social stability or national security. Each side documents the other's betrayal meticulously while experiencing its own as an unfortunate necessity — and the psychology of the previous section explains why the abandonment feels righteous from inside rather than hypocritical. Fear supplies the emergency, certainty supplies the justification, and conformity supplies the applause. Weaponized fear needs an enemy, and the opponent's excesses — which are real, since the opponent is running the same cycle — supply an inexhaustible one.

Notice what the rule buys you: prediction. If principles were a matter of individual character, betrayals would arrive at random — this leader weak, that one venal. Instead they arrive on schedule, at the transition into power, in whichever faction is transitioning, which is exactly the signature of structure rather than character. It follows that a movement's manifesto is close to worthless as a forecast of its behavior in office. The forecast that works reads its incentives: ask not what a faction says it values, but what those values will cost it once the coalition it must hold together is the governing one. And it follows, too, that the observer who catalogues the other side's betrayals while trusting his own side's promises has misunderstood the phenomenon — the cycle is not a fact about *them*.

That is the equilibrium a society falls into when the cycle runs unopposed: competing authoritarianisms, each justifying its abuses by citing its opponents' excesses. Each faction's overreach becomes the other's recruiting poster; each round of retaliation licenses the next; and the contest stops being between liberty and control and becomes a contest over whose control. Nothing in the machinery ever reverses itself, because every actor inside it is behaving rationally by its incentives.

What interrupts the ratchet is a different kind of actor. Principled behavior — upholding the same values in power and out of it, regardless of short-term advantage — is inherently costly. It means sacrificing expediency, enduring discomfort, and tolerating dissent one has the means to silence. Because it is costly, it is rare; because the cycle cannot be broken any other way, it is indispensable. Principled actors are what make a liberal order more than a truce between authoritarianisms waiting for their turn: they stabilize expectations, reduce the transactional friction of politics, and keep norms of accountability and transparency alive by observing them when observation hurts. Breaking the authoritarian cycle requires valuing liberal principles as terminal goals — things held for their own sake — and not as instrumental tactics. What that commitment looks like as a working discipline of governance is the subject of [Centrism as a Discipline](27-centrism-as-a-discipline.md).

## Three Axes

Andrew Doyle has argued that authoritarianism is humanity's default state, and that liberalism is not truly an ideology at all but the absence of ideological dogmatism — a method for negotiating our flawed and uncertain condition. The first half of that is the argument of this chapter. The second half is nearly right, and getting it exactly right requires saying what the two orientations actually disagree about. The opposition runs along three axes.

**Authority versus autonomy.** Authoritarianism centralizes decision-making in hierarchical authorities — political, cultural, or religious — and demands submission and conformity toward them. Liberalism takes individual autonomy as foundational: agents own their own lives, and self-determination is not a means to some further political end but the end itself.

**Coercion versus consent.** The authoritarian project inherently runs on coercion; the credible threat of harm is not a regrettable last resort but the ordinary instrument of rule. Liberalism inverts the burden: it treats every act of coercion as standing in need of justification, and builds its institutions on voluntary consent and negotiation instead. It does not pretend coercion can be abolished outright — the conditions under which it survives the justification test are the business of [The Grey Zone](05-the-grey-zone.md) — but it insists that coercion is always the thing on trial, never the judge.

**Dogmatic certainty versus conditional epistemology.** Authoritarianism insists its truths are fixed, immutable, and non-negotiable. Liberalism is epistemically humble: truths are conditional, context-dependent, and permanently subject to scrutiny and revision. This third axis is the deepest, because the other two follow from it. If your doctrine is infallible, then dissent is simply error, error has no rights, and coercion in truth's service is a favor to its victims. If instead all truth is conditional, then you need institutions that keep revision possible — free speech to surface criticism, consent to register values, revocable authority to correct mistakes. The politics falls out of the epistemology.

State the axes that starkly and an objection arrives on schedule: liberal societies also want order. They have laws, police, courts, hierarchies of their own. Doesn't everyone want stability? Yes — and the shared goal is precisely where the distinction sharpens rather than blurs. Both orientations aim at social coordination. Authoritarianism imposes order coercively, from above, by overriding agency; liberalism grows order voluntarily, out of consent, reciprocity, and rights. Order is not the differentiator. The technology for producing it is — and the liberal technology is the only one that produces order *for* its participants rather than extracting it *from* them.

The same clarification disposes of law. Authoritarian states have law in abundance — rule *by* law, the statute as an instrument of power, binding subjects but never rulers, applied arbitrarily and read most creatively against enemies. Liberal societies uphold rule *of* law: a universal constraint, uniform, predictable, and impartial, that limits even — especially — those in power. The presence of a legal code tells you nothing about which order you are living in. The test is whether the code binds the men who wrote it.

So Doyle's formulation can be sharpened. Liberalism is not merely an absence of dogmatism, as if it were defined by what it lacks. It is the political embodiment of a definite epistemic position: that human knowledge is fallible, that every truth is conditional, and that a decent political order must therefore be built to survive being wrong — criticism legal, authority revocable, cooperation voluntary. That is not the absence of conviction. It is a conviction about human fallibility, held firmly enough to build institutions around, and it is what makes liberalism uniquely suited to navigating an uncertain world without surrendering autonomy or reaching for the whip.

The picture, assembled: a default and a discipline. The default costs nothing, because it comes pre-installed — six instincts deep, regenerating after every collapse, patiently cycling through every faction that tastes power. The discipline must be learned, argued for, and paid for by every generation, and its rarest and most essential input is people who hold its principles when holding them hurts. That much explains why liberalism is always embattled. It does not yet explain why liberalism so often *loses* — why, even where it is established and demonstrably delivering, it keeps forfeiting contests to ideologies with worse track records and better stories. The answer is structural, and it is the subject of [Why Liberalism Loses](24-why-liberalism-loses.md).
