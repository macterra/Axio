**Thesis.** Alignment is only defined for agents — things that can recognize rules, respond to reasons, revise; for forces there is only prediction and containment. What makes something an agent is empirically bounded: destructive ablation identifies four load-bearing components (binding reasons, deliberative meaning, commitment revision, temporal continuity) whose removal collapses agency into mechanical operation — "the issue is not that an agent holds the wrong values, but that there is no agent capable of holding values in the first place." And agency must fail safely: the distinction between misaligned agency and agency collapse puts fault tolerance before value alignment.

**Sources.** you-cant-align-a-hurricane (Jan 27) is the spine and register: force vs agent, "you can prepare for a hurricane; you can align with a nation," control blunt and brittle under scale. alignment-is-a-domain-constraint (Dec 15) is the substance beneath it: some futures must be undefined rather than negatively valued — "not bad, not forbidden, just undefined" — and the deliberative-vs-physical reachability split (Rowhammer is a security failure, not an alignment failure). the-load-bearing-parts-of-agency (Jan 17, VIII.6 explainer) supplies the four pillars and the ablation methodology — note its subtitle "Why Some AI Systems Can Never Be Aligned" is this chapter's subtitle-level claim. alignment-after-agency (Jan 11) supplies misaligned-agency vs agency-collapse, permissible rupture ("agency may fail temporarily, provided its failure is explicit, bounded, and recoverable"), survivability-first engineering, and the honest note that this implies architectural change. axionic-commitments supplies the conditional-domain move (acceptance places you inside the framework's domain; rejection outside — "neither stance carries further implication") and the canonical agency-as-authorship / harm / coercion definitions.
