**Thesis.** Governance under plural authority is mechanically representable without any privileged chooser inside the kernel: authority is a traceable, revocable, expirable permission token ("closer to a cryptographic capability than to a ruler"), governance is nothing more than the lawful transformation of such tokens over time, and the kernel is a physics engine — it does not interpret, optimize, or choose. Removing every escape hatch has a price the program pays openly: conflict persists without arbitration, deadlock is lawful, authority decays unless renewed, refusal is the system behaving correctly, and failure regimes (deadlock, fragmentation, suppression, orphaned execution) are structural properties, not capability deficits. Meaning influences power only where it is given an explicit structural foothold — and at societal scale, the Sacrifice–Collapse Theorem draws the design boundary: any system whose performance improves by non-consensually degrading a captive class's agency drives toward closure.
