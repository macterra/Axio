The Pragmatic Self

Agency as Necessary Fiction

One of the deepest truths about human existence is hidden in plain sight, precisely because confronting it directly feels like pulling the existential rug out from under us:

Agency is fundamentally interpretive—not a metaphysical fact, but a pragmatic construction.

We intuitively see ourselves as unified, coherent agents—individual selves who make conscious decisions, steer our lives, and take responsibility for outcomes. This intuitive picture of agency seems self-evident, an unquestioned baseline assumption underpinning morality, law, and personal identity.

Yet neuroscience, psychology, and philosophy relentlessly undermine this comforting assumption. They reveal that the sense of a unified self, the "I" behind our eyes making choices, is not fundamental to reality. Instead, it is a narrative construct our brains continuously generate, retrospectively knitting together fragments of perception, impulse, deliberation, and habit into a coherent story. We do not find agency in nature; rather, we impose it upon a continuous flow of neural and physical processes.

At the metaphysical level, no discrete "agent" stands outside causality, pulling levers. Your brain, body, genetics, environment, and past experiences collectively shape every choice—none of these choices originate from an independent entity we call the self. Thus, from a purely metaphysical perspective, the idea of a self-directed agent making independent choices evaporates.

But here's the essential twist: agency isn't merely an illusion; it is a highly consequential pragmatic reality.

The Pragmatic Reality of Agency

  1. Agency Shapes Real Outcomes
    Even though agency is constructed, the narrative of self that you hold profoundly influences your thoughts, emotions, decisions, and actions. Your belief in personal agency directly shapes your life trajectory. It determines your goals, priorities, and moral judgments, impacting your relationships, career, health, and overall well-being.

  2. Agency Structures Social Interaction
    Human civilization is built around recognizing each other as agents capable of choice. Accountability, justice, responsibility, cooperation—all these rely on treating each other as decision-makers. Thus, the constructed self profoundly impacts social interactions, laws, and ethics. Society treats you as an agent, which reinforces your self-perception, creating a powerful feedback loop.

  3. Agency Grounds Ethics and Meaning
    Ethical reasoning—questions of right, wrong, justice, responsibility—assumes individuals are capable of choice. Even if metaphysically debatable, this assumption is pragmatically indispensable. Ethics without agency collapses into incoherence. Similarly, personal meaning, goals, and the narrative coherence of life depend critically on the pragmatic fiction of selfhood and agency.

Pragmatic vs. Metaphysical

Confusion arises precisely because humans instinctively conflate these two perspectives:

  • Metaphysical perspective: Agency is nonexistent as an independent causal force; decisions are outcomes of deterministic or probabilistic causal chains.

  • Pragmatic perspective: Agency is an indispensable narrative construct that shapes subjective experience, social cooperation, and ethical frameworks.

Recognizing this distinction frees us from confusion. Just as money, national borders, and laws are constructed yet powerfully impactful, agency's narrative construction does not diminish its practical significance.

The Consequences of Clarity

Understanding agency clearly as interpretive rather than metaphysical yields profound philosophical and ethical insights:

  • Responsibility becomes about shaping future behavior, not judging an intrinsic moral worth.

  • Personal identity becomes dynamic, a continually revised interpretation rather than a static essence.

  • Free will debates lose metaphysical urgency, shifting towards practical discussions of effective choices, accountability, and psychological autonomy.

Conclusion

Agency's constructed nature doesn't invalidate it; rather, it underscores its pragmatic necessity. Agency is consequential because it is the interpretive fiction that enables humans to cooperate, flourish, and find meaning.

Far from undermining human dignity, understanding agency's interpretive basis enriches our moral imagination, deepens our ethical reasoning, and clarifies the real—but pragmatic—nature of selfhood.
