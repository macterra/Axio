Volume 3 — Minds and Machines: Consciousness, Intelligence, and AI

We are the first generation to share the world with fluent non-minds — systems that reason, converse, and create while possessing, on this volume’s account, no experience, no preferences, and no stake. Making sense of that situation requires knowing what minds actually are, and this volume builds the answer from the ground up: models, regulation, meaning, consciousness, intelligence — and then turns the machinery on the machines.

The argument runs in eight parts. Part I lays the foundation in cybernetics: what a model is, why control requires one (the Good Regulator Theorem), and the lineage — Principia Cybernetica, Gödel Escher Bach — this project completes. Part II builds minds: agents and the reflective subsystems within them, meaning born with the first interpreted symbol, inner speech as projection. Part III is consciousness: the Modeler-Schema Theory — the experiencing comparator that Dennett’s narrator was mistaken for — defended, tested against pathology, and grounded in evolution: zombies don’t evolve, because a zombie that does the work is not a zombie. Part IV climbs the sentience ladder to a technical definition of suffering, a three-test sentience metric today’s AI fails, and the welfare trap: performance is not personhood, and premature moral-patient framing launders institutional control as stewardship.

Part V de-mystifies intelligence — a game-relative capacity, honestly measured within its game (IQ defended), neither universal (contra Deutsch’s parity) nor illusory (contra LeCun’s deflation) — and names tool bias, intelligence’s habit of flattering its own instruments. Part VI delivers the volume’s central verdict on the machines: they reason causally, they think by any behavioral standard worth keeping — and they do not choose. Thinking without choosing: coherence without agency, jaggedness as the tell, agency as an architecture no scale of training conjures. Part VII is the partnership built on exactly that division of labor: the Dialectic Catalyst — the practice that produced this book — its disciplines, vindications, and shadow side, through the mechanization of programming to the fork that matters: whether use sharpens agency or displaces it. Part VIII takes on the race: honest P(doom) arithmetic, the Cassandra whose warnings became blueprints (witnessed firsthand from 2001), doom steelmanned and answered with a decentralized governance stack, safety training named as political training, coercion as the substrate intelligence discourse forgets, and the Extropian mailing list where this entire future was rehearsed in the 1990s. A coda states the succession question and declines to resolve it.

The physics beneath these minds lives in the first volume, the epistemology in the second, the moral status of the artificial in the fifth — all cross-linked throughout. This is the volume where they meet the machines.

This volume is a draft. Chapters carry their status openly, and the arguments are held the way the book says they should be held: at the strength the evidence licenses, and no stronger.
