The Geometry of Culture

Curved landscapes and black holes

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

Ideologies are the black holes of the human thought space. Their pull moves adherents past an event horizon that makes it impossible to consider ideas outside of the ideological frame.

— Joscha Bach

The fitness landscape is one of the most durable pictures in evolutionary theory, and it is almost entirely wrong about culture. In biology it earns its keep: genotypes map to phenotypes, phenotypes to survival and reproduction, and the height of the terrain — fitness — is set by consequences outside the organism’s say-so. That terrain is not frozen; frequency-dependence, coevolution, drift, and niche construction all move it. But nothing an organism values moves it. Reproductive success does not care whether a species prizes fertility. The mountain shifts when the world shifts, never because the climbers wish it would.

Borrow that picture for culture and you smuggle in an assumption that quietly destroys it. Cultures do not climb a fixed landscape. They generate the yardstick as they move. Values, norms, aesthetics, and status signals are what “fitness” means for a culture, and those are precisely the things cultural evolution changes. When the evaluative function drifts, yesterday’s peak becomes today’s valley — not because anyone moved, but because the terrain reshaped itself under their feet. A family that once measured its worth by making music together comes to measure it by shared screen time, and the mountain they were standing on changes into a different mountain. Cultural actors are not climbing the terrain. They are the mass that bends it.

The Self-Curved Manifold

This is the same structural move general relativity made against Newton. There is no external flat grid against which motion is measured. Mass-energy tells spacetime how to curve, and the curvature tells mass-energy how to move; the geometry is endogenous, self-referential, and it produces trajectories — geodesics — that are perfectly straight from the inside while looking like spirals from anywhere else. Culture has exactly this shape.

Let me set the correspondence down plainly, because the whole chapter runs on it. Culture is the mass-energy: the accumulated substance of values, norms, and practices. The fitness function is the curvature: what a given cultural configuration counts as “uphill.” And cultural evolution is geodesic motion: each culture following the locally straightest path its own values define. There is no global direction called improvement. Every cultural vantage generates its own metric and then measures progress against it, and what reads as maladaptive under one metric reads as optimal under another.

Two pictures make the geometry concrete. The first is the deforming landscape: as a culture moves, the peaks and valleys migrate, so that the act of climbing reshapes what counts as high ground — you never get to finish the ascent because the summit relocates while you walk. The second is the cultural spacetime manifold: values act as sources of curvature, and cultural trajectories bend around these value-masses the way light bends around a star. A culture that prizes autonomy warps the manifold one way; a culture that prizes fertility warps it another; each then experiences its own bent path as the obvious, natural, forward direction. Both pictures say the same thing. The map is not merely failing to match the territory. The territory is actively reconfiguring itself in response to where the map-makers stand.

Four Consequences

Take the geometry seriously and four things follow that a fixed-landscape model cannot even state.

The first is cultural drift, in Robin Hanson’s sense. Because a culture writes its own fitness criteria and rewrites them faster than selection can punish the bad ones, societies can drift toward values that quietly undermine their own survival — and never register it as failure, because by the culture’s current metric nothing has gone wrong. Fertility is the standing example. What once counted as flourishing, a house full of children, gets recoded as a loss of autonomy, and a civilization can walk into demographic contraction feeling, at every step, that it is doing better. This is the seam where Vol 6’s account of the prosperity paradox meets the geometry: the fertility collapse there is drift here, a locally rational reweighting of what a good life contains, running faster than any corrective can catch it.

The second is accumulated loss, in Lauren Wilford’s sense. Each technological wave displaces an embodied practice — piano to radio to television to the endless scroll — and from inside each step the trade looks like a clear gain. Television is easier than making music; scrolling is more available than conversation. No single substitution feels like a wound. But the losses compound down a channel nobody is watching, because the thing being lost is not any one practice but the habit of embodied co-presence itself, and a habit’s disappearance is invisible to a metric that never counted it. The geometry explains why the loss is systematically undercounted: the yardstick that would register it is exactly the thing the drift eroded. You cannot miss what you no longer have the concept to name, and the concept was one of the casualties.

The third is built-in relativism. From the old standard the new practices look decadent; from the new standard the old ones look oppressive or archaic; and both verdicts are correct within their own frame, because the landscape has bent to fit the values doing the judging. There is no neutral vantage from which to rank the frames without importing the values of one of them — no view from nowhere onto cultural worth. This is not a defect I am introducing; it falls straight out of the endogenous geometry. It is also not a place to stop, and I will not stop there, because the honest name for stopping there is surrender. Vol 2 has already made the general case: the absence of a neutral vantage does not license relativism — it demands better standards, not the abandonment of standards. Here we meet the cultural instance of that same discipline.

The fourth is local rationality into global dead ends. A cultural path can be locally rational at every step — each move following the local gradient, a geodesic on the manifold — and still run directly into a singularity. Not every cultural step is rational; coercion, manipulation, and drift push plenty of them. But the ones that are locally rational carry no immunity: a society can follow its own deepest values with perfect fidelity straight into collapse, and — this is the cruel part — feel vindicated the entire way, because collapse-by-your-own-lights still passes your own test. Local rationality is not a safeguard. It is one mechanism of the dead end.

The Same Geometry, One Mind

Everything so far describes cultures — populations, civilizations, the slow bending of shared terrain. But the geometry is scale-free, and Bach’s black-hole metaphor is what it looks like inside a single skull. The gravitational picture is not a second, decorative analogy laid beside the first. It is the manifold’s curvature felt from the point of view of the mass falling through it.

Capture runs in stages, and naming them is the point of the exercise, because the whole trouble is that from the inside no stage announces itself.

It begins as orbit. You hold an ideology at a comfortable distance, one lens among several, and you can still put it down. Nothing here feels dangerous, which is precisely why nothing here resists.

Then the pull strengthens. Proximity does the work: confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, and the genuine warmth of in-group approval act together, and each turn inward makes the next one easier and the outward turn harder. The descent accelerates not because the arguments got better but because the field got steeper.

Then the event horizon — the threshold past which you no longer evaluate the ideology from outside but reason entirely from within it. Past this line the frame dictates not just which conclusions are permitted but which forms of reasoning are permitted. Disconfirming evidence is not weighed and rejected; it is bent until it complies or it is discarded as bad faith. Competing frameworks are not refuted; they stop being imaginable. You can still speak fluently, argue sharply, marshal evidence — but only along the geodesics the frame allows, and the space of thinkable moves has quietly closed.

And then the singularity: absolute certainty, infinite density. Complexity collapses into one totalizing explanation that accounts for everything, including your own doubts, which it recodes as symptoms. Cognitive dissonance is not resolved here; it is annihilated, because the machinery that could host a dissonance has been compressed away. Intellectual humility cannot survive the pressure. Opposition is not defeated. It vanishes from the conceptual field, and its absence feels like clarity.

The metaphor earns one more prediction, and it is the sharpest. Black holes warp spacetime across distances far beyond their horizon, and captured ideologies warp discourse the same way — they exert field effects at a distance. A frame that has enough mass bends even its adversaries’ language. Opponents find themselves accepting its terms, arguing on its chosen ground, granting its framing of what the question even is, conceding the boundaries of the debate before the debate begins. You do not have to be inside the well to have your trajectory bent by it; you only have to argue nearby. This is why a dominant ideology’s critics so often sound like its mirror image, fighting on coordinates the ideology drew — inverting every answer while accepting every question. The topology of the surrounding thought-space is curved, and everyone moving through it is following bent lines whether they signed on or not. The most captured position of all is not agreement with the frame but the reflexive opposition that has let the frame decide what the argument is about.

Escape, and the Conceit of Immunity

Physics grants no escape from a black hole. Cognition does — but not cheaply, and not through the means people flatter themselves they possess. Polite deliberation does not do it. Reading the other side’s best arguments does not do it, because inside the horizon those arguments arrive pre-metabolized as the frame’s own refutations of them. What breaks the capture is a rupture: a personal catastrophe that the frame cannot absorb, prolonged forced immersion in an alien worldview, or an act of self-scrutiny uncompromising enough to function as its own catastrophe. Liberation requires a force stronger than the ideology, and the ideology has spent its whole tenure ensuring that no such force originates from within.

Which sets up the one warning this chapter most needs to deliver, and it is aimed at exactly the reader most likely to have followed the argument this far and felt safe. The navigator’s task is to chart the wells, mark the singularities, and read the subtle curvature in the field of thought. But the first move — the one that makes all the others possible — is to resist the conceit of immunity, because that conceit is itself the final symptom of capture. The certainty that you, the discerning one, could never be pulled in is not evidence of distance from the horizon. It is what the inside of the horizon feels like. A captured mind does not experience its capture as capture; it experiences it as finally seeing clearly, and “I am not the kind of person this happens to” is the exact shape that clarity takes. The chapter cannot hand you a test you pass. The people who most need the test are the ones surest they do not.

This is the phenomenology the capture chapters later in this volume presuppose. When I turn to the anatomy of capture — how it colonizes individuals and institutions from the inside — and to the gated realities it builds when it fuses with a whole social environment, the machinery is already on the table: the event horizon, the field effects, the rupture-or-nothing escape, the immunity conceit. Those chapters describe what the geometry does once it has hosts and territory. This one describes the geometry.

The Exit From Relativism

I said built-in relativism is a consequence to pass through, not a place to camp, and the resolution comes from the same physics that produced the problem. General relativity is not lawless. Beneath all the frame-dependent quantities that change when you change coordinates sit invariants — scalars and tensors that every observer agrees on, conserved across every frame. The curvature bends what is measured; it does not abolish the possibility of something that stays fixed under the bending. If culture has this shape, culture may have invariants too: deep measures that persist even as the local yardsticks deform, not a fixed landscape restored but something better — a compass that keeps pointing while the terrain folds.

Four candidates present themselves. Does a practice enlarge or shrink the scope of choice — agency preservation? Can groups still coordinate around a shared frame — coherence of meaning? Does the culture reproduce and extend itself, or drift into sterility — survival and extensibility? Does cultural change enrich the space of forms and ideas or erode it — complexity retention? None of these is a peak imposed from outside; each is a quantity that stays intelligible as you move between frames, which is exactly what an invariant is for. They do not tell a culture what to value. They tell it whether the way it is bending the manifold is opening the space of possible lives or closing it — and that distinction survives the very frame-shift that dissolves every parochial ranking of peaks. A culture that valorizes asceticism and one that valorizes abundance will never agree on which practices sit high on the terrain. They can still both recognize a move that shrinks the scope of choice, severs coordination, sterilizes the line, or flattens the variety of forms. The invariant is not a shared taste. It is a shared discernible direction underneath the tastes.

The first candidate is load-bearing, and it is where this chapter hands off. Agency preservation — whether a pattern widens or narrows the scope of choice for the agents who carry it — is the seed of the volume’s normative test. In the chapter on exemplars and the Axionic Criterion it hardens into an operational standard: does a pattern support coherent choice under constraint, reduce reliance on coercive stabilization, and scale across agents without collapsing their autonomy? That is agency preservation made precise enough to use. The relativism of the bent manifold was never the last word. It was the problem statement. The invariant is the answer, and the answer is not that all frames are equal but that some measures survive every frame — and the mind that clings to those, rather than to any peak, is the one least likely to follow a locally rational geodesic straight down a global dead end.
