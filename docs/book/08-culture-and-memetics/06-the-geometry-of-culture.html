The Geometry of Culture

Curved landscapes and black holes

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

Ideologies are the black holes of the human thought space. Their pull moves adherents past an event horizon that makes it impossible to consider ideas outside of the ideological frame.

— Joscha Bach

The fitness landscape is one of the most durable pictures in evolutionary theory. Even in biology, however, the terrain need not hold still: frequency dependence, environmental change, and niche construction can alter which variants reproduce. Cultural applications add reflective agents, institutions, and contested values, making any single landscape more obviously local to a population, mechanism, environment, and horizon.

Borrowing the picture for culture creates a second danger: conflating evolutionary fitness with what people consider good. Cultural fitness, as used here, means differential propagation or retention under specified conditions. Values, norms, aesthetics, and status signals can help create those conditions, but they are not identical to fitness. When practices change the standards and institutions that later filter practices, the landscape is endogenous. A family that changes how it values music and screen time has changed both its ends and the local filters on behavior.

The Self-Curved Manifold

General relativity supplies a vivid analogy for this feedback: mass-energy affects spacetime geometry, and geometry constrains motion. Culture does not exactly share that mathematical structure. Unless a model specifies variables, a metric, and dynamics, curvature and geodesics remain metaphors for reciprocal shaping between practices and the standards or institutions that filter them.

The correspondence is therefore disciplined rather than literal. Accumulated norms, practices, and institutions play the role of sources; changing incentives and standards play the role of curvature; cultural trajectories play the role of paths through the resulting terrain. The analogy directs attention to feedback and path dependence. It does not imply that a culture is one agent, that every step is rational, or that no cross-cultural comparison is possible.

Two pictures make the feedback concrete. In a deforming landscape, action changes some of the conditions that make later action attractive or sustainable. In a cultural manifold, strong institutions and values bend the feasible paths available to participants. A population that prizes autonomy may build one set of incentives; one that prizes fertility may build another. Members need not agree, and neither picture determines which path is good. Both ask how map-makers alter the territory they later navigate.

Four Consequences

Take the geometry seriously and four things follow that a fixed-landscape model cannot even state.

The first is cultural drift. Practices and preferences can change faster than slow consequences become visible, including consequences for demographic continuity. Fertility is one contested example: economic conditions, technology, institutions, and changing ideals of family and autonomy can jointly produce contraction. Calling this drift describes movement relative to a selected baseline; it does not prove that the former standard was correct or that each individual choice was irrational.

The second is accumulated loss, in Lauren Wilford’s sense. Each technological wave displaces an embodied practice — piano to radio to television to the endless scroll — and from inside each step the trade looks like a clear gain. Television is easier than making music; scrolling is more available than conversation. No single substitution feels like a wound. But the losses compound down a channel nobody is watching, because the thing being lost is not any one practice but the habit of embodied co-presence itself, and a habit’s disappearance is invisible to a metric that never counted it. The geometry explains why the loss is systematically undercounted: the yardstick that would register it is exactly the thing the drift eroded. You cannot miss what you no longer have the concept to name, and the concept was one of the casualties.

The third is perspectival evaluation. From one standard a new practice can look decadent; from another, an old one can look oppressive. The verdicts are conditional on premises, but they can still be tested for factual accuracy, coherence, effects, and consistency. There is no value-free ranking of every cultural end, yet this does not make incompatible empirical claims equally true. As Vol 2 argues, the absence of a neutral vantage does not license relativism; it requires standards and premises to be disclosed.

The fourth is local adaptation into system-level dead ends. Some steps are locally rational for the agents taking them; others result from error, imitation, coercion, or chance. Even individually sensible actions can aggregate into collapse when feedback is delayed or costs are displaced. The geodesic image illustrates path dependence, not a proof that every observed trajectory is optimal by anyone’s lights.

A Related Metaphor, One Mind

Everything so far describes population-level feedback. Bach’s black-hole metaphor applies a related image to individual cognition, but the analogy is not automatically scale-free. Individual belief revision, group coordination, and institutional persistence have different mechanisms. What they may share are reinforcing feedback, rising exit costs, and reduced exposure to correction.

Capture runs in stages, and naming them is the point of the exercise, because the whole trouble is that from the inside no stage announces itself.

It begins as orbit. You hold an ideology at a comfortable distance, one lens among several, and you can still put it down. Nothing here feels dangerous, which is precisely why nothing here resists.

Then the pull strengthens. Proximity does the work: confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, and the genuine warmth of in-group approval act together, and each turn inward makes the next one easier and the outward turn harder. The descent accelerates not because the arguments got better but because the field got steeper.

Then the event horizon — the threshold past which you no longer evaluate the ideology from outside but reason entirely from within it. Past this line the frame dictates not just which conclusions are permitted but which forms of reasoning are permitted. Disconfirming evidence is not weighed and rejected; it is bent until it complies or it is discarded as bad faith. Competing frameworks are not refuted; they stop being imaginable. You can still speak fluently, argue sharply, marshal evidence — but only along the geodesics the frame allows, and the space of thinkable moves has quietly closed.

And then the singularity: absolute certainty, infinite density. Complexity collapses into one totalizing explanation that accounts for everything, including your own doubts, which it recodes as symptoms. Cognitive dissonance is not resolved here; it is annihilated, because the machinery that could host a dissonance has been compressed away. Intellectual humility cannot survive the pressure. Opposition is not defeated. It vanishes from the conceptual field, and its absence feels like clarity.

The metaphor earns one more prediction, and it is the sharpest. Black holes warp spacetime across distances far beyond their horizon, and captured ideologies warp discourse the same way — they exert field effects at a distance. A frame that has enough mass bends even its adversaries’ language. Opponents find themselves accepting its terms, arguing on its chosen ground, granting its framing of what the question even is, conceding the boundaries of the debate before the debate begins. You do not have to be inside the well to have your trajectory bent by it; you only have to argue nearby. This is why a dominant ideology’s critics so often sound like its mirror image, fighting on coordinates the ideology drew — inverting every answer while accepting every question. The topology of the surrounding thought-space is curved, and everyone moving through it is following bent lines whether they signed on or not. The most captured position of all is not agreement with the frame but the reflexive opposition that has let the frame decide what the argument is about.

Escape, and the Conceit of Immunity

Physics grants no escape from a black hole. Cognition can change, although not by one guaranteed route. Disconfirming experience, sustained exposure to alternative models, trusted dissent, changed incentives, or unusually rigorous self-scrutiny may reopen comparison. “Rupture” names the felt discontinuity in some cases; it should not become an excuse for forced immersion or manufactured catastrophe. The practical target is restored error correction.

Which sets up the one warning this chapter most needs to deliver, and it is aimed at exactly the reader most likely to have followed the argument this far and felt safe. The navigator’s task is to chart the wells, mark the singularities, and read the subtle curvature in the field of thought. But the first move — the one that makes all the others possible — is to resist the conceit of immunity, because that conceit is itself the final symptom of capture. The certainty that you, the discerning one, could never be pulled in is not evidence of distance from the horizon. It is what the inside of the horizon feels like. A captured mind does not experience its capture as capture; it experiences it as finally seeing clearly, and “I am not the kind of person this happens to” is the exact shape that clarity takes. The chapter cannot hand you a test you pass. The people who most need the test are the ones surest they do not.

This is the phenomenology the capture chapters later in this volume investigate. When I turn to the anatomy of capture and gated realities, the hypotheses are on the table: rising exit costs, field effects on discourse, weakened correction, and the conceit of immunity. Those chapters must cash the metaphors out in observable mechanisms and consider alternative explanations.

The Exit From Relativism

Perspectival evaluation is not a place to stop. General relativity has coordinate-invariant quantities, but the analogy cannot manufacture cultural invariants. A proposed cross-cultural measure must earn standing through argument, operational definition, and evidence. The candidates below are better treated as disclosed dimensions of evaluation — some descriptive, some normative — than as tensors every observer must recognize.

Four dimensions are worth tracking. Does a practice enlarge or shrink the scope of choice — agency preservation? Can participants still coordinate and interpret one another — coherence of meaning? Does the relevant population or tradition persist under the chosen identity criterion — survival and extensibility? Does cultural change enrich or erode the variety of available forms — complexity retention? These dimensions can conflict. Persistence is descriptive, complexity depends on a measure, coherence can serve domination, and agency preservation is a normative commitment adopted elsewhere in this book. Making them explicit permits comparison; it does not guarantee agreement.

Agency preservation is load-bearing for this book because the book chooses reciprocal agency as its political and ethical commitment. Exemplars and the Axionic Criterion turns that commitment into questions about coherent choice, coercive stabilization, and scale. The curved-landscape metaphor supplies the problem — standards and practices reshape one another. The answer comes not from geometry but from agents disclosing what they are trying to protect, gathering evidence about effects, and keeping judgment open to correction.
