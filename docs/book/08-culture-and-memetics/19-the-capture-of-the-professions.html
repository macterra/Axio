The Capture of the Professions

Psychiatry, the academy, engineering

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

For most of its history, psychiatry claimed to be a science. Its task was to identify, describe, and treat disorders of the mind on the strength of empirical evidence, clinical observation, and diagnostic criteria that meant the same thing on Tuesday as they had on Monday. Words had definitions. Diagnoses had boundaries. The same clinical logic applied whether the patient was a stockbroker or a saint, and whether the belief under examination flattered the prevailing politics or offended it. That is what it meant for psychiatry to be a discipline rather than a faction.

That claim is now under strain, and the way it fails is instructive. It does not fail through a single dramatic betrayal. It fails through a set of small institutional adjustments, each defensible on its own, which together turn a truth-telling profession into an instrument of the tribe that captured it. Psychiatry is worth dwelling on not because it is the worst case — it is not — but because it fails so legibly. The pattern it reveals is transferable. Once you can name the symptoms in one profession, you can find them in any profession whose authority rests on being trusted to tell an inconvenient truth.

The Five Symptoms

Capture announces itself the same way each time. There are five symptoms, and they are diagnostic.

Selective redefinition of terms. Diagnostic language gets rewritten in ways that blur or abandon objective definitions. Categories once anchored to observable reality are replaced with socially malleable ones, and — the tell — the same clinical logic is no longer applied across contexts. Some false beliefs become exempt from being called false, depending on how politically sensitive they are.

Policy preceding evidence. Institutional guidelines change in response to activist pressure or legal trends rather than new findings. Line up the shifts on a timeline and there is no underlying scientific breakthrough to explain them — only the arrival of a new political imperative. The guideline moves; the evidence does not.

Asymmetric application of principles. In an ordinary case, a belief that directly contradicts physical reality is treated as a symptom to be evaluated. In a politically protected domain, those same evaluative tools are suspended, softened, or reframed. The asymmetry is the abandonment of value-neutral science, visible in the seam.

A chilling effect on inquiry. Clinicians who question the new orthodoxy risk reputational damage, sanction, or their jobs. Even mild dissent is recast as a moral failing rather than a scientific disagreement, which silences exactly the debate a self-correcting field depends on.

Institutional gatekeeping. Professional bodies fold activist representatives into the drafting of standards, granting political actors direct influence over clinical definitions — and those same bodies begin issuing policy positions on matters that have nothing to do with core competence.

None of the five requires bad faith from any individual. Every clinician can remain sincere while the institution that speaks in their name drifts. That is what makes the diagnostic worth having: it reads the institution, not the person. When psychiatry subordinates its standards to ideological loyalty, it trades the credibility of a science for a season of political peace. Diagnostic categories lose reliability. Patients receive inconsistent care depending on the political status of their condition. Public trust erodes as psychiatry comes to look like another branch of politics — which, by then, it is. The danger was never that psychiatry acquired a moral compass. The danger is that the needle now swings to whatever direction the prevailing winds blow, while the profession goes on insisting it points at true north. A field captured by ideology can no longer tell the truth when the truth is inconvenient. And if psychiatry will not tell the truth about the mind, who will?

There is a longer lineage here than a decade of institutional drift. Psychiatry is the institutional descendant of the Freud meme complex — a profession built, in no small part, on a self-sealing explanatory scheme that taught a century of practitioners to treat resistance as confirmation and doubt as symptom. I trace that inheritance in Echoes of Freud. A discipline that learned early to make its own unfalsifiability a feature was always going to be an easy host for the next totalizing narrative that came along. The symptoms of capture are, in part, the old immune weakness expressing itself in a new pathogen.

Neutrality Was the Contract

Run the diagnostic across the professions and the academy lights up on every symptom at once. But the academy deserves its own treatment, because it makes the deepest version of the mistake and pays the most complete version of the price.

Consider a mathematician — call him the Mozart of his field, one of the most celebrated living minds in the most abstract of disciplines. In one year he lends his name, alongside hundreds of colleagues, to an open letter declaring that his country is a “white supremacist society” and that complicity with its systems of oppression runs to the nation’s origins. The message is explicit and was meant to be: neutrality is complicity, and scholars must take a side. A few years later the same mathematician is back in the headlines, this time as a victim: a hostile administration has slashed his research funding, and he laments that this is not a routine policy shift but a deliberate dismantling of the institutions and freedoms that have sustained science for generations. Politics, he protests, has invaded the domain of science and sabotaged its neutrality.

The irony is painful, and it was structural rather than accidental. For centuries the academy survived and thrived precisely because it was trusted to stand outside the tribal wars. Mathematics, physics, and biology carried weight because they operated above the fray; their authority rested on independence, on the ability to produce knowledge unsullied by partisan loyalty. That was the social contract, and it was the only protection the academy ever had. Universities command no armies and control no budgets of their own. Their entire defensive position was the public’s belief that what came out of them was true regardless of who found it convenient.

When scholars declared that every field, even the most abstract, must announce its allegiance to a political vision — that silence was no longer permitted and neutrality was itself a form of collusion — they tore up the contract in their own hands. If a mathematician tells you that his discipline is complicit in oppression, he has told you that his discipline is political. And if it is political, why should a politician not take him at his word and treat it as a faction to be rewarded or punished like any other? By insisting that neutrality is complicity, the academy destroyed the neutrality that protected it. The moment scholars abandoned impartiality they forfeited the only claim to universal legitimacy they had, and the funding retaliation that followed was not an aberration but the natural consequence of the logic they had legitimized.

This is the general law that psychiatry only hints at: capture invites counter-capture. The academics imagined they could wield politics as a one-way weapon — mobilizing moral fervor to shield their institutions from scrutiny while never suffering scrutiny in return. But power never flows in one direction for long. The sword forged to enforce an orthodoxy is available to whoever seizes it next, and the next hand is rarely friendly. A profession that politicizes itself does not thereby win the political game; it merely volunteers to play a game it was previously exempt from, against opponents who are better at it. Science can survive lack of funding, social indifference, even persecution. What it cannot survive is the loss of the belief that it is neutral, because that belief was the whole of its armor. The tower thought it was seizing the moral high ground. It was sawing off the branch it sat on. The dynamics of who wins once a domain has been politicized — and why “our side should capture it first” is a trap — I take up in The Politics of Safety.

The Category Error in Engineering

The third case is the clarifying one, because engineering is the discipline where the stakes are most physical and the confusion is most precise. Here the capture arrives under the banner of decolonization, and it rests on a single mistake that is worth stating exactly.

The mistake is to treat engineering as a culturally situated worldview when it is in fact a universal method. Engineering is not a narrative or a tradition that happens to carry Western assumptions. It is a method for generating reliable predictions about the behavior of physical systems, and its authority comes from one criterion only: a model is good if it predicts and bad if it fails. That epistemic backbone — quantification of variables, systematic modeling of constraints, optimization under competing objectives, iterative testing, falsifiability — is what lets engineering scale across cultures, languages, histories, and political systems. It is what makes bridges stand, reactors run, circuits compute, aircraft stay up, and water plants deliver clean water. Newton’s laws, Kirchhoff’s laws, the Navier–Stokes equations, and the laws of thermodynamics do not consult the worldview of the engineer applying them. To call this method a “Western way of knowing” is to confuse its historical origin with its epistemic structure. The method is universal not because the West imposed it but because reality enforces it.

The confusion has a name, and it is worth carrying out of this chapter: it is the confusion of verification with meaning. A “decolonizing and indigenizing” engineering curriculum places engineering and Indigenous knowledge traditions on the same epistemic plane, as parallel ways of knowing that deserve equal space in the classroom. The posture aims at respect and commits a category error. Engineering evaluates a claim by verifying whether it corresponds to outcomes in the physical world. A cultural tradition evaluates a claim by its meaning within a historical and relational context. These are not interchangeable standards. Meaning explains significance; verification explains behavior. Both matter, and they answer different questions. A curriculum that collapses the two deprives engineering of its essential evaluative framework — the capacity to tell a true prediction from a false one.

The concrete case that surfaced this was a real course, flagged by the engineer Paul Raymond-Robichaud and documented in a published course paper for a Canadian engineering faculty:

A required course in an accredited engineering program: “Decolonizing and Indigenizing Engineering.” The assessment is reflective journaling, participation in discussions and ceremonies, and a summative reflective project. No modeling. No design exercise. No analysis of a physical system. No testing of a hypothesis. This is an engineering course.

The assessment model is the whole argument. Reflection is genuinely valuable in ethics and in reckoning with the human impact of engineering; it is not a substitute for the cognitive architecture required to produce a design that does not fail. Replacing modeling, analysis, and testing with introspection and narrative processing is not integration. It is skill displacement — swapping the competencies that define the practice for the ones that flatter a worldview. An engineer who reflects well but designs poorly is not an engineer. The bridge does not care how thoughtfully its designer examined his assumptions about power and knowledge; it cares whether the moment calculation was done right.

This is where the Axio framework makes the cost precise. Agency, in this framework, is the ability to construct, evaluate, and choose among the models that shape one’s dealings with reality. Engineering is among the most agency-amplifying domains humans have ever built, because it hands individuals and communities the power to predict and reshape physical systems. But agency depends on epistemic clarity — the ability to test an assumption, the freedom to reject an unfounded story, the discipline to privilege a model that works over a model that merely feels meaningful. When a curriculum prescribes an interpretive frame rather than teaching students how to evaluate and choose among frames, it does not expand agency; it constrains it. Substituting worldview relativism for engineered models is not empowerment. It is epistemic enclosure — the fencing-off of the very capacities that would let a student think past the frame he was handed. Capture, seen from inside the framework, is always an enclosure of agency dressed as a liberation of it.

And the physical stakes give the whole diagnostic its edge. When truth becomes negotiable, safety becomes negotiable. A captured psychiatry produces inconsistent care; a captured academy produces distrust; but a captured engineering produces failures with body counts, because the discipline’s entire value is its indifference to story. It cares only whether a model corresponds to reality. Take that away and you have not decolonized engineering. You have disarmed it.

Respect Without Relativism

None of this is an argument against the history, the ethics, or the community. The critique dismisses nothing about Indigenous history, treaty obligations, environmental stewardship, or culturally informed practice — these are real components of responsible engineering, and an engineer who does not understand the context his design will operate in is a worse engineer. The point is that respect for cultural knowledge does not require flattening epistemic distinctions, treating empirical success as culturally contingent, or importing rituals into professional training.

There is a version of integration that is coherent, and it is worth stating so that the critique is not merely negative. Teach the history of engineering projects and their impact on Indigenous communities: it illuminates real harms and prepares students to practice more responsibly. Analyze cases where disregarding local knowledge caused environmental or social damage: it sharpens ethical judgment without diluting technical standards. Incorporate ecological insights that carry genuine predictive value — traditional knowledge of watersheds, fire regimes, and species behavior often does — into the modeling itself, where it strengthens the engineering rather than replacing it. Build thoughtful consultation and consent into projects, so that engineering respects community autonomy while keeping its methodological spine intact. This is the shape of respect without relativism: learn from another culture without substituting its epistemic foundation for the one that makes the work reliable. Respect the culture, learn the history, engage the community — and keep engineering anchored to truth.

The Vice and the Obsolescence

There is a final turn, and it belongs to economics as much as to memetics. While the professions have been captured from within, they are being made obsolete from without. The academy’s credential and the licensed profession’s seal were always, at bottom, proof-of-work tokens — expensive, hard-to-fake signals that their holder had done the labor and could be trusted to tell the truth in a domain the public could not check directly. That signal is collapsing on two fronts at once. Ideological capture destroys its content, so that the credential no longer certifies neutral competence but membership in a faction. And cheap, capable machine intelligence destroys its scarcity, so that the underlying labor the credential attested to can be had for the price of a query. I develop that second dynamic in Ornament and Advantage; here it is enough to note that the two failures compound. A profession that has spent its neutrality to win a culture war finds, when it turns to defend itself, that the market has stopped believing its signal for entirely separate reasons. Capture-from-within meets obsolescence-from-without, and the profession is squeezed between them.

The three cases are one mechanism. A profession earns its authority by being trusted to tell an inconvenient truth; capture is the process by which a tribe converts that trust into a weapon and, in doing so, spends it. The five symptoms are the instrument for detecting it early — before the redefinitions harden, before the chilling effect empties the room of dissenters, before the gatekeepers finish drafting the new standards. They are a special case of the general anatomy I lay out in Anatomy of Capture, and they feed the larger claim of The Forbidden Pattern: that the institutions we rely on to see clearly can develop, collectively, a blind spot precisely where they most need sight. Psychiatry’s question generalizes to every captured profession. If the people we trust to tell the truth will only tell us the convenient half of it, then the truth has nowhere left to live — and we will have arranged its exile ourselves, one defensible institutional adjustment at a time.
