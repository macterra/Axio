Parity Where It Pays

The equality paradox, symmetrical

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

The countries that have done the most to unbind women from institutional constraint produce, per capita, some of the fewest female engineers and physicists on earth. This is the gender equality paradox, and it is almost always introduced as a scandal — a puzzle to be explained away, usually with the implication that Norway and Finland must be harboring some subtler, invisible barrier that the crude ones only masked. The paradox runs the other way from intuition. Where women enjoy the greatest freedom and face the fewest formal obstacles, their share of STEM fields is lower, not higher, than in poorer and more constrained societies. The cross-cultural data are consistent enough that the effect has a name and a shape: when survival pressure relaxes, people drift toward their intrinsic interests rather than away from them.

That much is now familiar, at least among people willing to look at it. What is almost never said aloud is that the paradox has a mirror image, and the mirror is where the argument actually lives.

The Shape of the Divergence

Three explanations are usually offered for the STEM gap, and all three survive scrutiny. The first is biological: on average — and every claim here is an average over distributions that overlap heavily — men and women differ in a handful of dispositions relevant to occupational sorting, including risk tolerance. The second is interest: measured vocational preference divides along a people-versus-things axis more sharply than almost any other psychological variable we have, with women’s interests weighted toward the people end and men’s toward the things end. The third is the freedom effect itself: as the cost of following one’s preferences falls, expressed preference tracks intrinsic preference more closely, and the average divergence widens. Affluence and liberty do not homogenize choices. They amplify the differences that scarcity had been suppressing.

There is a fourth fact that the STEM framing tends to bury, because it is inconvenient for everyone. Men are not simply overrepresented at the top; they are overrepresented at both extremes. Male variance is greater. There are more men than women among the eminent and more men than women among the ruined — more male Fields medalists and more male vagrants, more men in the physics faculty and more men under the overpass. Women cluster more heavily toward the middle of many distributions. When you demand parity at the prestigious tail, you are quietly assuming that the tail is the only place the sorting happens. It is not.

Whether “gender” is the right word for any of this — whether the category is discovered or constituted — is a separate quarrel, and I have kept it separate; the two ontologies of gender explain why that quarrel never resolves. This chapter is about outcomes, not categories. The outcomes are measurable, and they are lopsided in a way that the standard account cannot metabolize.

We Do Not March for the Morgue

Consider the occupations with the highest fatality rates: logging, commercial fishing, roofing, structural steel, deep mining, oil extraction, waste collection, long-haul trucking, and frontline combat. Across every culture and political system that keeps the records, these jobs are overwhelmingly male. In the United States, men account for well over ninety percent of workplace deaths. Year after year, the people who fall off the roof, drown on the boat, or are crushed in the shaft are almost all men.

Now apply the three explanations. Why is dangerous work male? Because, on average, men are more risk-tolerant and physically better matched to the loads (biology); because men are more drawn to high-hazard, thing-oriented labor and women more inclined to avoid it (interest); and because, as freedom of choice expands, women exit these occupations with even greater consistency than they exit engineering (the freedom effect). It is the identical apparatus. Not a loose analogy — the same three mechanisms, running in the same direction, producing a disparity far larger than the one in STEM. If the reasoning is sound for the boardroom and the physics department, it is sound for the trawler and the scaffold. If it is bias-laundering for the morgue, it was bias-laundering for the boardroom.

And here is the asymmetry that gives the chapter its title. Society demands gender balance in prestigious fields and shows no measurable concern for balance in the fields that maim and kill. There are campaigns, mandates, pipelines, and prizes for women in engineering, in the C-suite, in venture capital, in tenured science. There is no campaign for women on oil rigs. No one has proposed a diversity target for logging crews. No foundation funds the underrepresentation of women in roofing deaths. We do not march for equality in the morgue.

Take that asymmetry seriously for a moment, because it is doing more work than any statistic in the chapter. If the operative concern were equality as such — parity in the distribution of outcomes between the sexes — then the enormous male surplus in occupational death would be among the gravest inequities on the board, and the silence about it would be inexplicable. The silence is not inexplicable. It is perfectly explicable the moment you notice that parity is demanded exactly where the seats are comfortable, safe, and high-status, and demanded nowhere else. The revealed concern was never equality of outcome. It was access to prestige — a fight over who gets the good chairs, dressed in the language of a fight over all chairs. The equality frame is the costume; the distribution of the demand is the body underneath.

This move — comparing the case people are loud about with the structurally identical case they are silent about, and reading the difference — is not a one-off rhetorical flourish. It is an instrument, and it is the instrument of the taboo test too: the asymmetry between what a norm claims to be about and where it actually bites is often the fastest route to what the norm is really for. Wherever a principle is invoked selectively, the selection pattern, not the principle, tells you the motive.

The Supply Chain Nobody Counts

The morgue is the sharp end. The blunt end is everywhere, and it is worth counting because it dissolves the fantasy that the imbalance is confined to a few visibly grim jobs.

Trace any sophisticated artifact — an iPhone, a Starship, an undersea fiber-optic cable — back through the full chain of labor-hours that produced it, and a pattern surfaces at every link. Resource extraction — the mining and refining of lithium, cobalt, copper, rare earths — routinely runs over ninety percent male. Heavy manufacturing, precision machining, metallurgy, and aerospace assembly commonly exceed ninety percent. Logistics and infrastructure — trucking, shipping, freight, port handling, heavy construction — land somewhere in the eighty-to-ninety-five percent range. Installation and maintenance of the physical substrate, from launch pads to data centers to telecom towers, sits well above ninety. Even the engineering and design layer, the most gender-integrated stage, remains roughly three-quarters to four-fifths male at the large firms.

There are real exceptions, and honesty requires naming them. Electronics assembly, especially in Asian factories, draws substantial female participation — often the majority. Testing, quality assurance, customer-facing sales, and support skew balanced or female. But these are a modest fraction of total labor-hours across the global chain. A reasonable aggregate estimate is that roughly eighty to ninety percent of the labor embodied in a complex technological object was performed by men; for the heaviest-industry products, rockets and deep-sea infrastructure, it approaches or exceeds ninety-five.

The point of the accounting is not to award men a medal for building the world. It is that the same explanation runs symmetrically in the other direction and must be accepted there too, on pain of contradiction. Nursing, primary education, veterinary care, psychology, social work, and childcare are predominantly female for the same reason mining is predominantly male: because women choose these fields at markedly higher rates when they are free to choose. If male dominance in extraction is voluntary sorting rather than exclusion, then female dominance in nursing is voluntary sorting rather than exclusion — and the campaign to fix the second while celebrating the first is the prestige asymmetry again, running along a different axis. Voluntary sorting cannot be systemic injustice in the fields women avoid and free choice in the fields women prefer. It is one thing or the other, and it is free choice.

Friction Is Not Injustice

None of this denies that the minority-sex entrant into any field has a harder path than the majority-sex entrant. She does, and the friction is real. A woman entering a male-dominated trade — or a man entering nursing — meets social isolation, fewer colleagues who share his or her background interests, implicit stereotypes, occupational signaling that started shaping expectations in childhood, thinner access to mentors and networks that formed around the majority, and workplace norms quietly calibrated to the majority sex. These are not imaginary. They raise the cost of the crossing, and they deserve attention.

But friction is not injustice, and the distinction is the whole argument. Injustice is someone coercively barred from a choice they would otherwise make: the qualified applicant refused for her sex, the apprentice blocked at the gate. Friction is the ordinary cost of being in the minority anywhere — a cost that voluntary effort can lower and that no amount of coercion can honestly erase, because much of what produces it is simply the majority preference it sits inside. The right responses to friction are the ones that lower the cost without commandeering the outcome: surfacing role models and success stories, holding competence assessment and promotion genuinely unbiased, building mentorship cultures that do not exclude the newcomer. What these have in common is that they widen the door without dragging anyone through it. They are remedies aimed at the barrier, not at the tally.

This is also the reason the argument does not collapse into a denial that discrimination ever happens. It happens. Some claims of it are true, and a serious account has to be able to say which — the when the claim is true case is the deliberate counterweight to this one, an adjudicated instance where the barrier was real and the injustice genuine. The two chapters are a pair: this one dismantles the reflex that reads every disparity as a wrong, and the next refuses the opposite reflex that would wave every specific complaint away. A disparity is a question, not a verdict. Sometimes the answer is discrimination. Usually, at the level of whole occupations, it is sorting.

The Dilemma That Does Not Go Away

Strip away the euphemisms and a single fork remains. If you value equality of opportunity — freedom from coercive obstruction, an open door — then you must accept inequality of outcome, because free people with divergent average preferences will not distribute themselves evenly, and the freer they are, the less evenly they will distribute. If instead you insist on equality of outcome — a matched tally in the fields that matter to you — then you must accept coercion, because nothing short of force will hold free choices to a distribution they do not spontaneously produce.

There is no third door, and the attempt to build one is where most of the confusion lives. “Equality of opportunity,” pushed hard enough to guarantee equal outcomes, quietly ceases to mean an open door and starts to mean an engineered starting line — which requires coercively redistributing the results of every prior round, and so is not equality of opportunity at all but equality of outcome wearing its clothes. That incoherence is worked out in full under the heading of agency, not equality; it is enough here to notice that the middle position dissolves on contact. And when the demand narrows further to the wage gap — the summary statistic in which all of this is supposed to crystallize as proof of injustice — the biology of parenthood and the choices that follow from it account for the aggregate far better than discrimination does, which is the argument of the loaded dice of parenthood.

Coercion, moreover, is never free. Every quota, every forced equity target, every seat filled by mandate rather than choice extracts a price — in efficiency, in resentment, in the corrosion of the presumption that the person in the chair earned it — and the price falls heaviest on the very people the mandate was meant to lift, who now carry the suspicion that they are quota fill. The choice between accepting unequal outcomes and accepting coercion is a real choice with real costs on both sides, and it should be made with open eyes rather than smuggled past under a slogan.

I would make it the same way each time. Remove the unjust barrier wherever it is found; lower the friction by voluntary means wherever it is worth lowering; and then let free people sort themselves, including into the fields that will kill them. The alternative is to march for the morgue — to insist that the distribution of death, too, must be balanced by decree — and no one, tellingly, is willing to say that out loud. That unwillingness is the whole case. We already know that equal outcomes are not what anyone wants when the outcome is a coffin. We only pretend otherwise when the outcome is a corner office.
