The Wound and the Weapon

Victimhood as currency

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

The meeting is about a budget. Somewhere in the second hour, a participant prefaces an objection with an account of her trauma — a real injury, sincerely described, and entirely unrelated to the line items on the table. Watch what happens to the room. The conversation does not continue with one more datum added; it reorganizes. Disagreement with the proposal now risks reading as disregard for the wound. The speaker has moved from participant to witness, a role that confers a kind of procedural immunity, and everyone present feels the shift even if no one can name it. Nothing false has been said. And yet the terrain of the discussion has been tilted, deliberately or not, by the introduction of suffering into a context where suffering was not the subject.

There is a standing claim about moments like this — blunt, cynical, and popular: people who introduce trauma into irrelevant contexts are not over-sharing; they are seeking power. This chapter takes that claim seriously, because it contains a sharp insight, and then refuses it as a universal law, because it collapses a genuine ambiguity into a caricature. The previous chapters of this part examined weapons forged from words — labels that reclassify critics, vocabularies that authenticate insiders. This chapter examines a weapon forged from injury. Victimhood functions in contemporary culture as a currency: it can be earned, displayed, spent, counterfeited, and — this is the part that makes the analysis delicate — it is backed by something real. The same currency circulates at three magnifications. At the psychological register, a single teller and a single story. At the sociological register, the fate of entire groups across generations. At the gendered register, a culture deciding, selectively, who counts as an adult. One dynamic, three scales, and the coin must be examined at each.

The Double Register

Start with what trauma actually is, because the power analysis only makes sense against the healing analysis. Trauma is not simply a wound of the body or brain; it is a fracture in the story one tells about oneself. A coherent psyche depends on continuity — past, present, and future stitched into a legible whole — and trauma rips a hole in that fabric. It leaves a gap in the narrative where meaning should reside, and into that gap seeps confusion, shame, and silence. The therapeutic impulse, whether in psychoanalysis, narrative therapy, or ordinary friendship, is to help the injured reclaim authorship. To speak the wound aloud is to begin repairing the story. So far, this is humane and true, and nothing in what follows retracts it.

But once trauma is recognized as a disruption in narrative, a darker corollary follows: narratives are not merely personal. They are social, political, and cultural. They frame who is heard and who is silenced, who is the victim and who is the oppressor, who deserves sympathy and who deserves censure. To invoke trauma is therefore never a neutral act. It shifts the axis of power in a conversation, redirects the spotlight, reorders the terms of debate, and places the speaker in the privileged role of witness. This is the sharp insight inside the cynical claim, and it deserves its full force: by dragging a wound into a space where it does not belong, a speaker tilts the narrative terrain in her favor. The move is not only therapeutic; it is rhetorical. Victimhood becomes a lever.

Yet here the hot take falters, and the correction matters as much as the insight. Not every disclosure of trauma is a calculated grab for dominance. To say so is to collapse human motives into a caricature of manipulation. Sometimes trauma bursts forth clumsily because the sufferer has lost all sense of proportion — that is what trauma does. Sometimes the disclosure is desperation for connection. Sometimes it is a maladaptive attempt at self-explanation, the fractured story leaking into whatever container is nearest. Reducing all trauma-talk to narrative power games is as crude as dismissing all illness-talk as attention-seeking. It mistakes a real dynamic for a universal law.

The truth is that trauma occupies a double register, and the registers cannot be separated. On one, the injured seek coherence: they tell their story because they must, because silence corrodes the soul. On the other, stories shape worlds, and trauma-talk inevitably carries political weight whether or not the teller intends it. To ignore either register is to misunderstand both. The lesson is not to sneer at trauma as power-play, nor to sanctify it as pure authenticity, but to recognize the dangerous ambiguity and meet it with discernment: is this story being told to stitch a soul, or to seize the stage? Often it is both. That is the sobering recognition at the base of everything in this chapter — in the politics of meaning, wounds are weapons, and to understand trauma is to see not only the broken story but the battlefield on which it is retold.

When Weakness Pays

An ambiguity in individual psychology becomes a currency only when a culture starts honoring the note, and ours has. Sociologists Bradley Campbell and Jason Manning gave the phenomenon its name — victimhood culture — a moral order in which status is increasingly gained through perceived vulnerability rather than through honor’s touchiness or dignity’s restraint. Once status flows toward the wound, the double register stops being a private ambiguity and becomes a public incentive: the power reading of trauma-talk, which at the individual level is only sometimes true, becomes structurally rewarded at the cultural level.

Nietzsche saw the shape of this order long before it had a sociological name. “Strange though it may sound,” he wrote in The Will to Power (§685), “one always has to defend the strong against the weak; the fortunate against the unfortunate; the healthy against those degenerating and afflicted with hereditary taints.” Taken at face value the sentence is shocking, and taken at face value it deserves to be. Read as a diagnosis of moral incentive structures, it is a warning worth keeping: a culture that pays for weakness will stigmatize strength, and a morality organized around ressentiment — his word for the resentment of the strong dressed up as a moral system — will aim less at uplifting the disadvantaged than at diminishing whoever stands above them. The warning is not a license for callousness, and I do not take it as one; the ethics of need and claim, and the affirmative alternative to resentment-built value systems, are Volume 5’s territory, and the psychology of the disparity-to-injury inference has its own chapter. The point to carry forward here is narrower and structural: when a moral economy equates suffering with virtue and vulnerability with authority, it has created a market — and markets get supplied. The fuller Nietzschean case is on the record; what this chapter needs from it is the single observation that the currency, once honored, mints itself.

The Sowell Test

Scale up from the individual teller to the group, and the currency question becomes empirical: does victimhood, held as a group’s organizing narrative, actually purchase what it promises? There is a widespread account on which discrimination and oppression essentially dictate the success or failure of minority groups — on which a group’s outcomes are the arithmetic shadow of its mistreatment. History is unkind to this account, and Thomas Sowell spent a career documenting the unkindness.

Consider Jewish communities over the past century and a half. They endured about the worst that organized hostility can inflict: genocide, pogroms, explicit legal exclusion, forced displacement, property confiscation, and a global prejudice durable enough to earn its own chapter in this volume. If severity of oppression dictated outcomes, these communities should have been destroyed. Instead, in country after country, they rebuilt, adapted, and excelled — economically, academically, culturally. Nor is the case unique: Armenians after the genocide, overseas Chinese minorities across Southeast Asia — repeatedly expropriated, excluded, and periodically massacred — show the same pattern. Groups facing severe, sustained discrimination have nonetheless prospered, and the trait they share is not mild treatment. It is a particular internal culture: educational rigor, entrepreneurship, strong family cohesion, internal support networks, and a long-horizon orientation toward investing in human capital. Cultural capital, in a phrase.

Let me state the conclusion carefully, because it is routinely strawmanned. The claim is not that discrimination is unreal, mild, or harmless. Discrimination unquestionably exists and causes genuine harm, and nothing here trivializes historical injustice — this volume contains a chapter devoted to adjudicating a discrimination claim as true. The claim is comparative: the decisive factor shaping a group’s long-term outcomes is not the severity of external discrimination but the internal cultural response to that adversity. Severity does not predict trajectory; response does. And this should not surprise anyone who has absorbed the economic baseline that poverty is the default condition of mankind and wealth the anomaly requiring explanation: the question about any group is never what pushed it down — entropy and hostility push everything down — but what internal machinery lifted it.

Now run the comparison forward. What does the victimhood narrative — the story that one’s life outcomes are overwhelmingly determined by external, uncontrollable forces — do to the machinery that lifts? It dismantles it. The narrative undermines exactly the psychological resource on which advancement runs: the belief that one’s choices and actions influence one’s outcomes. Preach helplessness with enough institutional authority and you produce it — learned, rehearsed, and eventually inherited.

Worse, the narrative builds a perverse incentive structure and then locks it in. Victimhood culture links its rewards — social recognition, moral authority, resource allocation — to perceived oppression rather than to achievement, self-reliance, or competence. Think about what that gradient selects for. Behaviors essential to socioeconomic advancement — delayed gratification, personal initiative, resilience under setback — earn nothing in this economy; displays of grievance earn status, sympathy, and budget. No conspiracy is required, and none should be inferred: patterns propagate when they match their incentive environment, and a narrative that converts suffering into standing is a high-fitness pattern wherever suffering is compensated. The result is a moral economy that perpetuates and deepens the very inequalities it decries — a machine that runs on the wound and therefore cannot afford to heal it.

The alternative is not denial of structural disparity; it is a different repair strategy. Where genuine disparities exist, address them through universal policies that empower broadly — improving educational access, investing in poor neighborhoods, stripping away the regulatory barriers that price the poor out of entry — policies that benefit disadvantaged groups disproportionately precisely because those groups are disproportionately poor, without resorting to racial categories at all. Structural universalism repairs without paying the perverse premium: it rewards the behaviors that build cultural capital instead of the narratives that liquidate it, and it does so without carving the polity into creditor and debtor races. The group-level lesson is the individual-level lesson at scale. The wound is real. Organizing your story around it is a choice, and the choice has compounding consequences.

The Infantilization Reflex

The third register is the strangest, because here the currency is not claimed but conferred — victimhood assigned to people who never asked for it, over their explicit objection, by third parties who profit from the assignment. Its cleanest specimen is a gendered asymmetry in contemporary moral discourse.

Adult women in their twenties are treated as politically sovereign, economically capable, and sexually autonomous. We trust them with every high-stakes domain of modern life: voting, binding contracts, medical sovereignty, six-figure debt, parenthood, career decisions with fifty-year consequences. Until, that is, they choose romantic partners who violate egalitarian expectations — older men, higher-status men. Then, remarkably, the same culture reframes them as fragile ingénues misled by cunning operators. A twenty-three-year-old woman’s consent, fully honored at the bank, the ballot box, and the surgical consult, is suddenly treated as compromised — not by force or fraud, but by narrative disapproval. Call this the infantilization reflex: a cultural maneuver that grants women full agency when their choices align with prevailing norms and withdraws it when their choices conflict with ideological commitments or status interests.

The inconsistency is not subtle. Either agency is real and we honor it, or it is provisional and we withdraw it whenever it produces aesthetically discomforting outcomes. A culture that does the second while proclaiming the first is not protecting anyone; it is running a protection racket on its own principles. And the tell is the retrofitted moral language. Sexual selection generates predictable patterns — women in their twenties hold peak mate-choice leverage, men in midlife hold peak resource and status leverage — and the pairings at that intersection reliably trigger envy and ideological anxiety. Culture reacts by reaching for vocabulary that moralizes the discomfort after the fact: “power imbalance,” “predatory dynamics,” “grooming-adjacent.” Notice the engineering in that last term — the deliberate adjacency to a genuine crime, borrowing the moral charge of child abuse for application to adults, without ever having to allege anything criminal. This is the weaponized-label mechanism of two chapters ago applied to relationships: the vocabulary does not describe a harm; it manufactures a victim.

Why does the reflex persist despite its incoherence? Because incoherence is cheap and the incentives behind it are real. Three are worth naming. Intrasexual competition: older women have an obvious interest in reframing younger rivals as vulnerable rather than competitive. Status preservation: lower-status men benefit from narratives that discourage women from pursuing higher-status partners. And ideological consistency maintenance: egalitarian moral theory struggles with the durable reality of hypergamy — with the fact that women’s revealed preferences are autonomous and often non-egalitarian — and it is easier to discredit the preference than to revise the ideal. These forces converge on a single maneuver: proclaim the chooser incompetent rather than admit that her desires operate independently of the ideology. The reflex is envy and anxiety wearing the language of ethical concern, and it functions as a political technology — for if twenty-something women cannot adequately assess their own partners, then institutions and activists acquire license to regulate relationships, constrain mate choice, and expand the jurisdiction of moral intervention. The protective language is the bailey’s drawbridge; the acquired authority is the point.

The Axio framework leaves no room for this maneuver, and it is worth being precise about why. Agency, as this book defines it, is a physical capacity: the capacity of an embedded agent to model its possible futures, generate coherent preferences over them, and act to realize them. It is structural and embodied. It does not flicker off because an observer disapproves of the outcome, any more than a bridge loses its load capacity when an engineer dislikes what is being carried across it. Treating agency as context-dependent and revocable-by-disapproval is therefore not a moral subtlety; it is an epistemic error — the moral counterpart of a type error. If a twenty-three-year-old woman is an agent in every other domain of high-stakes decision-making, she is an agent in mate choice. If she is not, then she is not an adult in any meaningful sense, and society must retract far more than dating autonomy — the contracts, the ballots, the medical consents, all of it rests on the same capacity. Consistency forces the issue: either we accept adult agency in full, or we are engaged in paternalism wrapped in moral rhetoric.

To reject the infantilization reflex is not to endorse every relationship outcome, celebrate every pairing, or deny that people make choices that harm them. Agents err; that is what having futures to choose among means. It is to affirm the deeper principle that adults are responsible for their choices, and that we distort both truth and dignity when we pretend otherwise. A culture that resolves its cognitive dissonance by denying the agenthood of the chooser has not protected her. It has spent her adulthood to balance its own books.

One Currency, Three Frauds

Lay the three registers side by side and the unity is visible. At each scale, something genuinely valuable — the healing of a broken story, the redress of real discrimination, the protection of the genuinely vulnerable — backs the currency and makes it honorable to hold. And at each scale, the same debasement threatens: the wound converted into a lever, suffering converted into status, vulnerability converted into jurisdiction. The psychological register shows the ambiguity in its native state, where healing and power are fused in a single act of telling and even the teller may not know the mixture. The sociological register shows what happens when a culture pays out on the ambiguity for two generations: perverse incentives, learned helplessness, and group trajectories bent downward by the very narratives marketed as advocacy. The gendered register shows the currency’s final mutation, victimhood imposed from outside — minted about people rather than by them — because third parties found the denomination useful.

The discernment this demands is genuinely difficult, and the difficulty is the point. Automatic indulgence fails, because it funds the counterfeiters and, worse, debases the currency for the genuinely injured — every fraudulent note spent makes the real ones harder to honor. Automatic suspicion fails, because the wounds are real, the discrimination was real, the vulnerable exist, and a culture that sneers at every trauma disclosure has amputated something it needs. What remains is the unglamorous work of asking, case by case: stitching a soul, or seizing the stage? Repairing a disparity, or capitalizing a grievance? Protecting an agent, or annexing her agency? Often the honest answer is both — and then the task is to honor the first without funding the second.

There is one more turn of the screw. Everything in this chapter describes the exploitation of sympathy — the conversion of our best responses to suffering into leverage against us. That exploit has a general form, and it is aimed not just at conversations and cultures but at the recruitment pipelines of whole ideologies: the discovery that moral trust is the easiest resource to metabolize into power, and that the kindest people make the best conscripts. That machinery — the exploitation of the best in us — is where this part of the book has been heading, and it is the hinge on which the volume turns from the weapons of discourse to the anatomy of capture.
