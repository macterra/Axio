Enforcement and Erasure

From silencing the speaker to erasing the record

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

Start from a principle I hold without qualification: freedom of association. Individuals and organizations must always retain the right to associate — or to disassociate — with whomever they choose. Employers may fire employees whose speech they find repugnant. Customers may boycott a business. Friends may cut ties. Association means nothing if it cannot be ended, and the right to walk away is not a grudging exception to a norm of forced togetherness; it is the substance of what makes any association voluntary in the first place. I defend it absolutely.

So the question this chapter opens with is not whether people may disassociate. They may. The question is what happens when many of them do it at once, loudly, and in a coordinated way — the phenomenon that gets called cancel culture. Is that merely free association scaled up, the aggregate of a thousand private exits? Or does it mutate, somewhere along the way, into something the same principle actually condemns? The answer is that it does mutate, and the interesting work is locating the exact point of mutation, because the defenders and the critics of cancellation are usually arguing past each other by conflating stages that need to be held apart.

Three Tiers

Cancellation is not one thing. It is a ladder, and the rungs are morally distinct. The mistake that poisons the whole debate — made in good faith by defenders and cynically by opportunists — is to treat the entire ladder as a single act, so that either all of it is protected free association or all of it is mob tyranny. Neither is true. Pull the rungs apart and the ethics resolve.

Tier What happens Verdict
1. Pure disassociation Individuals and organizations independently withdraw: firing, boycotting, cutting ties, declining to buy the book. Legitimate. The direct exercise of the right of association.
2. Coordinated disassociation Groups organize petitions, boycotts, and denunciation campaigns to distance themselves from a speaker. Borderline. Legitimate as persuasion; illegitimate the moment it threatens bystanders.
3. Suppression by proxy Pressure on third parties to cut ties under threat, aimed at cutting a speaker off from all audiences. Illegitimate. Censorship by proxy — an attack on listeners, not a choice by the boycotter.

Tier 1 is pure disassociation, and it is entirely legitimate. If I refuse to buy your book, or a studio fires someone over remarks it finds odious, that is disassociation and nothing more. The principle survives replication intact: if ten thousand people each independently decide the same thing, ten thousand exercises of a right do not sum to a wrong. Country fans dropped the Dixie Chicks after their anti-Bush comments in 2003. Some readers put down J.K. Rowling’s books over her stance on gender. Customers boycotted Chick-fil-A in 2012 over its executives’ remarks on same-sex marriage. In every case, people walked away without trying to prevent anyone else from staying. That is not censorship. It is the ordinary, healthy operation of a free society sorting itself by conviction.

Tier 2 is coordinated disassociation, and it lives on a knife’s edge. When groups organize — petitions, open letters, boycott campaigns — this is still, in its native form, free association. There is nothing illegitimate about saying, We find these views repugnant, we will not support this person, and we invite you to join us. That is persuasion, and persuasion is the lifeblood of the very open discourse cancellation is accused of threatening. The line is crossed not by coordination but by tactic. Compare two sentences. We won’t support them; join us if you agree is an appeal to conscience. Denounce them, or we come for you next is a threat. The first recruits; the second intimidates. The decisive point — and it is the point most often lost — is that disassociation itself is never coercion. Choosing to walk away, even threatening loudly to withdraw support, is simply freedom in use. It becomes coercion only when it is paired with a credible threat of harm beyond disassociation: smearing reputations, harassing families, fabricating accusations against anyone who fails to fall in line. At that moment the act is no longer association at all. It is intimidation wearing association’s clothes. When Disney fired James Gunn in 2018 over decade-old tweets, the disassociation was one thing; the atmosphere in which his defenders stayed silent for fear of being targeted themselves was another, and it is the second that marks the danger.

Tier 3 is suppression by proxy, and it is illegitimate outright. Here the campaign stops being about the canceller’s own affiliations and starts trying to make it impossible for anyone else to associate at all. The mechanism is pressure on third parties — venues, platforms, employers, banks — to sever ties they would otherwise have kept, under threat of becoming the next target. The Hollywood blacklists of the 1950s did not merely let individuals shun alleged communists; they coerced studios into a universal, industry-wide refusal, so that a writer could find no employer anywhere. Clubs were pressured not to book Lenny Bruce, and law enforcement piled on to shut down the performances that survived. The modern form is the organized platform-ban campaign, whose explicit aim is to cut a speaker off from every audience simultaneously — not to walk away, but to ensure no one else can stay. The shift is unmistakable once you look for it. Tier 1 says I won’t listen. Tier 3 says no one may.

The Harm Is Never the Walking Away

Set the three tiers side by side and the moral variable jumps out. It is not the number of people involved, nor the intensity of their disapproval, nor even whether they organize. It is the target of the action. Tiers 1 and 2 act on the canceller’s own relationships: I decide whom I will pay, employ, platform, befriend. Tier 3 acts on everyone else’s: it reaches past the speaker to seize the choices of third parties who never asked to be enlisted. The harm of cancel culture, in its pathological form, is never that people walk away. People have every right to walk away. The harm is that they try to prevent anyone else from listening, affiliating, or deciding differently.

This is why the framing of cancellation as “free association gone large” is not just imprecise but backwards. Free association, exercised at scale, is Tier 1 — and Tier 1 is fine. What people actually object to when they object to cancel culture is Tier 3, which is not an exercise of the right of association but a violation of it, because it overrides the associational choices of the audience. The canceller at Tier 3 is not choosing his own company. He is choosing yours, for you, against your will.

That last observation is the seam with the political and legal theory, which I do not re-derive here. The deepest reason Tier 3 is a wrong is not that it is unkind to the speaker; speakers can absorb disapproval, and no one is owed an audience. It is that it robs the listener — the person who would have chosen to hear, read, or affiliate, and whose access is destroyed without consultation. That is the listener’s right, and it is the load-bearing principle under everything in this chapter. The architecture by which suppression-by-proxy gets institutionalized — the mechanics of platform pressure, financial deplatforming, and coordinated takedown — is the subject of the censorship machine. Vol 7 owns the law and the architecture. This chapter owns the cultural mechanics: how ordinary disapproval escalates, tier by tier, into the suppression the law should not perform and the culture should not tolerate.

Cancellation also does not run on nothing. It runs on accusation, and the accusations that fuel Tier 3 campaigns are manufactured by the machinery examined in The Grammar of Bad Faith — the motivated misreading that converts a factual claim into a firing offense, the strategic outrage that supplies the pressure, the goalpost-shifting that ensures no apology is ever enough. Cancellation is what that grammar does when it stops arguing and starts organizing. The tactics that lose the argument are repurposed to remove the arguer.

The Terminal Stage

Suppression by proxy silences a speaker in the present. There is a further stage, and it is worse, because it does not stop at silencing what is being said. It goes after the record of what was said. The word for it comes from Orwell: the memory hole, the chute in the Ministry of Truth down which inconvenient facts were dropped to be incinerated, vanishing not only from circulation but from the possibility of retrieval.

The distinction is the whole point. Censorship suppresses an idea that everyone still knows exists; the memory hole cultivates the illusion that the idea never existed at all. Censorship says you may not read this. The memory hole says there was never anything to read. One leaves a scar the society can later reopen; the other heals the wound over so cleanly that no one remembers being cut. In our world the mechanism is rarely a furnace. It is selective editing, vanishing web pages, quietly revised histories, and coverage that evaporates from the news cycle before it can lodge in memory. Six cases mark the range, from the merely convenient to the totalitarian.

Churchill’s United States of Europe (1946). In the ruins of the war, Winston Churchill called for a United States of Europe — envisioning, notably, Britain outside the union even as he urged continental integration. The speech later became inconvenient to Euroskeptics, who did not refute it so much as behave as though it had never been delivered. Pro-Europeans cited it; Euroskeptics acted as if it were unspoken. Selective omission as ordinary political tactic — the mildest rung.

The Holodomor. Through the 1930s, New York Times correspondent Walter Duranty systematically downplayed Stalin’s engineered famine in Ukraine. For decades the Holodomor was underreported in the West, its memory effaced by Soviet denial and Western journalistic distortion working in concert. Only later scholarship restored it to public consciousness — a genocide nudged, for a generation, toward the hole.

Soviet photo erasures. The quintessential case. Stalin’s regime airbrushed purged officials out of photographs, encyclopedias, and official records. Trotsky, Bukharin, and countless lesser figures became unpersons: not merely disgraced but retroactively deleted, so that the record showed they had never stood beside the leader at all. This was past propaganda’s ambition — an attempt to excise human beings from reality itself.

The Downing Street Memo (2005). The leaked British document indicating that intelligence had been shaped to fit the predetermined case for the Iraq War made headlines briefly and then, in the American press, simply dissipated. Nothing was formally banned. But the abrupt disappearance of a story of that magnitude from the news cycle is the memory hole operating in an open society, by inattention rather than decree.

Tiananmen Square (1989). Inside China, the massacre of June 4 has been nearly perfectly erased. Textbooks omit it, searches are scrubbed, and younger generations often learn of it only by colliding with a foreign source. It is among the most complete and most chilling modern instances of Orwellian erasure actually achieved at national scale.

The lab-leak hypothesis (2020–21). In the pandemic’s first year, the major platforms labeled discussion of a Wuhan laboratory origin as “misinformation” and removed it — posts deleted, accounts suspended. By mid-2021 official agencies conceded the hypothesis was plausible. A question that belonged in open scientific debate was consigned to the hole and then uneasily retrieved, which demonstrates something important: the erasure need not be permanent to do its damage. For the window in which it held, it foreclosed inquiry that should never have been closed.

Notice the ladder these cases form. Convenient omission, journalistic distortion, physical deletion of persons, disappearance-by-inattention, near-total state erasure, and platform-enforced foreclosure of a live scientific question. What separates the memory hole from every milder failure is intent and object: it does not attack an idea, it attacks the knowledge that the idea was ever contested. When it succeeds, it denies people not just the truth but the awareness that a truth was ever in dispute — and a population that does not know a question was closed cannot reopen it.

This is why the memory hole belongs at the top of the escalation, above even suppression by proxy. Tier 3 preventing you from listening is an assault on the present; you at least know you are being prevented. Erasure is an assault on the past, and it removes the very evidence you would need to notice the assault. The danger is at once subtle and immense: to control memory is to delimit imagination. A society whose record has been edited cannot imagine the alternatives that were struck from it, because it does not know they were ever there to be imagined. The permanently open questions of a free culture become, one deletion at a time, questions no one remembers to ask. The same disorder — a community editing its own environment until the excluded ceases to be thinkable — reappears at the level of whole populations sorting themselves into curated realities, which is the subject of Gated Realities.

The response is not complicated, though it is unending. It is vigilance, and a clear sense of the gradations, so that ordinary human forgetting is not mistaken for a crime and deliberate erasure is not mistaken for forgetting. A free society can survive errors, falsehoods, and bad ideas in enormous quantity; it metabolizes them through the very argument that cancellation and erasure exist to shut down. What it cannot survive is the enforced illusion that reality itself was otherwise — the manufactured amnesia that leaves it unable to recover what it was never allowed to know it lost. Forgetting is natural. Suppression is political. But memory-holing is totalitarian, because it seeks to annihilate the very possibility of remembering.
