The Coercion Continuum

Degree, not kind

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

A medieval peasant owed his lord a substantial share of everything he produced, enforced by the lash and the lord’s court. A modern French taxpayer surrenders a comparable share of everything he produces, enforced by the fine and the revenue court. One of these arrangements we teach schoolchildren to recognize as servitude. The other we call fiscal policy. The instinct — and it is a strong one — says that these are different in kind: that taxation is a civic duty and slavery a moral atrocity, and that putting them in the same sentence is a category error or a provocation.

I am going to argue that the instinct is wrong in a specific way. Taxation and slavery are not the same thing, but they are the same kind of thing: compelled extraction of a person’s labor and output, backed by credible threats of harm. They differ in degree — enormously, and the degree matters enormously. But a difference of degree along a single axis is exactly what the instinct denies, and the denial is doing real moral work. It exempts everything on the near end of the axis from the question the far end answers so easily: by what right?

The Spectrum

Put compelled extraction on a scale. At 0% stands total self-sovereignty: no external coercion, no forced labor, no compulsory obligations of any kind. At 100% stands the absolute appropriation of a person’s output: complete chattel enslavement. Every regime that has ever collected a compulsory share of its subjects’ production sits somewhere between the endpoints.

The placements are estimates, but the bands are instructive. At the low end (0–20%) sit societies with minimal extraction and substantial autonomy: the subjects of early British monarchs, the nineteenth-century United States, modern Singapore, pre-2020 Hong Kong. The moderate band (30–50%) holds most contemporary developed societies — Canada, Israel, Japan, Switzerland — which take a significant share while preserving robust personal and economic freedom. The 50–70% band is where it gets uncomfortable: medieval peasants and Imperial Spain share it with the Nordic welfare states and modern France. Above that (70–90%) come the regimes everyone agrees to condemn — Tsarist Russia, the Soviet Union, Nazi Germany — where extraction was joined to forced labor and comprehensive economic control. And at the extreme (90–100%) stands chattel slavery as practiced in the Americas: near-total appropriation, near-zero autonomy.

Two things follow from taking the spectrum seriously. The first is that no society is exempt. Coercive extraction is not a pathology of failed states; it is a universal feature of political organization, and every nation occupies a position on the axis whether or not it cares to know which one. The second is that the moral question cannot be quarantined at the far end. If appropriating 95% of a person’s labor is monstrous, appropriating 45% is not thereby innocent — it is a smaller quantity of the same thing, and it owes an answer to the same question.

The standard reply is that taxation, unlike slavery, is voluntary: we vote, we participate, we consent. But consent means something. Genuine consent requires a viable option to refuse, and compliance extracted under threat of prosecution and imprisonment is not agreement — it is precisely the credible threat of actual harm to gain compliance that defines coercion. The citizen who “agrees” to taxation because refusal leads to prison has consented in the same sense that the employee facing “sign or be fired” has consented, which is to say not at all. This is the problem of political authority, pressed most carefully by Michael Huemer: every mechanism offered to convert the state’s demands into agreements — tacit consent, residence, democratic participation — fails the tests we apply to consent everywhere else in life.

The second standard reply is that taxation buys things worth having: schools, hospitals, roads, courts. The benefits are real and I do not dispute them. But benefits do not dissolve the ethical question; they are the ethical question. That coercion produced something valuable does not show the coercion was justified — it shows only that the justification question is worth asking, and it must be asked, not presumed answered. What that justification would have to look like is the subject of The Grey Zone; for now the point is narrower: “it pays for good things” is the beginning of an argument, not the end of one.

Partial Ownership

There is a libertarian one-liner that the state “owns a piece of you.” It is usually dismissed as a metaphor, and an overheated one. Simon Goddek states the strong version:

From birth to death, the state owns a piece of you. You need permission to build, to sell, to drive, to grow, to speak. Try living without their papers or their taxes and see how “free” you are.

Subjected to technical analysis rather than eye-rolling, the claim holds up remarkably well. Ownership, defined precisely, is a bundle of enforceable claims over three distinct rights: the right to use a resource, the right to exclude others from it, and the right to dispose of it. Not a paper title, not a feeling of possession — enforceable claims. Now audit the state’s relationship to its citizens against each element.

Use. The state dictates how you may use your body, property, time, and speech. Nearly every significant activity — driving, building a house, operating a business, practicing a trade, in some places even marrying — requires its prior permission. Law constrains what you may ingest, which medicines you may access, what you may say in which venues. Your use rights over yourself are managed, at a fundamental level, by an entity that is not you.

Exclude. The state controls access. Occupational licensing excludes you from whole categories of economic activity unless it approves; borders, visas, and zoning law determine where you may live, work, and travel. It can — and routinely does — fence you out of portions of your own life.

Dispose. The state appropriates a share of everything you earn, under threat; taxation is extraction, not exchange. And when you die, estate taxes and inheritance regulation determine what you may pass on, to whom, and on what terms. Your right to transfer what is yours operates within boundaries the state draws and redraws at will.

Use, exclude, dispose: the state holds enforceable partial claims over all three. That is not a metaphor for ownership. That is the definition of ownership, satisfied. The conclusion writes itself, and it is the same conclusion the spectrum delivered from the other direction: partial enslavement and partial ownership are one fact in two vocabularies. The spectrum measures how much of your output is appropriated; the ownership analysis identifies the legal structure doing the appropriating.

One honest qualification, which matters more than it first appears. The ownership argument treats the citizen’s title to himself and his property as clean, and the state’s claims as pure imposition on that clean title. Titles to external property are often not clean: land acquired by conquest, wealth built on past expropriation, holdings whose lineage runs through exactly the kind of coercion this chapter indicts. Where a title is historically compromised, enforcing it can itself be the violation, and the same coercive act — defending “my property” — can flip moral valence depending on where the property came from. This does not rescue the state; the state’s claim on your labor does not become consensual because someone’s great-grandfather stole a farm. But it means the clean-title picture cannot simply be assumed, and it is one of the grey cases that The Grey Zone takes up in earnest.

The Structural License

Colin Wright speaks for most people when he says: “It’s not ‘authoritarian’ to enforce laws and arrest criminals.” As a defense of the state against the charge of authoritarianism, it has the virtue of being obviously true and the defect of skipping the essential question: who defines the law, and by what right? If the law itself is unjust, or if its enforcement rests on a monopoly of coercion over peaceful individuals who never consented to it, then enforcing it is authoritarian — by structure, not merely by abuse.

The structural definition: authoritarianism is a political system in which a central authority claims the legitimate right to initiate coercion over peaceful individuals without their explicit, ongoing consent. And statism — any system in which the state holds a monopoly on law and force, with no competitive alternative an individual can choose or refuse without forfeiting basic rights — satisfies that definition wherever it appears. Four features, jointly universal across statist regimes:

  1. Monopoly of force. There is no peaceful opt-out. (The state prosecutes private monopolies while holding the one monopoly that underwrites all its others — a hypocrisy examined in Monopoly Hypocrisy.)
  2. Presumed consent. Individuals are treated as having agreed to the system without ever signing anything.
  3. Unilateral rule-making. The state decides what counts as law and can redefine crime at will.
  4. Coercive enforcement. Compliance is backed by threat of force, including for victimless offenses.

Grant those four features and the differences between regimes, real as they are, become differences of position on a spectrum:

Type Core features Authoritarian risk Examples
Totalitarian statism Centralized control over all aspects of life — political, economic, cultural, personal. No meaningful dissent allowed. Extreme — coercion is overt, frequent, and unlimited. North Korea, Nazi Germany, Stalinist USSR
Strong statism State controls the economy heavily and regulates personal life, but allows limited dissent if it doesn’t threaten the regime. Very high — coercion is systemic; dissent is tolerated only superficially. China, Iran, Saudi Arabia
Democratic statism State power legitimized by elections; still claims a monopoly on law and coercion over non-consenting individuals. Civil liberties exist but are fragile. Moderate–high — consent is assumed, and laws can infringe rights via majority vote. USA, UK, EU states
Minarchist statism State limited to police, courts, and defense; no welfare, regulation, or cultural engineering. Low–moderate — coercion is rare, but the principle of monopoly remains. Hypothetical night-watchman state
Anarchism / statelessness No monopolistic state; law and enforcement are competitive, voluntary, and contract-based. Zero structural authoritarianism — though other risks, such as local coercive power, remain. Historical Icelandic Commonwealth, modern polycentric-law proposals

The difference between a friendly democracy and a brutal dictatorship is not that one is authoritarian and the other is not. It is that one uses its authoritarian power sparingly and with broad social acquiescence — for now. Whether the mechanism is gentle (traffic law enforced on non-consenting pacifists) or brutal (secret police breaking down doors), it is the same mechanism: the state asserting a right to coerce beyond anything that was ever voluntarily agreed. The underlying structural license to initiate coercion is identical across the whole table, and a license, once granted, can be expanded. Historically, it usually is. That is why the license itself, and not merely its current rate of use, is the correct object of moral scrutiny — a regime’s position on the spectrum tells you how bad things are; the license tells you how bad they can get without anything structural having to change.

The Slaveowner’s Claim

Set the two endpoints of the spectrum side by side and look at the structure of the claims being made.

The slaveowner claimed a right to another man’s output by birth, caste, or conquest. The state claims a right to a share of every citizen’s output by law. In both cases a ruling authority asserts a nonconsensual claim on the fruits of another’s labor and enforces it with credible threats of harm. The differences are real: the slaveowner’s violence was direct and the state’s is distributed; the whip has been replaced by the audit letter, the overseer by the revenue agency. And the state wraps its claim in moral abstraction — “the social contract,” “the common good,” “your fair share” — where the slaveowner rarely bothered. The psychological achievement is considerable: the subjects of the modern claim experience their compliance as virtue. I pay my fair share, the citizen says, as if virtue could be denominated in percentages of what has been taken.

Notice that the strongest defense of taxation collapses into this structure rather than escaping it. Taxation funds schools, hospitals, infrastructure. So it does — and slavery funded plantations, palaces, and a fair amount of classical civilization. The socially approved use of extracted labor does not negate the extraction; at worst it launders the coercion through moral vocabulary so effectively that the question of justification never comes up at all.

Here is what the isomorphism does and does not establish, and I want to be precise, because this is where the argument is most often either overclaimed or dodged. It does not establish that paying 40% income tax is the moral equivalent of being owned. The continuum itself forbids that reading: degree is the whole point, and the distance between a 40% claim and a 95% claim is the distance between a constrained life and a stolen one. What the isomorphism establishes is that they are points on one axis, instances of one kind of act — and therefore answerable to one question. Coercive appropriation of a person’s labor requires justification. At the slavery end, no justification exists or could exist; the institution fails every test absolutely, which is why its evil is so legible. At the taxation end, justification is at least conceivable — coercion that is limited, proportional, accountable, and genuinely necessary to protect agency might pass the test that the Agency Protection Principle states and The Grey Zone elaborates. But might pass means must actually be made to pass. The function of the instinct this chapter opened with — taxation and slavery are simply different things — is to excuse the near end of the axis from ever taking the test.

Moral history says the excuse will not hold forever. Every generation has blind spots that are obvious in retrospect: societies that debated theology and philosophy with great sophistication saw nothing to discuss in serfdom, in conquest, in owning human beings. What is ethically obvious today was invisible then, and there is no reason to believe the process stopped with us. It is entirely possible that future generations will look at compulsory extraction of half a population’s output — enforced by imprisonment, presumed consented-to by birth — the way we look at serfdom: not as the worst thing its era did, but as the thing its era could not see. In the darkest reading, they may judge that we did not abolish the ownership of human labor so much as perfect its administration — chains replaced by compliance, whips by withholding, submission by civic pride. I hold that as a live possibility rather than a settled verdict. Moral evolution is measured by the reduction of coercion, not by the prettiness of the excuses for it, and the prudent response to history’s pattern is to run the justification test on our own institutions before posterity runs it for us.

The continuum, then, delivers a diagnosis, not a sentence. It tells you what taxation is: coercive extraction, partial ownership, the same kind of claim the slaveowner made, at a different and much milder point on the axis. What it does not tell you is what follows. For that, two further questions have to be answered. First: what exactly is this mechanism funding, and what does an organization become when extraction is its revenue model? That is the subject of Extortion-Funded Organizations. Second: under what conditions, if any, could coercion of this kind be justified — and who bears the burden of showing them? That is the subject of The Grey Zone, and nothing in this chapter is complete without it.
