Extortion-Funded Organizations

The funding test

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

A restaurant owner receives two demands for payment. The first arrives in person: a well-dressed man explains that the neighborhood can be dangerous, that his organization provides protection, and that the monthly fee is due on the first. Everyone understands what happens to businesses that decline. The second arrives by mail, on official letterhead, itemizing what is owed and citing the statutes under which nonpayment leads to liens, seizure, and imprisonment. One of these demands is a felony and the other is a civic duty, and nearly everyone can tell you which is which. What almost no one can tell you is what structural feature distinguishes them — because at the level of mechanism, there isn’t one. Each is a payment extracted under a credible threat of harm. The differences are real, but they lie elsewhere: in scale, in institutionalization, in cultural acceptance. Not in the method.

The Coercion Continuum established the degree question: coercive extraction runs on a spectrum from full self-sovereignty to chattel slavery, and every tax regime sits somewhere between. This chapter asks a different and more clinical question — not how much is taken, but by what mechanism an organization sustains itself. It is a single diagnostic, and it can be asked of any institution on Earth: is this organization funded by voluntary exchange, or by payments extracted under threat?

The Funding Test

Most organizations can be classified by how their revenue arrives. I propose a three-way taxonomy, and I have chosen the terms deliberately for neutrality and symmetry.

A Voluntarily-Funded Organization (VFO) relies on voluntary transactions: sales, donations, membership fees, subscriptions. Private businesses, charities, clubs, and churches are VFOs. Every unit of revenue represents a choice someone made and could have declined at no penalty beyond forgoing the good itself. Withhold your payment and the organization’s only recourse is to withhold its product.

An Extortion-Funded Organization (EFO) derives its primary funding from payments extracted under a credible threat of harm. Each element of that definition is load-bearing. The threat must be credible — real and enforceable, or it collapses into bluff. The harm may be violence, imprisonment, property seizure, or any other coercive deprivation. And the payment is transferred under duress, not chosen. This is not a new notion of coercion invented for the occasion; it is the canonical definition — the deliberate use of a credible conditional threat of material setback to obtain compliance — established in What Counts as Coercion, applied to the question of institutional revenue. Nation-states funded by taxation are EFOs. So are mafias funded by protection rackets.

A Resource-Funded Organization (RFO) sustains itself through control of natural resources or captive monopolies rather than through either voluntary custom or direct extraction from a subject population. Petrostates are the clean example: a government that pumps its budget out of the ground can rule without taxing, which is one reason such governments so often feel free to ignore the people they rule.

The symmetry between states and cartels is intentional, and it is important to be precise about what it claims. It does not claim that a constitutional democracy and a crime syndicate are morally identical — they differ enormously in what they provide, how they are constrained, and how much of what they take flows back as services. It claims that their funding mechanism is identical, and that the differences lie in degree of institutionalization, legitimacy narratives, and social acceptance — not in method. That is a structural observation, and the taxonomy stays descriptive on purpose. Whether consent theories, social-contract arguments, or service provision can redeem a state-scale EFO is a genuine question — it is the question of The Grey Zone, where coercion’s legitimacy gets the conditional, burden-scaled treatment it deserves. But you cannot ask the redemption question honestly until you have stated plainly what is to be redeemed. The value of the term EFO is that it strips away the euphemism first: coercion, not voluntarism, is the lifeblood of the entity. Then we can argue about justification.

The Parasite, Strictly Defined

“All complex ecosystems have parasites,” Cory Doctorow has observed. Take the aphorism literally and it yields a second diagnostic lens on the EFO — this one economic rather than definitional. Define an economic parasite strictly: an entity that systematically extracts resources without proportional productive contribution, imposes inefficiency on the broader system, and benefits disproportionately at the expense of the system’s productive elements. This is a biological term of art, not an insult; parasitism is a resource-flow pattern, and the question of whether an institution instantiates it is empirical.

Governments meet all three criteria. The extraction is the EFO mechanism itself: taxation, tariffs, licensing fees, and — less visibly — inflation, none of which are voluntary transactions. Unlike trade, where both parties benefit by their own lights, extraction forcibly reallocates resources from productive agents to politically designated purposes.

The systematic burden is measurable: compliance costs and bureaucratic delay; resource allocation driven by electoral calculation and lobbying rather than by any signal of what people actually value; deadweight loss wherever a mandate overrides a voluntary arrangement. Political processes do not merely allocate resources differently than markets do — they allocate without the feedback that makes correction possible, because their revenue does not depend on anyone being satisfied.

And the benefits are asymmetric. What is extracted from everyone flows disproportionately to organized someones: bureaucracies, public-sector unions, politically favored corporations, military contractors. This is not capitalism malfunctioning; it is the impostor that Capitalism on Trial distinguishes from the genuine article — profit secured through the state’s extraction apparatus rather than through anything anyone freely chose to buy.

The parasite framing earns its keep in the conclusion it forces. You do not eradicate parasites from a complex ecosystem; you manage them. Given that governments exist and will continue to, the practical question shifts from elimination to load reduction: less compulsory extraction, fewer allocation distortions, more transparency about who benefits. That is a pragmatism, not a surrender — the same judgment a rancher makes about ticks.

The Necessity Argument Runs Backward

The standard objection arrives on schedule: government provides essential services — security, courts, roads — and these justify the compulsion that funds them. Notice, first, what the objection concedes. It does not deny the extraction; it argues the extraction is worth it. The moment the argument is phrased that way, the state’s advocate has already accepted the EFO classification and moved to the mitigation phase.

The necessity argument cannot settle the matter by naming the service. A coerced payment reveals nothing about the payer’s valuation, so “people fund it” cannot be used as evidence of consent when refusal is punished. But voluntary underfunding does not prove that nobody values the result at its cost: non-excludability, free-riding, transaction costs, unequal purchasing power, and assurance problems can separate willingness from observed payment. The economic analysis in The Myth of Underprovision therefore treats funding as evidence rather than definition. The political burden remains: identify the provision failure, compare voluntary and coercive mechanisms, and justify the imposed contribution under the Grey Zone rather than smuggling legitimacy in through the word necessary.

This gives us a funding inquiry rather than a semantic veto. Voluntary payment is strong evidence of value; coerced payment is not. Failure to attract payment is evidence whose weight depends on whether contributors can exclude free riders, coordinate assurance, and afford the contribution. A proposal that invokes those frictions must show them and then show why its remedy is less damaging than the failure. “Who will build the roads?” becomes an empirical and comparative question, not a proof for either monopoly or voluntarism.

When Plunder Funds Physics

The test is easiest to apply to institutions you already distrust. The instructive cases are the ones you love. So consider science — and permit me, for this one section, the older and hotter vocabulary, because the case study is precisely about what the neutral taxonomy looks like from inside the pasture.

There is a cartoon that shows a cow standing on a crate, pointing toward the “Meat Packer” building in the distance. Around him, the other cows graze indifferently. One turns to another and says, “I see Mr. Conspiracy Theory is at it again.” That is how it feels trying to explain the nature of state funding — even to those who think they are its greatest beneficiaries. Especially them.

When a physicist like Sean Carroll laments cuts to federal research funding as a cultural and civilizational loss — something sacred being taken from the public — he imagines the state as a neutral engine of progress, a steward of reason and enlightenment. It is not, and it never was. It is, as Bastiat put it, “that great fiction by which everyone tries to live at the expense of everyone else.” What is really happening when research budgets shrink is simpler and older than a war on science: the spoils of plunder are being reallocated.

Science is not an exception to the mechanism; it is a participant in it. Scientists, like farmers, manufacturers, and weapons contractors, organize politically to extract funding from the state. It feels noble only because the product — knowledge — carries cultural prestige. The method is the same: form a coalition, moralize your preferences, and fight for a bigger cut. When the funding is cut, the aggrieved scientist is not objecting to coercion — only to coercion that has stopped favoring him. He is the cow who trusted the fence, believed the pasture was freedom, and now sees his faction pushed closer to the slaughterhouse. His reaction is not anti-authoritarian. It is factional — the sound of a priest of reason discovering that the cathedral has new tenants.

Calling research a public good can name a real mechanism — benefits that are difficult to exclude and easy to share — while also functioning as advocacy for public funding. The label does not decide the policy. The relevant questions are which spillovers exist, which financing mechanisms can capture them, what research portfolios each mechanism selects, and whether a coercive levy survives the same burden applied elsewhere. Scientific prestige supplies no exemption, but neither does the existence of coercion prove that the underlying knowledge has no dispersed value.

What would science look like without plunder? It already exists, in pockets: crowdfunded research, open-source data, voluntary patronage, decentralized grant-making. These are not utopian sketches; they are working prototypes of a post-coercive epistemology — truth pursued without extracting tribute from unbelievers. The cow who sees the slaughterhouse isn’t safe, just awake. But awareness matters. If you don’t question the fence, you’re not enlightened. You’re livestock.

The Second Harvest

Now step back out of the hot register, because the final case shows why the neutral one is ultimately the right one: extraction does not require a villain. It does not even require intent.

A meme summarizes the mechanism in a whisper: if we tax them with inflation, we get them with capital gains as well. The satire is accurate. When a government expands the money supply, the purchasing power of every existing unit declines — a transfer of value from savers to the issuer of new currency, hidden in the arithmetic of prices rather than imposed by any statute. (The monetary machinery — what currency is, and why multiplying the numerators over a collapsing denominator taxes every holder at once — is What Is Money?’s territory; here I need only the classification: dilution is extraction, whatever the law calls it.) That is the first harvest.

The second follows from the first. Savers respond rationally to a weakening currency by moving wealth into assets that hold value — equities, real estate, Bitcoin. As the currency dilutes, those assets rise in nominal price even where their real value is unchanged. Then the tax code arrives and taxes the nominal gain as if it were profit. The same wealth is reduced twice: first by the erosion of purchasing power, then by taxation of the inflation adjustment itself. The accounting system recognizes the number, not the substance. A saver who exactly preserved her wealth is billed as though she had grown it.

The instructive part is that no one needs to have designed this. It is not a conspiracy but a structural blind spot. Public finance runs on aggregates — price indexes, monetary bases, nominal growth — that treat nominal changes as real data. The abstractions are useful for coordination and dangerous when reified: once policy mistakes the map for the territory, it acts on symbols rather than conditions, and it ends up punishing prudence and rewarding debt without anyone choosing that inversion. Inflation transfers value silently; nominal capital-gains taxation formalizes the transfer. Together they demonstrate that a modern fiscal system can produce extraction without intent, through nothing more than the inertia of its own definitions. Inflation creates the mirage of increased wealth; capital-gains taxation harvests it.

This is why the taxonomy had to be built structural rather than moral. A definition of extortion that requires a scheming extortionist would miss the largest extractions entirely — the ones executed by definitions, defaults, and price indexes while every official involved believes, sincerely, that he is administering a neutral system. The EFO test does not ask after anyone’s heart. It asks how the money moves, and under what threat, and it returns the same answer whether the enforcement arrives by knee-breaker, by statute, or by denominator.

So ask the question — of every agency, every institute, every organization that solicits your respect. Is it funded by voluntary exchange, or by extortion? The answer does not end the argument; classification is not condemnation, and the conditions under which coerced funding might still be justified are exactly what The Grey Zone exists to adjudicate — while The Archist Axiom takes up what could replace EFO funding even in the hardest cases. But the answer does start the argument honestly. An institution’s moral standing cannot be read off its mission statement, its prestige, or the nobility of its product. It starts with the lifeblood: how the revenue arrives, and what happens to the people who decline to send it.
