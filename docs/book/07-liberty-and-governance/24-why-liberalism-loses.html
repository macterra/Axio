Why Liberalism Loses

The structural unpopularity of liberty

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

Classical liberalism has the strangest track record in the history of ideas: it wins every experiment and loses every election. Societies organized around individual liberty, free exchange, limited government, and impartial law became rich, free, and creative; the contrast between open societies and their closed twins is not subtle. Yet liberalism remains unpopular even among its beneficiaries.

The Authoritarian Default supplied the evolved pull toward hierarchy, conformity, and certainty. This chapter owns the other half of the puzzle: structural handicaps specific to liberalism itself.

The position takes individuals as the moral unit; voluntary exchange as the engine of wealth; government as a protector of rights; law as universal and impartial; property as a basis of independence; pluralism as the condition of coexistence; and spontaneous order as the mechanism of complex cooperation. Why is that package so hard to sell?

The Structural Handicaps

Spontaneous order is counterintuitive. Human cognition searches for agents behind complex events. A king or conspiracy satisfies; an emergent equilibrium of millions of choices does not. When prices rise, someone must be gouging. Liberalism’s foundational insight — that no one is steering, and that this is often why coordination works — is a permanent marketing liability.

Its benefits are dispersed; grievances are concentrated. Competition lowers many prices invisibly. A tariff, subsidy, or license gives an identifiable group a visible benefit. Nobody organizes around careers that exist because a rule was never written, while beneficiaries of intervention know whom to lobby.

Politics rewards centralizers. Programs produce prestige, influence, ribbon-cuttings, and grateful constituencies; restraint produces no monument. Liberalism asks officeholders to win power and decline to use it, so later chapters seek structural constraints rather than better officeholders.

Democracy discounts the future. Visible benefits arrive before the next election; debt, distorted incentives, and institutional decay arrive later and diffusely. Trading future harm for present help is often rational under the electoral scoreboard.

The fixed-pie fallacy biases every audience. If wealth is a pile rather than a process, every fortune looks like theft and redistribution like common sense. Wealth Is Not a Pile owns the economics; the handicap is political.

Visible inequality looks like injury. Liberalism promises equality before law, not outcome. The inference from disparity to harm is invalid without a mechanism, as Against Envy argues, but it is emotionally immediate.

Narratives want heroes and villains. Anonymous entrepreneurs, incremental innovation, contract, and custom make poor protagonists. A philosophy of emergent order concedes the story to ideologies offering saviors and scapegoats.

Liberalism refuses to run an indoctrination machine. Its reliance on persuasion follows from its principles; rivals freely build catechisms, parties, and tribal belonging. Liberalism fields individual arguers against organized movements.

These handicaps intensify where collectivist traditions are stronger and liberal institutions thinner. Together they describe a systematic mismatch between what liberalism offers and what political competition rewards.

The Political Triangle

The left–right line obscures the contest. A more useful triangle maps three pure answers to what government is for:

Vertex Offers Characteristic goods
Individualism Self-determination Sovereignty, responsibility, accountability
Maternalism Care Welfare, support, social insurance
Paternalism Order Policing, defense, protection

Each appeal is honest. Individualism offers freedom to shape and own a life. Maternalism protects against hardship. Paternalism supplies stability and collective defense.

Ordinary left–right competition occurs mostly between the maternal and paternal vertices. The individualist apex is claimed rhetorically but rarely pursued, because concentrated interests, political incentives, narrative hunger, and the short electoral horizon pull parties downward. Liberal democracies settle near the middle: enough autonomy to generate wealth, care to blunt hardship, and order to keep the streets calm. Its stability is also its danger.

Permanent Adolescence

That equilibrium shelters citizens enough to foster dependence, protects them enough to reduce accountability, and regulates them enough to discourage risk. No single policy produces the result; maternal and paternal interventions remove hardships and dangers while also removing reasons to build resilience and choices once owned by citizens.

The result is permanent adolescence: real freedoms and real constraints, someone to feed the citizen and someone to ground him, but no point at which the weight of his life becomes fully his own. Citizens become consumers of governance rather than accountable agents.

The equilibrium may be pragmatically optimal in the short run. It wins because it offers care, protection, and enough freedom to finance both. It is also self-reinforcing: citizens formed by dependency demand more of it, and each crisis moves the polity away from the individualist apex. Liberalism loses to a comfortable arrangement that dissolves its constituency one reasonable protection at a time.

Losing by One’s Own Hand

Structural handicaps are not an alibi. Liberal movements also abandon their own discovery mechanism. Selecting standard-bearers primarily by identity criteria rather than demonstrated competence and appeal looks like tokenism; installing a candidate through insider anointment creates a legitimacy deficit. Both suspend competitive selection while praising it elsewhere.

Liberalism’s unpopularity is traceable: a subtle and demanding philosophy competes against rivals engineered for the evolved palate. The answer is not lament. Its advocates must make spontaneous order visible, give dispersed benefits a voice, build institutions that do not depend on restraint, and remember that a philosophy of adulthood cannot win by promising comfort. It can win only by producing adults who no longer want to be governed like children.
