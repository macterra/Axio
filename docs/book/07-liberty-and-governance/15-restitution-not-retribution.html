Restitution, Not Retribution

Justice as restoration, enforcement as trade-off

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

There is a class of crimes about which no decent person is neutral. Rape, murder, slavery — these do not merely injure; they destroy human agency, which is the thing this whole volume exists to protect. So the natural demand is zero tolerance: not one more rape, not one more murder, whatever it takes.

Take the demand literally and watch what “whatever it takes” turns out to be. A society that actually eliminated such crimes — not reduced them, eliminated them — would need to close every opening through which a free choice could become a criminal one. Ubiquitous surveillance, so that no act goes unobserved. Predictive policing, so that intent is intercepted before it matures into action. Neurological intervention, so that the disposition itself can be corrected at the source. Crime rates would plummet. And the society that achieved this would have become the most comprehensive destroyer of human agency in its own jurisdiction — more thorough than the criminals it set out to stop, because the criminals attacked some people’s agency and the cure attacks everyone’s.

This is the crime-fighting paradox, and it is not an accident of clumsy policy. It follows from what agency is. The ability to make meaningful choices inherently includes the possibility of choices that harm others; a world in which harmful choice is impossible is a world in which choice has been abolished. You cannot eradicate the abuse of freedom without eradicating the freedom.

The previous chapter asked how a legal order can know that an accusation is true. This chapter asks two further questions that sit on either side of that one: how hard should a society try to prevent harm in the first place, and what should it do once harm has occurred? The answers turn out to share a structure. In both cases the traditional system pursues a goal it cannot name coherently — total suppression, symbolic repayment — and in both cases the repair is the same move: stop asking how much the wicked should suffer, and start asking how much of the world can be restored.

The Marginal Equilibrium

Zero tolerance survives as a moral standard: a commitment to keep pushing, and a refusal to shrug at atrocity. As a policy target it is incoherent, because the two failure modes are not symmetric alternatives — they are the same failure. Allow too much freedom and agency is destroyed by victimization. Allow too much intervention and agency is destroyed by the interveners. Either way the thing being lost is the same quantity, which means the goal must be stated in terms of that quantity: maximize total human agency.

Stated that way, the enforcement question answers itself in the shape economists will recognize, and no formula is needed to see it. Every increment of enforcement buys some reduction in agency-destroying harm and costs some agency in freedom, privacy, and trust. Early increments are cheap and effective: basic policing against violence buys enormous harm-reduction for little liberty. Late increments invert: eliminating the last marginal crimes costs extraordinary intrusion for vanishing benefit. The optimum is where the marginal agency gained by further enforcement equals the marginal agency lost to it — and that optimum is reached well before zero crime. A residual level of harm, morally troubling as it is, is the price of a society whose members are still agents.

Two things follow. First, the residue is not hypocrisy. A society that tolerates some crime rather than becoming a panopticon has not compromised its principles; it has understood them. Second, the enforcement budget is not the only lever. The equilibrium depends on the terms of the trade — how much harm-reduction each unit of intrusion buys — and those terms can be improved. Culture and norms suppress harm through voluntary constraint, at no cost in liberty. Empowerment and education build the capacity to resist harm and to flourish despite residual risk. Transparent, accountable institutions deliver their protection with minimal intrusion. And technology — decentralized reputation systems, transparent auditing, privacy-preserving verification — can produce accountability without surveillance. None of these is a substitute for enforcement at the core; all of them shift the curve, so that the same freedom buys more safety.

There is a further reason not to over-invest in the enforcement machinery itself: it does not bind evenly. Law binds only where power cannot override it, so every expansion of coercive enforcement is an expansion that the well-connected will partially escape and the powerless will absorb in full. A society that responds to crime primarily by enlarging its apparatus of compulsion is buying its safety with the agency of its weakest members.

But the largest improvement available is not at the margin of prevention at all. It is in what the system does after harm occurs — and here the problem is not miscalibration but incoherence.

A Debt Owed to No One

The reigning theory of criminal justice is compressed into a single phrase: the offender must pay his debt to society. He pays it by serving time. Say the phrase slowly and its incoherence surfaces. When a crime occurs, specific individuals suffer tangible harm — a particular person was robbed, beaten, defrauded, bereaved. The phrase quietly replaces that person with an abstraction. Society becomes the creditor; the victim becomes a witness in someone else’s case, styled The People v. the accused, and when the debt is “paid” — years of the offender’s life burned in a cell — the victim has received nothing. No compensation was ever part of the transaction, because the victim was never party to it. Society is not the injured party; society, as a later chapter argues in full, is not the kind of thing that can be injured at all. Individuals are. A justice system that answers to the abstraction while the actual creditor goes unpaid has not administered justice; it has performed it.

This is the fork between the two families of justice. Retributive justice is punishment-focused: it asks what the offender deserves to suffer, delivers the suffering, and calls the account settled. Restorative justice is victim-centric: it asks what the victim lost, and directs the system’s entire force at replacing it. Retribution offers society psychological satisfaction — the symbolic assurance that wickedness meets consequence. Restitution offers the injured party their life back, to whatever degree a transfer of value can accomplish that.

Judged by results, the dominant model fails on four counts. It neglects victims: they emerge from the process uncompensated or nearly so, their restoration treated as a charitable afterthought rather than the point. It fails to deter: recidivism rates are the standing refutation of the claim that caging people reforms them or frightens others out of imitation. Its social cost is enormous: prisons consume vast resources to warehouse people in enforced idleness, producing neither restitution nor rehabilitation — pure expenditure, no repair. And it is morally incoherent: punishment divorced from restitution severs action from consequence. The offender who “pays his debt” by sitting in a cell never confronts the specific human being he harmed or the specific loss he caused; he confronts the state, an adversary against whom his self-image can cast him as the victim.

There is a carve-out to make honestly. Some harms genuinely are collective: environmental contamination, systemic fraud, the degradation of a shared resource. But notice that even here the correct response is restitution scaled up, not punishment redirected — the affected population is a large set of specific injured individuals, and compensation flows to them, not to the treasury of an abstraction. The carve-out extends the principle; it does not suspend it. And society retains a real role in either case, just not the role of creditor: it serves as the impartial arbiter that determines facts and proportionality, the referee rather than the plaintiff.

The deepest irony is that restitution is the part of criminal justice that never needed defending. Of all the coercions a legal order performs, forcing the thief to compensate his victim is the easy case — coercion that tracks a prior violation and aims to repair it, legitimate on every theory in the field. Imprisonment beyond what restitution and protection require is the hard case, the one that would need to survive the full burden of justification. Our systems have inverted the ordering: they treat the hard case as the default and the easy case as an optional garnish. If retribution nonetheless persists, it is not because arguments sustain it. It persists on cultural inertia, political incentives — no prosecutor was ever unseated for excessive toughness — and the ancient satisfaction of vengeance, which is real, human, and not a theory of justice.

Universal Compensatory Justice

What replaces it? An earlier statement of my position kept the offender’s moral accountability at the center — restitution as a personal moral debt, paid by the offender to the victim, with the offender’s reflection and reform among the goals; the mature version, Universal Compensatory Justice, discards that scaffolding and keeps the invariant: the victim’s restoration is what justice must accomplish, and the moral bookkeeping turns out to be machinery the system runs better without.

UCJ defines justice as a restorative process with a single criterion of success: restore the victim, as fully as possible, to their pre-harm state — independent of the perpetrator’s intent, character, or moral culpability. Everything in the design follows from taking that criterion seriously.

Compensation is immediate and layered. The victim’s own insurance pays first, at once, without litigation — the way a homeowner’s policy pays after a fire without first requiring the arsonist’s conviction. For the uninsured, a public restitution fund provides the same immediate relief, so that access to restoration never depends on the victim’s foresight or means. The current system makes the victim’s compensation contingent on the offender being caught, convicted, and solvent — three lotteries in sequence, resolved years after the harm. UCJ severs that dependency completely. The victim is made whole first; the accounting comes after.

Costs then migrate to their origin. Through subrogation — the unglamorous mechanism that already settles millions of insurance claims — the paying insurer recovers its outlay from the perpetrator’s insurer, or from the perpetrator directly if uninsured. The process is mechanistic and impartial: a tracing of financial responsibility back to the party who originated the risk, with no moral verdict anywhere in the loop. A factual determination is still required — who caused what remains a question of fact, and everything the previous chapter demands of adjudication still binds. But the stakes of error transform. A mistaken money judgment can be reversed and compensated; a mistaken decade in a cell cannot. A system whose worst error is recoverable is, for fallible adjudicators, categorically safer than one whose worst error is not.

Behavior is shaped by prices, not cages. The perpetrator’s consequence is actuarial: his premiums rise. Reckless drivers, negligent manufacturers, violent men — each becomes progressively more expensive to insure, and the rising price is the deterrent, felt continuously rather than threatened remotely. This is not a soft substitute for real accountability; it is accountability in its most honest form. Risk is harm: imposing risk on others is already a contraction of their futures, before and whether or not the bad outcome lands — so a premium priced to risk is priced to harm itself, charging the drunk driver for every night he drives drunk rather than only the night he kills someone. And unlike statutes, prices operate on the layer where behavior is actually determined: people respond to incentive gradients, not rulebooks, and the premium writes the gradient directly into every risky choice.

The commitments underneath the machinery are worth stating plainly, because each is a deliberate rejection. Anti-retributivism: punishment does not constitute justice; compensation does. Outcome-orientation: victim restoration is the sole criterion — not offender suffering, not social symbolism. Actuarial accountability: responsibility is determined by causation and priced by risk, not adjudicated as sin. Efficiency as a moral property: a justice system that restores victims in days rather than years is not merely cheaper; it is more just, because restoration delayed is restoration diminished.

The Objections

The perpetrator escapes justice. Only if justice means suffering — which is precisely the premise under examination, not a fact the objection may help itself to. Under UCJ the perpetrator does not escape anything: he bears the full cost of the harm he caused, traced to him mechanically and priced into his future. What he escapes is the ritual of degradation. Asking “but has he suffered enough?” is asking the retributive question, and the answer to it is that the victim’s restoration was the debt, and it has been paid.

Intentional harms go unchecked. Deliberate wrongdoing meets escalating economic consequences: each harm raises the price of the next, and the trajectory of a persistent aggressor is financial ruin — deterrence that compounds, where prison sentences merely repeat. The system distinguishes the malicious from the unlucky the way every insurance market already does: in the price.

It abandons rehabilitation. It unbundles rehabilitation. Therapeutic and educational programs remain available and desirable — as their own enterprise, run on their own logic, rather than smuggled into a compensation system they would distort. Making the victim whole and making the offender better are two different projects; UCJ’s claim is only that the first must never be held hostage to the second.

It lets the rich buy the right to harm. Universal minimum liability coverage sets the floor: everyone is insured, so every victim is compensated, regardless of who harmed them. Above the floor, premiums scale with risk, and the wealthy harm-doer discovers that his insurer has no sentimental attachment to his wealth — his price rises like anyone’s. The current system is the one with the wealth exemption: the rich already purchase superior outcomes through superior counsel; the poor already absorb the enforcement the rich deflect. A system of transparent prices is harder, not easier, to buy your way out of than a system of discretionary punishment.

Justice Without the Monopoly

Nothing in this machinery requires a state at its center. Insurance is a market. Subrogation is a contract. Risk pricing is an actuarial discipline older than most governments. The extensions that would complete the system are likewise decentralized by nature: specialized risk pools for high-risk trades and activities; independent arbitration for disputed claims, with transparent dispute-resolution rails replacing courtroom queues; reputation systems that make compensation histories legible the way credit histories already are, so that reliability earns its discount; analytics that price risk precisely instead of prejudicially; and the whole structure evolving by feedback rather than statute. Whether the residual functions — the arbiter of fact, the enforcement of the final judgment against the defiant — themselves require a coercive monopoly is the question this volume reaches in The Archist Axiom and the chapters that follow it. Here it is enough to notice how little of justice, once justice means restoration, still looks like something only a sovereign could do.

And notice, finally, what this does to the paradox that opened the chapter. The crime-fighting trade-off was never fixed; it depends on how much agency each unit of harm-reduction costs. Retribution sits at the expensive end: it deters weakly, restores no one, and consumes agency wholesale — the prisoner’s entirely, the taxpayer’s partially, the surveilled society’s increasingly. Compensation deters through prices, restores victims automatically, and requires no panopticon, because it needs to know who caused harm, not who might be thinking about it. It buys more safety per unit of freedom, which moves the achievable equilibrium — less residual harm and less enforcement intrusion, at the same time. The measure of a justice system was never the fullness of its prisons. It is how much of the broken world gets repaired — and by that measure, the system we have is not too harsh to keep. It is too wasteful.
