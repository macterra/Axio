Upgrading Liberty

From the harm principle to agency

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

A factory dumps solvent into a river and a thousand wells sour downstream. A con man walks away with a widow’s savings without ever raising his voice. A border guard turns back a family whose only offense is being born on the wrong side of a line, in the name of protecting the people on the right side of it. Each case poses the same question — when may we coerce? — and the liberal tradition’s most celebrated answer handles none of them cleanly. This chapter traces that answer through two upgrades: from Mill’s harm principle to the libertarian Non-Aggression Principle, and from there to a principle that finally says what the first two only gestured at.

Mill’s Sentence

The liberal tradition has run for a century and a half on a single sentence. John Stuart Mill, On Liberty, 1859: the only legitimate reason for exercising power over an individual, against their will, is to prevent harm to others.

Everything admirable in classical liberalism is compressed into that sentence, and it is worth pausing on how much Mill got right. He saw that liberty is threatened on two fronts, not one: by the state, whose power can oppress even when democratically granted, and by society itself — the tyranny of the majority, which enforces conformity through shaming and ostracism without ever passing a law. He drew the crucial line between self-regarding actions, whose consequences fall on the actor and which should remain entirely free from interference no matter how unwise, and other-regarding actions, which risk harm to others and may be restricted. He insisted that mere offense, moral disapproval, and majority distaste never meet the threshold for coercion — harm must be concrete, demonstrable, and non-trivial. And he placed the burden of proof permanently on whoever would curtail liberty: personal sovereignty is the rule, coercion the rare exception that must justify itself. (Mill’s other great argument, that free discussion is the engine of truth-seeking, belongs to a later chapter — The Listener’s Right — where it gets the modern restatement it deserves.)

All of this survives into the framework I will defend. What does not survive is the sentence’s foundation, because the sentence has a hole in the middle of it. Harm is the load-bearing term, and Mill never defines it. He tells us what harm is not — not offense, not disapproval — and trusts the reader to recognize what it is. For a nineteenth-century readership that may have been safe. It is not safe now. A century and a half of moral inflation has poured through the hole: hurt feelings claimed as injury, disagreement as violence, the sight of someone else’s prosperity as a wound. Whoever gets their grievance classified as harm acquires a license to coerce, so the classification is contested by everyone with a grievance — and the harm principle, which was built to be liberty’s shield, becomes a blank warrant with “harm” written where the limits should be. A principle is only as sharp as its central term, and Mill left his undefined.

The Non-Aggression Principle

Libertarianism’s repair attempt was the Non-Aggression Principle: initiating aggression against another’s person or property is inherently illegitimate, and coercion is justified only in response to such aggression. The NAP has the virtue of its simplicity — it draws a bright line and puts defense on one side and predation on the other. But inspect it closely and it inherits Mill’s problem plus two of its own.

First, aggression is as ambiguous as harm ever was. Is fraud aggression? No one is touched. Is blackmail? Psychological manipulation? Pollution drifting across a property line? The principle delivers confident verdicts exactly where no one needed them — assault, robbery — and falls silent on every case actually in dispute.

Second, the principle is circular. Aggression means, on examination, illegitimate force — nobody counts the repossession of stolen goods as aggression. But which force is illegitimate is precisely what the principle was supposed to tell us. Coercion is justified in response to aggression; aggression is whatever coercion we have already decided is unjustified. The definition chases its own tail, and there is no foundation at the bottom of the loop.

Third, the NAP quietly consumes a theory it cannot supply. “Person or property” — but whether fencing a field is aggression or homesteading depends entirely on whose field it is, which means the principle presupposes settled, legitimate property rights before it can classify a single act. Property rights themselves need justification; the NAP borrows them on credit and never pays. A principle that assumes its hardest question is not a foundation. It is a conclusion wearing a foundation’s clothes.

The Agency Protection Principle

Both principles fail the same way: they are rules about coercion built on an undefined or circular central term. The repair is to ground the rule in the thing actually at stake. What makes fraud, assault, threats, and manipulation wrong is not that they breach a definition — it is what they do to their victim, and what they do is the same thing in every case: they contract the victim’s capacity to make and act on voluntary choices. The value at the bottom of the liberal tradition, the thing the harm principle and the NAP were both groping toward, is agency.

So state the rule in terms of it. I call this the Agency Protection Principle:

Coercion is justified if, and only if, it prevents or remedies violations of voluntary agency.

The terms here are not left to intuition; they are defined and defended in Volume 5, and I will use them as defined. Coercion is the credible threat of actual harm to gain compliance — four elements, each load-bearing, developed in What Counts as Coercion. Harm is the non-consensual contraction of an agent’s reachable futures — enforced impossibility, not mere difficulty — the definition built and stress-tested in What Counts as Harm. With those definitions plugged in, the APP repairs each of the three defects in turn.

The ambiguity dissolves, because agency violations share an identifiable common feature where “aggression” had only a family resemblance. Fraud closes off the futures the victim would have chosen under true information; a credible threat poisons every branch but compliance; violence forecloses futures bodily. The hard cases stop being hard in principle: the question is always whether someone’s reachable futures were non-consensually contracted, and that is a question about the world, not about a word.

The circularity breaks, because the principle no longer defines justified coercion in terms of a synonym for unjustified coercion. It bottoms out in agency — a value with independent content, the capacity that makes valuing and choosing possible at all.

And the dependence on presupposed property rights inverts. The APP does not assume ownership; it explains what ownership is for. Property, consent, and contract are ethical when and because they protect voluntary agency — a claim I cash out fully in Rights Are Forged — and Reciprocal. Rights come out of the framework rather than being smuggled in beneath it.

Notice, too, that the principle is protective and remedial, not merely retaliatory. It licenses coercion to prevent a violation — intercepting the kidnapper, freezing the fraud in progress — and to remedy one, forcing the thief to make his victim whole. This is not a new permissiveness; it is exactly the shape of the three justifications defended in The Boundaries of Force: pre-consented coercion operates within agency, defensive coercion protects it, compensatory coercion restores it. In every justified case, coercion is in the service of agency. The APP is that conclusion promoted to a principle.

From Liberty to Agency

The upgrade does more than repair definitions. It replaces liberty with agency as the fundamental political goal, and the replacement matters.

Agency is the capacity to intentionally choose and pursue one’s own goals. Liberty — freedom from coercion — is essential to it, but not exhaustive of it. Agency has two further components: capability, the physical, cognitive, and material wherewithal to execute meaningful choices, and opportunity, access to choices worth making. A man subject to no coercion whatsoever, but destitute, illiterate, and optionless, is free in the thin sense and impotent in every sense that matters. Liberty is a component of agency; agency is the point.

Making agency the goal does not soften the framework — it makes the framework’s trade-offs explicit instead of masking them. Pure liberty-maximization runs aground the moment any legitimate societal end requires constraint, and its partisans are forced to smuggle the trade-offs in through the definitions. Agency puts them on the table. Interventions can be compared by their net effect: agency gained minus agency lost, with an intervention favored when \(\Delta(\text{Net Agency}) > 0\) — and with the agency destroyed by the intervention’s own coercion always on the ledger, counted first.

Run the cases from the opening. The factory’s effluent non-consensually contracts the reachable futures of everyone downstream — a genuine agency violation, which is why environmental regulation can be justified within this framework where a naive libertarianism must pretend the question away. The con man violated the widow’s agency as surely as a mugger, though he never touched her — fraud closes futures through misinformation, and the APP licenses both stopping him and forcing restitution. And the border guard? Turning back a peaceful family contracts their reachable futures enormously — access to better lives, foreclosed by force — while the migrants themselves have violated no one’s agency by walking. Most immigration restrictions fail this framework’s test at the threshold, an argument Borders and Movement carries through in full. Coercive redistribution fares no better as a general matter: the coercion is a certain, immediate agency loss, and the promised gains rarely survive honest accounting — Part V of this volume is the long version.

The Constraint and the Comparator

I have now stated two rules, and the reader keeping score will notice they are not the same rule. The APP is deontic: coercion is justified if and only if it prevents or remedies violations of voluntary agency — triggered by a violation, silent otherwise. The net-agency test is aggregative: it compares totals, and taken alone it could license coercing people who have violated no one, whenever the arithmetic came out positive. Taken alone, in other words, the comparator is the greater-good machine — the one move The Boundaries of Force excludes absolutely, because a rule that lets sufficiently large benefits purchase the violation of any agent has no boundaries at all, only a price list. Conversely, the APP alone is a gate without a decision procedure: many different interventions can pass it, and the gate cannot rank them, size them, or say when to stop.

So let me state, once and precisely, how the two rules compose. The APP is the constraint on when coercion may be used at all: no violation of voluntary agency to prevent or remedy, no license — however attractive the sums. Net agency is the comparator among interventions the APP has licensed: of the responses that pass the gate, choose the one that preserves the most agency, and if every licensed response destroys more agency than it protects, choose none. The gate comes first and is never overridden by the ledger; the ledger disciplines whatever discretion the gate leaves open.

The composition does real work. The factory case passes the gate — pollution is a violation — and the comparator then adjudicates among responses: outright ban, effluent standard, liability rule, each scored by the agency it protects downstream against the agency it costs upstream. The border case fails at the gate before any arithmetic begins: the peaceful migrant has violated nobody, so there is nothing for coercion against her to prevent or remedy, and no projected benefit to the settled population can buy a license the constraint refuses to issue. That is the shape of the framework everywhere: deontic where it must be, consequentialist only inside the fence.

And even coercion that passes both tests is not thereby blessed. It is provisionally justified, under conditions — specific, proportional, evidence-bound, reversible — with the burden rising as the stakes do. Those conditions are the subject of the Grey Zone, which is where this volume’s account of legitimate coercion is completed; nothing in this chapter is a blank check.

Objections

Four objections arrive predictably, and they deserve answers.

This is utilitarianism in disguise. No — the gate is precisely the component utilitarianism lacks. Utility aggregates across persons without limit; the APP forbids coercing non-violators before any calculation begins, so no accumulation of benefits to others can justify sacrificing an innocent’s autonomy. And the currency differs: agency is measured in capabilities and open futures, not in preference-satisfaction or felt happiness, and the framework books coercion itself as harm on its own ledger. A calculus that counts the cost of its own instrument, and is fenced by a deontic constraint besides, is not the calculus Bentham built.

It ignores moral rights. It explains them. Rights are not free-floating absolutes discovered in nature; a right is a defensible boundary around agency whose justifying principle survives reciprocal application — the account developed in Rights Are Forged — and Reciprocal, from which the classical list of body, property, speech, and association falls out as the boundaries agency needs to survive conflict. Nor can rights be prior to a theory of justified coercion, because enforcing a right is coercion: “you have a right to your property” cashes out as a standing threat against whoever would take it, and that threat needs exactly the license this principle supplies. Within the framework no right is metaphysically absolute, but the burden on overriding one is so severe that the practical difference from absolutism is vanishingly small — and unlike absolutism, the framework can say why the burden exists.

Agency cannot be quantified, so the rigor is fake. The framework needs direction, not decimals. Whether an intervention opens more futures than it forecloses is usually answerable ordinally even when no cardinal measure exists — nobody needs a unit of agency to see that quarantining a carrier of a lethal pathogen protects more choosing than it suspends, or that jailing a dissident does not. Where the direction is genuinely unclear, the tie does not go to the coercer: the default is liberty, the burden of proof sits where Mill left it, and uncertainty is an argument for staying the state’s hand, not for forcing it.

It enables authoritarian overreach — any tyrant can promise long-term agency gains. The tyrant faces three walls. Coercion is by definition agency loss, so every scheme starts in deficit, its costs concrete and immediate, its promised harvests speculative — and the framework discounts speculation against certainty. The gate bars coercing non-violators outright, whatever is promised, and authoritarian projects are built almost entirely of coercing non-violators. And whatever passes must satisfy the Grey Zone’s conditions — specificity, proportionality, evidence, reversibility, containment — which are engineered precisely to strip vague greater-good rhetoric of its purchase. A framework in which coercion always costs, non-violators are never fair targets, and justifications expire is the worst tool an authoritarian could pick up. That is not an accident of the design. It is the design.

What survives of Mill is what deserved to survive: the individual sovereign by default, the coercer forever on trial, offense and disapproval never sufficient grounds. What has been upgraded is everything the sentence left blank. Harm now has a definition, coercion has a definition, the principle has a foundation that neither chases its tail nor borrows property rights on credit — and liberty itself has been located inside the larger thing it was always serving. The goal of politics is not merely that no one be coerced. It is that everyone can choose.
