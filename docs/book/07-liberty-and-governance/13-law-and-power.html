Law and Power

Who the rules actually bind

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

In September 2025, a missile fired by U.S. forces struck a small boat off the coast of Trinidad. The vessel was suspected of carrying drugs. Commanders watched it burn on a live drone feed, and as the smoke cleared they saw something they had not planned for: two survivors clinging to the smoldering wreck. The Special Operations commander overseeing the attack ordered a second strike — reportedly to comply with instructions from the Secretary of Defense — and the two men were killed in the water.

I want to state the legal situation plainly, because it is not complicated. There is no plausible legal theory, domestic or international, under which either strike was lawful. The men were not combatants in any armed conflict. They had been convicted of nothing, charged with nothing, warned of nothing. Suspicion of smuggling is not a capital offense in any legal system on Earth, and even if it were, suspicion is not a verdict. These were intentional killings of civilians outside any war — and the second strike, against helpless survivors, is the kind of act the laws of war were specifically written to prohibit.

Conduct like that should trigger investigations, hearings, and formal charges. Instead it triggered nothing. The event dropped out of public consciousness within days. Institutions fell silent. The machinery of enforcement never engaged — not because the facts were hidden, but because everyone who might have engaged it understood, without needing to be told, that it would go nowhere.

There is a comforting civic mantra meant to cover moments like this: no one is above the law. It is repeated with the same ritual confidence as justice is blind and the rules apply equally to all. But these are not descriptions of the world. They are aspirational myths — moral architecture deployed to obscure a far simpler truth: law binds only where power cannot override it.

How the Strong Slip the Net

The previous chapter divided law by its function — conflict resolution versus conflict manufacture. This chapter asks a different question: whatever a law’s function, whom does it actually bind?

Legal systems depend on something deeper than written rules: the capacity to impose consequences. A statute with no enforcement behind it is a press release. When an actor’s reach extends beyond the system’s ability to respond, the rules lose operational force — not repealed, not amended, just dead at the point of application.

The United States sits precisely in that zone. It holds veto power over the global institutions that would judge it. It refuses the jurisdictions that might hold it accountable. It classifies the evidence that would be needed to test its actions. And even the allies who object privately will not challenge it openly, because they depend on American security guarantees and economic weight. The point is not that the rules are unclear. The rules governing the Trinidad strikes are among the clearest in international law. The point is that no institution has the leverage to enforce them upward.

Run the counterfactual and the structure becomes explicit. If a small state had carried out the identical attack — a missile strike on civilians in international waters, followed by a second strike on the survivors — the legal process would move predictably: condemnations, resolutions, sanctions, possibly a referral to an international court. The conduct is the same in both cases. Only the identity of the actor changes. And the identity of the actor, not the conduct, determines the legal consequence.

This exposes the uncomfortable fact the mantra exists to conceal: the global order treats law as a gravity field that weakens with altitude. Small and medium powers orbit within it. Hegemons drift beyond its pull. Once you see this, the silence surrounding the strikes stops being surprising. It is not a scandal in the system; it is the expected output of a system calibrated to control most actors while leaving a small number functionally exempt.

What Law Actually Is

Axio reads law as a coordination technology, not a moral guarantee. It works when agents stand in roughly balanced positions relative to one another — when each party’s capacity to impose costs on the others is sufficient to make defection expensive. Under those conditions, written rules do real work: they let agents predict one another, settle disputes without violence, and plan across time. The rules are load-bearing because the balance of power behind them is load-bearing.

When one actor becomes capable of projecting force without fear of reprisal, the coordination collapses at that point. The rule-set remains written; the enforcement circuits are dead. Under this lens, a hegemon does not violate the law in the naive sense — it operates outside the law’s effective domain. The distinction matters. A violator is inside the system, gambling against enforcement. An actor outside the effective domain faces no gamble at all, because there is nothing on the other side of the table. This is the same asymmetry by which coercion beats intelligence: where overwhelming force is available, the argument — legal or otherwise — is simply over.

And this is not an anomaly or a corruption of the current order. It is how the current order is built. The postwar institutions were designed by the powers they would nominally constrain, and the design shows.

Rather than treating impunity as an occasion for moral outrage — though outrage is warranted — I treat it as a diagnostic. Impunity is information. It reveals where enforcement capacity stops. It reveals where coercive authority has escaped feedback. It reveals where institutional constraints are too weak to shape behavior, and where future harms will accumulate because no corrective force exists to check them. The lesson is not merely that the world is unjust, though it is. The lesson is that our political architecture concentrates power faster than it distributes responsibility — and the gap between the two is exactly where events like the Trinidad strikes happen. The epistemic dimension of that event — what it means for a state to treat an accusation as a warrant for execution — is taken up in the next chapter. Here the point is structural: whatever the epistemics, no process existed that could have imposed a consequence.

So the phrase “above the law” turns out to denote something more precise than privilege. It denotes a structural position: a point in the political landscape where reciprocal constraints cannot reach. The Trinidad strikes make that topology visible — an actor with overwhelming reach, acting in a space where no mechanism can impose consequences, before an audience that has stopped expecting one.

The Tail That Wags the Dog

If the top of the power distribution escapes the law, it is worth asking who the law is actually written for. The answer is another uncomfortable one: much of it is not written for you either. It is written for someone else — the small slice of the population whose behavior generates outsized risks, costs, and disruptions. In statistics, they are the tail of the distribution. In civic life, they drive policy far more than the median citizen ever will.

Anyone who attended a public school has seen the dynamic up close. Entire disciplinary systems get built around a handful of chronically disruptive students. Teachers enforce rules not because the average kid needs them but because a few do — and without those rules the environment collapses. This is not a moral judgment about the few; it is an engineering fact about order: you design for the most likely points of failure.

The pattern scales. Zoning law exists in part to keep a few high-risk uses of property away from everyone else’s homes. Large swaths of the criminal code, the safety regulations, and the social spending exist to contain, manage, or offset costs created by a small minority. Call it the Tail-Risk Principle: in many domains, a small proportion of actors creates a disproportionate share of the problems — a manifestation of the Pareto distribution, not a conspiracy — and because the harm they can cause is non-trivial, the system calibrates itself to contain them. Seat-belt laws, fire codes, and drunk-driving enforcement apply universally because a few people would otherwise take risks that endanger many. Safety nets exist because some fraction of the population will fall into chronic need. The compliant majority lives under rules sized for the non-compliant few.

The Oversimplification Trap

Where this analysis gets distorted is in the rhetoric built on top of it. A popular cynical claim holds that all of society’s costs and restrictions trace to the lowest-performing twenty percent — that the bottom quintile is the reason you can’t have nice things. The framing is rhetorically potent because it compresses a complicated dynamic into a single villain. It is also wrong, on two independent grounds.

First, the bottom quintile is not a monolith, and it is not where the largest harms live. Most people in the bottom fifth of any performance distribution cause no unusual burden to anyone; they are poor, or slow, or unlucky, not destructive. Meanwhile some people far above that quintile cause enormous harm — white-collar fraud, political corruption, environmental destruction — from positions the bottom quintile could never occupy. The tail that matters is the tail of harm caused, and it does not coincide with the tail of income or test scores.

Second, many constraints have nothing to do with bad actors of any kind. Traffic drives on one side of the road because coordination requires a convention, not because anyone misbehaved. Shared infrastructure, standard weights and measures, and genuine public goods generate rules whose purpose is coordination, not containment. Attributing every restriction to the worst-behaved is not analysis; it is scapegoating with a statistical accent.

And once you correct the first error, the fuller picture snaps into focus. Look at the top tail. Elite-driven crises — financial collapses, corporate fraud at scale, large-scale environmental damage — routinely impose costs exceeding those of street-level crime and petty disorder combined. And the regulatory response to elite catastrophe is just as universal as the response to street crime: the compliance regimes built after each financial crisis bind every small business and every household, not just the institutions that caused the collapse. The tail that wags the dog comes in more than one shape. Policy evolves in response to the extremes at both ends of the spectrum.

The Symmetry

Put the two halves of this chapter side by side and the finding states itself.

The bottom tail generates the rules; the top tail escapes them. The chronic offender at the bottom of the distribution is the design case for the criminal code, and the code lands on him with full force — he lives at the bottom of the gravity field, where law’s pull is strongest. The elite malefactor at the top of the distribution generates rules too — whole regulatory architectures are erected in the wake of elite catastrophe — but the pull weakens with altitude, and at the very top, where states and hegemons act, it approaches zero. The Trinidad strikes and the seat-belt ticket are governed by the same field equation.

And the compliant middle pays twice. It surrenders freedom to rules sized for the bottom tail — the safety instructions on products it would never misuse, the licensing regimes, the criminal statutes it will never trigger. And it absorbs the harms of the top tail — the bailouts, the poisoned rivers, the wars of choice — precisely because the actors responsible sit above the enforcement ceiling. The majority is governed by the worst among us at one end and unprotected from the worst among us at the other.

Explains, Not Justifies

I want to be careful here, because the Tail-Risk Principle is genuinely explanatory and it is tempting to let it slide into justification. It does explain why universal rules exist: designing for the tail is built into the mathematics of risk, and a system that designed only for the median would be a system that collapsed at its most likely points of failure. Any honest account of governance has to concede that much.

But explanation is not justification. That a universal rule responds to a real tail risk does not establish that this rule, enforced this way, on everyone, is a legitimate use of coercion. Every universal mandate backed by penalties is still coercion, and it still owes the full accounting that the Grey Zone demands: a specified violation or risk, evidence proportionate to the imposition, the least restrictive means that actually addresses the tail, reversibility, and containment of the enforcing institution itself. Some tail-driven rules pass that test — a rule against drunk driving tracks a real, lethal, measurable risk imposed on others. Many do not; they persist because “designed for the tail” makes a permanent excuse, and because the agencies built to contain last generation’s tail need next generation’s rules to justify their budgets. The Tail-Risk Principle tells you why the pressure for universal rules is constant. It does not tell you which rules survive scrutiny. That has to be settled case by case, and the burden stays where it always was: on the coercer.

So the real civic question is not whether to design for the tail — we have no choice about that. The question is how much the behavior of the least compliant should be allowed to set the rules for everyone else, and at what cost in freedom, efficiency, and shared space. Should we all be governed by the worst among us, or should we accept more risk to give the rest more room to breathe? Understanding that this is the live question changes how you read the news, how you vote, and how you react to yet another warning label. It explains why politics so often feels like it was built for someone else’s problems. It was.

Constraint by Architecture

That question has answers at the bottom of the distribution — calibration, proportionality, the grey-zone discipline. The top of the distribution is harder, because the problem there is not a badly calibrated rule but the absence of any enforcement at all. And for that problem, moral appeal is worthless. Telling a hegemon that no one is above the law is telling it something it already knows to be false. Editorials do not create enforcement capacity. Norms bind only those who can be made to keep them.

The remedy, if there is one, is architectural: political systems built so that no concentration of power can detach itself from consequence — where reciprocal constraint operates as an inevitability rather than an aspiration, engineered into the structure the way conservation laws are built into physics rather than requested of participants. What such an architecture would look like — which political configurations keep every actor inside the field where consequences reach, and what governance looks like when constraint is a property of the system rather than a promise of the powerful — is the question the last part of this volume takes up, in The Admissible Region and Axiocracy. The Trinidad strikes tell us what happens in the meantime: wherever power outruns reciprocity, the law is exactly as strong as the powerful want it to be, and two men clinging to wreckage have no appeal.
