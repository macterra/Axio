Beyond Clown World

Meaning without absolutes

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

The complaint is now a genre. A culture that has lost its absolutes, the argument runs, has descended into farce — a clown world — where nothing is sacred, every value is a joke, and the only honest response is either despair or a sneer. The diagnosis usually comes packaged with a cure: that meaning can be redeemed only by a story about God, that without divine authority significance must vanish into absurdity, and that the choice before us is between inherited absolutes and a clownish void.

The diagnosis is not wrong about the symptom. The cure is wrong about everything else. It confuses the collapse of objective morality with the collapse of meaning itself, as if the two were the same thing wearing different hats. They are not. That confusion — between a value’s loss of cosmic backing and its loss of force — is the whole error, and it is worth taking apart slowly, because almost everyone who reaches for the return-to-God cure has already made it without noticing.

The Symptom Is Real

Start by granting what deserves to be granted. Something has curdled. The nihilism the complaint points to exists, and it shows up in mirror-image forms across the political spectrum.

On the left it takes the shape of perpetual deconstruction: every value unmasked as complicity in oppression, every commitment exposed as power in disguise, until the unmasking is the only remaining activity and there is nothing left it would refuse to unmask. On the right it takes the shape of ironic detachment — lulz culture, the shrug, a shallow Nietzschean posture of will-to-power that treats sincerity itself as the mark of a sucker. The two look like enemies. They are the same vacuum from opposite sides: a space where older certainties once stood and nothing has been built to replace them.

So the people diagnosing clown world are seeing something. The mistake is not in the seeing. It is in the inference they draw from it — that because the old absolutes are gone, and because their departure hurts, the only repair is to bring the absolutes back.

The Error: Contingency Is Not Collapse

Here is the move to refuse. To recognize that values are contingent — that they depend on culture, history, biology, the kind of creatures we happen to be — is not to abolish them. It is to see them clearly. Contingency and collapse are being run together, and they are not the same.

A value does not stop mattering when you learn where it came from. My commitment to honesty does not evaporate when I understand it as historically and biologically shaped rather than decreed from outside the universe. Reflective ownership need not reject inheritance: I can examine, reaffirm, revise, and become answerable for a value first learned from others. The source does not settle the commitment’s worth, and a naturalistic genealogy does not dissolve it.

The clown-world dichotomy — God-given morality or clownish void — is simply too crude to hold the actual options. It offers two boxes and insists you pick one. But the recognition of contingency does not put you in the void box. It opens a third box the dichotomy pretended did not exist. Losing the illusion that meaning comes from outside is not the same as losing meaning. It is the beginning of making meaning honestly.

Conditionalism: The Harder Truth

The name for one part of this recognition, in this book, is Conditionalism, developed as a theory of interpreted truth claims in an earlier volume. Values add a separate input: a valuer or normative premise. Meanings add agents and practices that construct or sustain them. The same discipline of exposing conditions runs through all three registers without making them the same kind of thing:

  • Truths are conditional: if these axioms, then this theorem; if these initial conditions, then this outcome.
  • Values are agent-relative: a terminal commitment is supplied by valuers, while facts constrain what advances it; if we desire flourishing, cooperation may matter under stated social conditions, and if we commit to reciprocal authorship, honesty and restraint can acquire force under the framework’s rules.
  • Meaning is conditional: if I care about this life, then these actions matter, and matter enough to organize a life around.

This looks at first like a demotion, and the clown-world reflex reads it as one — as though “conditional” were a synonym for “flimsy.” It is the opposite. Conditionalism is not a counsel of despair but a counsel of responsibility. Once you see that meaning is conditional on what you care about, you can no longer outsource the caring. There is no cosmic order that will do it for you, no tablet handed down that settles the question in advance. The significance of your life is grounded in your agency or it is grounded nowhere.

Which yields the line this chapter is built around, and the one I want carried out of it: we do not simply inherit significance; we must choose it, construct it, and defend it. Each verb is doing work. Choose — because no frame selects itself; the frame is the thing at stake. Construct — because a meaning is built and maintained, not found lying in the road. Defend — because a contingent value has no automatic protection and will be eroded by entropy, fashion, and rival frames unless someone holds the line. Received unexamined, an inheritance asks nothing of you; taken up — endorsed, reworked, defended — it asks everything, which is exactly why owned significance means something where significance merely obeyed produces only obedience.

Phosphorism: Affirming Chosen Values

Conditionalism clears the ground; it does not tell you what to build. It exposes conditions without selecting which values are worth holding. The clown-world argument assumes that once values lack cosmic certification, commitment must be arbitrary. This does not follow. Chosen commitments are shaped by causes and reasons and may be deeply costly to revise; the relevant contrast is reflective endorsement versus authority claimed solely from an external source. The positive framework developed in Volume 5 is Phosphorism, which I will cite rather than reconstruct.

Phosphorism is the conscious affirmation of a definite set of values — life, intelligence, complexity, authenticity — carried forward not as eternal decrees but as deliberate, revisable commitments. I value them because of what they make possible, what their absence costs, and how they relate to the agents and world I encounter. Those reasons eventually depend on terminal commitments rather than a cosmic fact. Calling them mine records endorsement and answerability, not sole authorship of their origin.

So a third option comes into view. Not the void, and not divine command, but conscious affirmation: Conditionalism exposes the conditions, while Phosphorism declares which contingent values this author endorses. The Credo applies the same move by assigning protected priority to a disclosed cluster of commitments, and the ethics of affluence asks how chosen constraints can restore stakes where necessity has receded.

The Alignment Grid Re-Axed

There is a map that makes the geography of this argument visible, and it comes from an unlikely source: the alignment grid of Dungeons & Dragons. The game sorts characters on two axes — lawful to chaotic, good to evil — and the nine-box result turns out to be a surprisingly good diagram for philosophies of meaning, once the axes are relabeled.

Reinterpret them like this. The horizontal axis runs from objective purposes to subjective purposes — from the belief that meaning is inherent in reality, independent of any mind (Eternalism), to the belief that meaning is entirely a human construct or illusion (Nihilism). The vertical axis runs from higher purposes to mundane purposes — from the pursuit of transcendent, grand, idealistic goals (Mission) to the focus on practical, immediate, worldly ones (Materialism). Cross the two and you get nine positions:

Objective purposes Balanced Subjective purposes
Higher purposes Eternalist mission — the crusade; a transcendent destiny written into the cosmos Mission — transcendent, idealistic goals Nihilist mission — will-to-power; heroic self-assertion over an abyss
Balanced Eternalism — meaning fixed from outside, absolute and imposed True Neutral — Meaningness; meaning made by navigation Nihilism — no meaning at all; every value a joke
Mundane purposes Eternalist materialism — scientism; a mechanistic order that grants no purpose Materialism — practical, worldly, immediate goals Nihilist materialism — hedonism and consumption as the only honest ends

The four poles are the four ways to go wrong by committing to a single axis-end. The eternalist takes meaning to be fixed from outside and defends absolutes that no longer persuade — the clown-world cure, named on the map. The nihilist denies there is any meaning to defend — the clown-world symptom, named beside it. The zealot of Mission chases transcendence while despising the mundane conditions that sustain it; the materialist tends only to the mundane and lets the horizon go dark. Each pole is a real temptation, and each is incomplete in the same way: it mistakes one end of one axis for the whole territory.

True Neutral

The center of the grid is not a compromise between the poles, and this is the part that the diagram is most likely to be misread on. True Neutral is not the absence of commitment, not the lukewarm midpoint of someone who could not decide. It is the position that meaning arises from navigating between the extremes rather than from occupying any of them.

David Chapman’s Meaningness project is the natural occupant of that center, and it earns the spot by argument, not by splitting the difference. Chapman holds that eternalism and nihilism are both incomplete: eternalism imposes rigid absolute truths and ignores the fluid human contexts in which meaning actually lives; nihilism dismisses meaning altogether and ignores the significance humans inevitably and unavoidably create. Mission and materialism fail symmetrically — the first sacrifices the practical to the ideal, the second the ideal to the practical — and the repair in each case is not to pick a side but to integrate ideals and practicality continuously, as the situation demands.

That word continuously is the whole point. True Neutral, understood this way, is not a resting place. It is a discipline. It requires constant balance and vigilance, an active and agile response to shifting contexts rather than a static doctrine you can adopt once and stop thinking about. Meaning arises from skillful navigation, not from any fixed dogma — including the dogma of the center itself. The equilibrium is dynamic: you are always leaning against whichever pole is currently pulling hardest, and the moment you stop leaning you fall toward it. This reading of Chapman — meaning as the ongoing work of holding the middle against every extreme — is the seam this chapter shares with the fuller treatment in Integrating Meaningness, where the synthesis of participation and agency is worked out as a single loop.

Notice how exactly this maps onto the argument that got us here. Conditionalism is the refusal of the objective pole — the recognition that no meaning is fixed from outside. Phosphorism is the refusal of the subjective pole in its nihilist form — the insistence that chosen meaning is real meaning, affirmed and defended rather than dissolved. The third option and the True Neutral center are the same stance drawn on two different diagrams. To live at the center is to accept that values are conditional and then to affirm them anyway, holding the line against the eternalist’s false comfort on one side and the nihilist’s false honesty on the other.

The Freedom in It

So the clown-world complaint has its answer. The malaise it names is real; the void is genuinely there, and the memes resonate because they point at something true. But the solution is not a retreat into discredited absolutes, and it is not a surrender to the void either. It is to accept the conditional nature of meaning and to bear the responsibility of consciously choosing what to hold sacred — knowing that the holding is what makes it sacred, that nothing external will do the work, and that the work is never finished.

This is not nihilism. Nihilism is the claim that because meaning is not guaranteed from outside, there is none. What I am describing is the opposite claim: meaning can be inherited, encountered, revised, and made, then owned through participation and reflective endorsement. It is ours to sustain and defend against the erosion that comes for every unguarded value. That is not a loss. It is what freedom looks like when you stop asking the cosmos to live your life for you. It is an expression of agency, and there is more room for it beyond clown world than the old dichotomy allowed.
