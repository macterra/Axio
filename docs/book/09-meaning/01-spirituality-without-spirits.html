Spirituality Without Spirits

The word as living fossil

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

Say the word spiritual out loud and notice what it does. It carries a weight that no synonym quite matches. Call someone deep, reflective, reverent, awake — none of it lands the way spiritual lands. The word promises access to something beyond the ordinary: beyond the ego, beyond the daily grind, beyond the brute fact of being a body that eats and works and dies. And it makes that promise even in the mouths of many people who affirm no spirits at all. “Spiritual but not religious” names a recognizable modern identity, but it covers mixed metaphysical commitments rather than a uniform naturalism. That ambiguity is the clue: people can reach, unembarrassed, for a word rooted in vital breath while disagreeing about whether any invisible substance animates matter or survives death.

This is not sloppiness. It is a clue. A word survives for the same reason any pattern survives — it keeps earning its keep. When the literal referent of a term dies but the term does not, that tells you the word was doing a second job all along, one that outlived the ontology it came dressed in. To understand what “spiritual” is doing now, you have to trace how it got here: six stages, from a puff of air to a category of experience that a modern materialist still cannot do without.

From Breath to Ghost

Start with the etymology, because it is unusually revealing. Spiritual descends from the Latin spiritus: breath, wind, the moving air. Greek pneuma, Sanskrit prāṇa, and Hebrew ruach likewise join breath or wind to life and, in some uses, an animating principle. These traditions are not interchangeable, and their terms acquired different meanings. The recurring association nevertheless makes sense. Watch a living thing and a dead one side by side and one conspicuous difference is breath. Breath became a sign of being alive, and then a vehicle for thinking about whatever life is. At the root of the English word, spirit is closer to animating breath than to the fully developed immortal soul that later theology supplied.

The second stage is where the trouble and the richness both enter. If breath is what makes me move with purpose, then perhaps the other things that move with apparent purpose are breathing too — animated by some unseen agent of their own. The wind that bends the trees, the storm that drowns the crops, the fever that takes a child, the figure that visits in a dream: each behaves as if something wanted something. Humans are relentless agency-detectors, and the detector is tuned to fire on the slightest provocation, because over evolutionary time the cost of missing a real predator dwarfed the cost of flinching at a stick. So the machinery that evolved to model other minds gets pointed at the whole moving world, and animism is the result: forests, rivers, animals, diseases, and luck itself read as alive with spirits, each with intentions to be placated or courted. And once the world is full of invisible agents, a social role opens up — the shaman, the priest, the king who can speak to them on the tribe’s behalf. The agency detector does not just populate the world with spirits; it builds the first institutions around the people who claim to broker with them. This is the deep root the later volumes will keep returning to: the same over-firing detector that made the rivers into gods is still running, and much of culture — up to and including religion rebuilt later as a deliberate memetic engine — is what grows on top of it. Volume 8 traces that machinery — the hyper-agency detection and the memeplexes it feeds — in detail; here it is enough to mark where the reification starts.

The Capture and the Severance

The third stage is capture. As diffuse animism consolidated into organized religion, the vital breath was promoted. It stopped being the wind in the trees and became the immortal soul: a discrete, immaterial substance, seat of the self, detachable from the body, and — this is the decisive move — subject to judgment. To be spiritual, in the traditions that formed around this idea, was to align that soul with a divine order. In Christianity the spiritual man lives by the Holy Spirit against the promptings of mere flesh; in the dharmic traditions, spiritual practice is the discipline that works liberation from attachment and the cycle of rebirth. Whatever the local content, the structure is the same: spirituality becomes the path of salvation, the technology of the soul’s alignment with what is highest and most real. The word now names an entire apparatus of doctrine, ritual, law, and submission, and the loose animist breath has hardened into a metaphysical claim with an institution standing behind it.

The fourth stage is the severance, and it is the one modern readers think they live on the far side of. The Enlightenment broke the religious monopoly on explanation. Materialism and the sciences steadily removed invisible agents from many accounts of how the world works: infection replaced spirits in explanations of fever, pressure gradients displaced wills behind storms, and gravitation described the movements of the heavens with no one steering. A literal immaterial substance that animates matter and outlives it was not refuted in a single blow; it became unnecessary to more and more successful explanations. This volume adopts that naturalistic judgment rather than relitigating it. The case against holding beliefs cut loose from correction is made in full elsewhere. What matters here is whether the experiences once interpreted through spirit survive the change of explanation.

But here is what a simple disenchantment story gets wrong. Rejecting a supernatural interpretation does not eliminate the experiences and needs it organized: the pull toward something larger than the self, the sense that some moments and commitments exceed ordinary utility, the drive to connect to what is higher, deeper, or more enduring than one life. Religious and nonreligious people interpret that continuity differently. This volume asks whether a naturalistic account can house it through human capacities, practices, relationships, and choices without treating the prior metaphysics as part of the definition.

What the Word Now Names

So watch one modern use of the word once supernatural ontology is no longer shared. “Spirituality” can detach from a doctrine of spirits and name experiences or practices the doctrine once organized. By the twentieth century one can be spiritual without being religious, although many spiritual people retain supernatural beliefs. In the naturalistic usage developed here, the word names an orientation: the felt reach beyond the ordinary, the ego, or survival toward something treated as worthy of alignment.

And the striking thing is how many roads climb to it. One person finds the reach in prayer, another in meditation; one in art and one in psychedelics; one in philosophy and one — this is the case I care most about — in science, in the vertigo of actually grasping the scale and lawfulness of what is. These practices share no doctrine. The Buddhist on the cushion and the physicist at the board disagree about nearly everything they could put into a proposition. What unites them is not a belief held in common but a vector held in common: the attempt to connect to something larger, higher, or deeper than the transacting self. Spirituality, understood functionally, is the name of that vector — not a claim about what is out there, but a description of a direction the human animal keeps trying to face.

That functional reading is the load-bearing move of this book’s final volume, so let me state it plainly. I am not going to treat the sacred, the spiritual, or the meaningful as proof of anything supernatural, and I am not going to treat their naturalistic explanation as exposure of a fake. Both moves confuse experience with interpretation. One mistake takes the reality of the hunger as proof of a corresponding ghost. The other assumes that rejecting the ghost should embarrass the hunger into silence — as if a mistaken theory of bread made starvation unreal. The third position is the interesting one: take the experience as real, its interpretation as contestable, and ask what actually feeds it under the naturalistic account this volume adopts.

The Living Fossil

Which brings the genealogy to its sixth and final stage, and to the word itself. Biologists call a coelacanth or a horseshoe crab a living fossil: an organism whose form was laid down under conditions that no longer obtain, still swimming around long after the world that shaped it is gone. Spiritual is a living fossil of exactly this kind. Its form was set by animism — by the assumption that life runs on invisible breaths and that death releases a soul — and every time we use it, that dead assumption swims along in the shadow of the word. When we say a place or a piece of music or a night under the stars was spiritual, we mean it touched awe, reverence, significance, alignment, a sense of the larger. We do not mean we detected a ghost. But we reach for the ghost-word anyway, because the whole lineage I have just traced left no other term with the same aura. Awe is too thin, reverence too churchy, meaning too abstract, significance too clinical. Only spiritual carries, folded into a single word, the full sense of orientation toward what transcends the self. The animist shell persists because the secular content it now carries has never been given a shell of its own.

So the honest thing is to say exactly what the word is doing. On the reading offered here, it survives not because mysticism has proved a supernatural ontology but because the word remains useful: an inherited shell filled with secular transcendence. The spirit hypothesis is set aside; the vocabulary and the human capacities it organizes remain. Keep the hunger; question the haunting. That sentence is the program of this volume, and everything that follows is an attempt to make good on it — to show how the hunger can be fed without pretending its metaphysical interpretation arrived with the experience.

That is the arc from here to the last page. First, the sacred is reconstructed as a protected role in practical ordering — sometimes an apex, cluster, threshold, or conflict rule — requiring no god within this model; that is the secular sacred. Then comes practical wisdom: disciplines of attention and agency for people who do not take commandments as externally guaranteed. The constructive core argues that existential meaning is not simply inherited from ancestors or discovered in the cosmos but made — generated by valuing creatures who author their own significance, and that this is a liberation rather than a loss. Finally the Great Unfolding offers a disclosed secular meta-myth pointed at the deep future. The whole begins with a decision: preserve the hunger while keeping its metaphysical interpretation open to examination.
