Ultimate Beauty

Where the reasons run out

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

Take any choice you have ever made and interrogate it the way a child does — why? — and then do it again to the answer, and again. I eat this way because it keeps me healthy. Why do I want to be healthy? Because health gives me energy. Why do I want energy? Because it lets me do more, and live longer. Why do I want to live longer? At each step the justification points outward, toward some further thing the choice is for. This is instrumental reasoning, and it is most of what passes for practical thought: a chain of because-clauses, each link borrowing its warrant from the next.

But keep pulling the chain and something happens. The because-clauses run dry. I do not want to live longer for anything else; I want it directly. Press on health, on energy, on longevity, and eventually you strip away every layer of instrumentality and reach a value that is chosen for itself and for nothing beyond itself. Ask why of it and there is no next link to hand you. The question does not get a further answer. It gets a different kind of answer — or none at all.

The Credo of the previous chapter named four such things: agency, flourishing, authenticity, truth. Four pillars, affirmed against an indifferent cosmos, worth defending and worth orienting a life around. I declared them; I did not derive them. This chapter is about what holds them up — which is to say, about what is underneath the whole recursion of justification when it finally hits bottom. My claim is that the bottom is not logic and not ethics. It is aesthetics.

The Chain of Reasons and Its End

The regress of practical justification faces the same trilemma as the regress of belief. In Rationality Without Foundations I traced the epistemic version: every chain of grounds must either stop at something exempted from challenge, continue forever, or curve back into itself — dogma, infinite regress, or circularity, and all three look fatal. The practical chain has the same shape. Justify a choice by the goal it serves, justify that goal by a further goal, and you are building exactly the kind of tower that has no ground floor.

The epistemic escape, following Bartley, was to drop the demand for justification altogether: a belief is held rationally not because it rests on something secure but because it is held open to criticism. The regress does not terminate; it dissolves, because nothing was ever required to touch bottom. That works for beliefs, which answer to the world and can be tested against it. But values are not beliefs. A preference is not true or false, and no amount of exposure to evidence will tell me whether to want longevity for its own sake. So the practical regress cannot be dissolved the same way. It really does bottom out — and the interesting question is what it bottoms out in.

The honest answer is: in values that are self-justifying, meaning not that they justify themselves by argument but that they need no argument. They are recognized as good, fitting, or compelling in themselves. When the last because falls away, what remains is not a proof and not a duty. It is a recognition — this resonates, this fits, this is worth it — and that recognition is aesthetic.

Aesthetics as the Terminal Attractor

I mean aesthetics in its widest philosophical sense, not the narrow one that confines it to art and taste in pictures. Aesthetics is the faculty by which a pattern is felt to be right: the sense of coherence, elegance, fittingness, resonance. It is the capacity to recognize that something is worth choosing prior to and apart from any further reason for choosing it. This is not decision theory’s calculus and not logic’s rigor. It is something more primitive than either, and it is what orients both — the thing that hands reason its targets so that reason can go to work calculating how to reach them.

That aesthetics is not opposed to reason but under it, holding it up, is the point most easily missed. Reason is superb at means and mute about ends. It can tell you, with great precision, how to get what you want; it cannot tell you what to want, because every answer it offers is instrumental, another for the sake of that only defers the question. When the deferrals stop, what closes the account is a recognition of fittingness — you appeal, in the end, to harmony, to elegance, to beauty. Not as decoration on the argument but as its floor.

Philosophers have circled this without always naming it. Nietzsche located the root of values in taste rather than in reasoned deduction — one’s morality is, at bottom, a report of what one finds beautiful and what one finds contemptible. Wittgenstein held that what lies past the reach of propositions cannot be said but only shown: the value of the world, he thought, lies outside it, in a register that language points at and cannot capture. And Peirce, most explicitly, ranked aesthetics as the first of the normative sciences — prior to ethics and prior to logic, because before we can ask what is right to do or valid to infer, we must have some sense of what is admirable in itself, some conception of the ideal end that the good and the valid then serve. Three very different thinkers, arriving from three directions at the same floor. The recursion of justification ends in aesthetics because aesthetics alone can supply a terminus that is not a further reason — an anchor that answers the last why with a recognition instead of a receipt.

The Convergence of Vantages

What convinces me this is structural, and not a quirk of one framework, is that you reach the same terminus no matter which discipline you descend through. Change the vantage and the endpoint holds.

Approach it as decision theory and the chain bottoms out in a utility function. The theory is a magnificent machine for turning preferences into optimal actions, but it takes the preferences as given. Ask why the utility function assigns the values it does and the theory has nothing to say — those numbers are its inputs, not its outputs, and any attempt to justify them lands you back in circularity or in a further, ungrounded function.

Approach it as ethics and the chain bottoms out in moral intuitions — the immediate sense that cruelty is wrong, that a promise binds, that a life matters. These are recognized, not derived. Every attempt to derive them from something deeper either smuggles in another intuition or produces a system so counterintuitive that we reject it by appeal to the very intuitions it was meant to replace.

Approach it as agency — the bare fact of being a chooser — and the chain bottoms out in preference itself, the felt orientation without which there would be no choosing at all, only mechanism.

Three descents, one floor. Utility functions, moral intuitions, and raw preference are the same terminus seen through three windows, and in each case what sits at the base is a recognition that some pattern is to be pursued, holding its warrant in itself. This is why the scientist’s preference for an elegant theory, the mathematician’s pull toward a simple proof, and the engineer’s delight in a clean design are not ornamental asides to their work. They are the same terminal condition of choice, surfacing in domains we like to think of as purely rational. Beauty is doing load-bearing work in physics; it always was.

The Benign Circularity

The obvious objection is that grounding choice in aesthetics is arbitrary — that to say “I choose it because it is beautiful to me” is to say nothing, or to confess a mere whim. But look at the alternative. The alternative to a terminus is an infinite regress, a justification that never closes, and an agent who could not act until it closed would never act at all. To choose anything requires that the chain of reasons end somewhere, and wherever it ends, the ending will be a value held for its own sake, unsupported from below. The circularity of root values — the fact that they can be defended only by appeal to themselves and their kin — is not a defect peculiar to my framework. It is the necessary structure of any value system whatsoever. A hierarchy of values must have an apex, and the apex, by definition, has nothing above it to justify it.

This is the practical face of the position that the entire theory of value in this book rests on: that all value is subjective, that there is no objective moral order out in the world to read the correct values off of. If there were such an order, the regress could terminate in it — the last why would be answered by a fact about the universe. But there is no value without a valuer, no worth floating free of some agent who does the valuing. So the terminus was never going to be a fact. It was always going to be a recognition, located in an agent, resonating in an agent, and answerable to nothing outside that agent’s own sense of fittingness.

Naming this honestly is the whole point. The dishonest move — the move nearly every value system makes — is to pretend that a further reason waits below the floor: a commandment, a natural law, a proof of what any rational being must want. There is no such floor beneath the floor, and the search for it is the same incoherent demand that foundationalism made of knowledge and got nowhere with. What cannot be justified can still be revealed — I can bring my aesthetic recognitions into the light and look at them. It can be refined — I can interrogate a preference, challenge it against experience, discover it was cramped or borrowed or cruel, and expand it. It can be lived. What it cannot be is replaced with something deeper, because there is nothing deeper. As with belief, the terminus is a commitment and not a foundation: not a stopping point exempted from scrutiny, but a starting point I hold open, offered to reflection even though no reflection will ever hand me a reason beneath it.

Why the Pillars Shine

This is where the creed of the last chapter gets its grounding, and where the Phosphorism of Volume 5 shows what it was doing all along. Phosphorism chooses its values in the open — life over death, intelligence over ignorance, complexity over simplicity, flourishing over suffering, and above all authenticity, the value that governs how every other value is held. The recursion of justification finds its natural home there, because every one of those choices is an aesthetic recognition and not an instrumental one.

Life is not chosen because it purchases some further good; it is chosen because existence itself glows with intrinsic worth. Intelligence is chosen because clarity and understanding are beautiful. Complexity is chosen because emergent richness resonates. Flourishing is chosen because the blossoming of a potential into its fullness is elegant. Authenticity is chosen because coherence between what one is and what one does simply feels right — and that felt rightness is exactly why, in Volume 5, a hard moral choice made with integrity strikes us as beautiful and a genuinely beautiful choice carries moral weight. The two domains converge because they were always the same faculty. To live authentically is to live beautifully; existence becomes a work of art whose medium is choice. That is not a metaphor decorating the ethics. It is the ethics, seen from underneath.

So the four pillars of the Credo stand where they stand for the same reason. Agency, flourishing, authenticity, truth are not conclusions of an argument that could in principle have gone the other way if the premises had. They are recognitions — patterns that, when I look at them clearly, I find luminous and worth building a life around. I cannot prove them to someone who feels nothing before them, any more than I can prove a sunrise. I can only show them, and hope the showing lands. This is not the death of reason. Reason keeps every one of its jobs: it guides, it calculates, it optimizes, it tests my recognitions for coherence and catches the ones that contradict each other. What it cannot do, and was never able to do, is select its own goals. Only aesthetics does that. When the reasons run out — and they always run out — what remains is the plainest and most profound recognition an agent can have: this is beautiful, and so I choose it.
