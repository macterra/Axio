The Secular Sacred

The protected priority

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

Ask a secular person whether anything is sacred to them and you may get a small, embarrassed laugh — as if the question belonged to a museum. Push the same person on whether they would sell a vote, hand a child to a stranger for the right price, torture an innocent to spare themselves an inconvenience, or trade away their own capacity to think freely, and the laughter stops. There it is: something treated as not for sale, protected from ordinary bargaining, or permitted to outweigh a long list of competing goods. That is what I will call a sacred value. A person’s protected commitments may form one apex, a cluster, or a context-sensitive ordering rule; the important question is whether they know what performs that work.

The word sacred carries supernatural, ritual, social, and experiential meanings that no single structural model exhausts. This chapter isolates one of them: the protected role some commitments play in practical ordering. Religious traditions may understand that role through gods, holiness, covenant, or special places; a naturalist can study the role without accepting or refuting every associated metaphysical claim by definition. The result is a secular reconstruction of sacredness, not the discovery of what the sacred “always really was.”

The Sacred Is a Position, Not a Substance

Start from how values behave under pressure. You value your comfort, your reputation, your friendships, your projects, honesty, fairness, the wellbeing of people you have never met. Most of the time these coexist without incident. But values conflict, and when they do, something has to give. You cannot keep the promise and catch the flight; you cannot tell the whole truth and spare the feelings; you cannot maximize your own security and take the risk the cause requires. Such conflicts expose rankings, thresholds, and sometimes genuinely incommensurable commitments. What counts as sacred in this model is whatever an agent protects from ordinary trade-off or uses to govern those hard cases — whether one terminal value, a small cluster, or a rule for adjudicating among them.

This reframing does two things at once. It strips out the metaphysics — no supernatural property is being asserted, only a role in practical ordering — and it makes sacredness empirically examinable. A person may deny having a single highest value and be right: plural values can remain in tension, and judgment can be contextual. But actual choices still reveal what is protected, what is traded, and which rules settle a conflict. “I hold nothing sacred” is therefore a claim to test against practice, not a contradiction by definition.

The model helps explain one feature of desecration: violating a protected commitment can feel categorically different from frustrating a strong preference. To price what was treated as outside ordinary exchange changes its practical status and may threaten identity or group membership. That reaction can reveal a commitment’s role, but it does not prove the refusal is rational, good, or beyond review. Sacredness is a descriptive classification before it is an endorsement.

Once you look for protected commitments, the modern landscape comes into focus. Autonomy, equality, national identity, safety, and progress can each be sacralized in particular communities. The analogy concerns insulation from ordinary bargaining, identity, taboo, and the treatment of questions as threats. It does not make secular politics and religious holiness identical.

Three Accounts at Three Levels

The sacred can be studied from three distances, and three secular thinkers — one sociological, one phenomenological, one architectonic — each occupy one of them. They are not rivals. They are looking at the same object from social, experiential, and structural range, and the picture is only complete with all three.

The social level: sacredness as coalition glue. Robin Hanson’s account is the view from between people. Groups elevate certain values to sacred status because sacralization binds coalitions. When a value becomes sacred it stops being negotiable, and a value that cannot be negotiated cannot be used to split the group. Its function is not clarity but unity: it signals loyalty, suppresses internal bargaining, and manufactures a shared identity out of people who would otherwise haggle. The mechanism runs on abstraction. Sacred values are held in what Hanson calls far mode — kept deliberately vague, idealized, seen as if from a distance — precisely so that everyone can affirm them without colliding over the divisive specifics that near-mode detail would expose. “Freedom,” “justice,” and “the nation” command universal assent exactly to the degree that no one is forced to say what they concretely require.

This is where Hanson turns sharp, and the sharpness is worth keeping. The same vagueness that lets a sacred value bind a coalition makes its domain epistemically dangerous. Inside the halo, questioning is read as betrayal rather than inquiry; the demand for specifics is itself treated as a hostile act. Sacredness is a coordination technology — genuinely powerful, genuinely stabilizing — and for that very reason often corrosive to thought. Whatever we build on top of the sacred, we build knowing this: the apex position is where a value system does its most important reasoning and is least willing to be reasoned with.

The phenomenological level: sacredness as a mode of engagement. David Chapman’s account is the view from inside experience, and it will return in its mature form later in this volume. Chapman refuses the forced choice between sacredness as an objective property of things and sacredness as a merely subjective projection onto them. It is neither. It is interactive — a patterned relationship among people, objects, histories, and practices, real without being fixed, felt without being arbitrary. A mountain, a tree, a storm, a story, an old photograph becomes sacred not because a supernatural essence has taken up residence in it but because of how you encounter it: with wonder, with ritual, with sustained attention, with the accumulated weight of return. Sacredness on this reading is a mode you can enter, available in full to the thoroughgoing naturalist who does not believe the mountain contains a spirit and still stands before it with the hair standing up on the back of the neck. Deny the sacred at this level and you do not become free of illusion; you flatten your own experience and, characteristically, leave a vacuum that some cut-rate pseudo-religion rushes to fill.

The architectonic level: sacredness as the protected commitment that arbitrates. This is the view I have been building toward. It asks neither what the sacred does between people nor how it feels within a person, but what role it plays inside a system of values. In the simplest case, the sacred occupies the apex: it arbitrates conflicts beneath it and is itself traded only under extraordinary pressure. More complex agents may protect a cluster of values or use priority rules that leave some dilemmas unresolved. The model does not require every conflict to collapse onto a single scalar. Hanson’s coalition glue and Chapman’s mode of engagement can still be read at this level: coalitions protect commitments from bargaining, and experiences feel sacred when they touch what a person has placed outside the ordinary economy of exchange.

Naming the Apex

Locating a protected role is one thing. Deciding what belongs there is another. Commitments are normally inherited through family, practice, language, experience, and community before they can be examined. That origin does not make them alien or invalid. The authorship question is whether an agent can understand, endorse, revise, and become answerable for them rather than whether they arose without influence.

Axio does not stop at the general observation that something may occupy this protected role. It names a candidate: Sacred Coherence. That chapter argues that intelligible criticism already relies on enough consistency, stable reference, and responsiveness to reasons to keep disagreement from dissolving into equivocation. Coherence emerges there not as a mere preference but as the one commitment a mind cannot coherently abandon, since to argue against coherence is already to appeal to it. That self-dependence is a strong recommendation, not a deduction that every agent must rank coherence above every substantive value; nor does internal coherence guarantee truth or goodness. I choose the discipline because it keeps claims and commitments answerable without supernatural warrant, and I state it as a candidate precisely so that the apex remains open to near-mode inspection.

I state it as a candidate rather than a proof because the point of this chapter is prior to the choice. Whether or not you accept coherence as an apex, find the values and rules your choices protect, look at them in near mode, and decide whether they deserve the authority they exercise.

Why Pretending You Have None Blinds You

The practical payoff is epistemic hygiene. An agent may have no single non-negotiable apex, but almost everyone uses priorities, thresholds, identity commitments, or conflict rules that deserve inspection. Calling those roles sacred is optional; hiding them from criticism is not liberation.

Consider what each of the three levels contributes to the warning. Hanson shows that sacralization can be exploited: a value you hold sacred but refuse to inspect is the ideal handle for a movement to grab, because you have pre-committed to not asking the specific questions that would reveal what you are actually being enlisted to support. A hidden apex is a hierarchy left unlocked from the inside. This is not an abstract hazard; it is the ordinary mechanics of capture. Someone who holds “safety” sacred but has never looked hard at it can be walked, one non-negotiable step at a time, into surrendering freedoms they would have died to defend if the surrender had been framed in those words — because the framing that reaches an unexamined apex never uses those words. Someone who holds “justice” sacred in far mode signs up for concrete programs they have refused to inspect at near range, and discovers only later, if ever, what the abstraction was cashing out as. In every case the exploit works through the sacredness, not against it. The halo that forbids the specific question is doing exactly what it evolved to do, and someone else is steering. Chapman shows the cost on the other face: reject the sacred wholesale and you do not get clear sight, you get a flattened world plus a susceptibility to whatever pseudo-transcendence markets itself to the newly empty. And the architectonic account delivers the flat structural fact underneath both: the sacred is already organizing your choices, ranking your options, ending your internal arguments — right now, whether you have acknowledged it or not. The only thing in question is whether you get to see it work.

So the counsel is to know what you protect: bring the values, thresholds, and rules that adjudicate your life into near mode and ask whether they deserve their authority. A sacred commitment need not be epistemically frozen. It can be protected from ordinary trade-off while remaining open to extraordinary revision through evidence, conflict with other commitments, and encounters with other agents.

This is the movement the rest of the volume proposes for a disenchanted reader. Experiences of transcendence can survive rejection of one explanation; sacred roles can be reconstructed without supernatural properties; existential meaning can be sustained without a cosmic author. The secular move offered here is not denial but reinterpretation and reflective endorsement. It remains one proposal among religious and nonreligious accounts, and it is the ground the volume’s Credo chooses in the open.
