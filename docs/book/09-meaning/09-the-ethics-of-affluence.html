The Ethics of Affluence

Choosing worthy struggles

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

Infinite Jest is a nine-hundred-page account of what happens to people for whom survival has become trivial. David Foster Wallace set his novel in a near-future North America that has solved, for its comfortable classes, the ancient problems: famine, plague, cold, the daily uncertainty of whether the body will still be fed next winter. Every appetite has its dedicated technology of satisfaction. And the book’s inhabitants — tennis prodigies, recovering addicts, functionaries, entertainers — are not liberated by this. They are drowning. The novel’s engine is a piece of entertainment so perfectly pleasurable that anyone who watches it will watch it until they die, unwilling to stop for food or water. That is the parable in its purest form: the terminal point of a life in which every resistance has been engineered away. Not deprivation but its opposite, and the opposite turns out to be lethal.

I take this seriously as a diagnosis, not a mood. The claim underneath it is structural, and it is the hinge of this chapter: agency exists only in the tension between constraint and possibility. Choosing is an operation that requires something to push against. A decision that costs nothing, forecloses nothing, and could equally have gone the other way with no consequence is not really a decision; it is a twitch. What makes a choice a choice is that possibility is bounded — that some paths are open and others closed, that pursuing one thing means forgoing another, that the world resists. Remove the resistance entirely and you do not get more freedom. You get the form of freedom with the substance drained out, an endless menu on which nothing carries weight because nothing was ever at stake.

When Abundance Annihilates Agency

This is why affluence is dangerous in a way that is easy to miss. The danger does not announce itself. Affluence does not attack meaning; it dissolves the conditions under which meaning is generated. Scarcity, for all its cruelty, supplied structure for free. When survival is uncertain, the day organizes itself: there is food to secure, shelter to maintain, threats to answer, and the value of an hour is legible because the hour was scarce and the stakes were real. Strip that away and the scaffolding goes with it. What remains is a person with unlimited capacity to satisfy every want and no external reason to prefer any want to any other. Choice, in that condition, does not expand. It collapses — into compulsion, into the path of least resistance, into whatever appetite is loudest, because there is nothing left to hold appetite in check and no cost to indulging it.

Wallace’s addicts are the sharpest illustration, but the point generalizes past the pathological cases. The tennis academy runs on manufactured struggle precisely because its students would otherwise have none; the halfway house works because it reimposes constraint on people whose lives had none left. Everywhere in the novel, the characters who are surviving are the ones who have found or been given something hard to do. The ones who are dying are the ones for whom everything has become easy. That is not an accident of plot. It is the thesis.

I want to be careful here, because this argument is one border-crossing away from a reactionary sentimentality that I reject. It is tempting to conclude that suffering is good, that scarcity was secretly a gift, that we were better off poor and struggling. That conclusion is false and I will not defend it. Affluence is not a curse. It is an achievement — the hard-won conquest of scarcity, the single greatest thing our species has accomplished, the reason a sick child today survives what would have killed her in every prior century. To romanticize the constraint that affluence removed is to insult everyone who suffered under it and everyone who worked to lift it. The prosperity that produced this problem is worth defending against those who would tear it down, even as it produces problems of its own — the fertility collapse of rich societies is one such problem, the erosion of meaning is another.

So the argument is not against affluence. It is against a specific and passive relation to it.

Passive Affluence and Wielded Affluence

The distinction that does the work is between affluence absorbed and affluence wielded. Passive affluence is prosperity received and consumed — comfort without challenge, plenty without purpose, a life spent lowering oneself into the warm bath of satisfied appetite and staying there. This is the condition Wallace anatomizes, and it is the condition toward which abundance tends by default, because default is exactly the problem: when nothing external compels effort, effort has to be summoned from within, and passivity is what happens when it is not. Passive affluence is not a moral failing so much as a gravitational one. It is where you end up if you do nothing, and doing nothing is what abundance makes possible.

Wielded affluence is the alternative, and it is an activity, not a state. To wield affluence is to treat prosperity as what it actually is — not an endpoint but a capability, a store of freedom that can be spent — and then to spend it on the deliberate reintroduction of the friction that abundance removed. The affluent person cannot be handed a worthy struggle by circumstance, because circumstance has been tamed. So the worthy struggle has to be chosen. This is the whole of the ethical move: to convert the freedom-from that affluence provides into a freedom-to, and to aim that freedom at constraints one imposes on oneself.

Chosen constraint is the operative idea, and it is not a paradox. It is the ordinary structure of every serious human undertaking that survives contact with prosperity. The athlete submits to a training regimen no scarcity imposed. The scholar binds herself to a problem that could be abandoned at any moment without material consequence. The person who fasts, or keeps a vow, or takes on a dependent, or ships a piece of work to a public that can judge it — each is manufacturing stakes where the world had ceased to supply them. None of these is a deprivation in the sense that poverty is a deprivation, because the essential thing is that the constraint is authored. It is resistance one has chosen to push against, which means the pushing is an expression of agency rather than a submission to necessity. That authorship is the entire difference between a diet and a famine, between a vow and a prison.

The Worthy Struggles

If the prescription is to choose worthy struggles, the natural question is which ones. The sources name five, and they are less a checklist than a map of the directions in which chosen friction reliably regenerates meaning.

Discipline of body and mind. The deliberate imposition of a standard one must strain to meet — physical training, sustained study, the practice of a craft to the point where it costs something. Discipline is the most portable form of chosen constraint because it requires no external permission and no other people; it is available to anyone willing to make a demand of themselves that the world no longer makes.

Creation. The bringing-into-existence of something that did not exist before — a book, a company, a garden, a proof, a piece of music. Creation supplies stakes because the thing can fail, can be judged, can fall short of the vision that motivated it. The gap between what one intended and what one achieved is a form of resistance no affluence can smooth away, because it is generated by the work itself.

Service beyond the self. Commitment to a cause, a person, or a community whose flourishing is not identical to one’s own comfort. Service reintroduces constraint by binding one’s choices to something outside the perimeter of appetite — obligations that cannot be indulged away because someone else depends on them being met.

Truth-seeking. The pursuit of what is actually the case in the face of one’s own error, bias, and preference. This is a chosen struggle because affluence makes comfortable falsehood cheap; nothing forces the prosperous mind to correct itself, so the correction must be undertaken as a discipline, a standing willingness to be shown wrong.

Meaningful risk. The staking of something one values on an outcome one does not control. Risk is the purest antidote to the weightlessness of total safety, because it restores the one thing abundance most thoroughly abolishes: consequence. A choice under genuine risk is a choice that matters, because it could actually go wrong.

What these five share is that each rebuilds, from the inside, the structure that scarcity once imposed from the outside. Each is a way of ensuring that some paths remain closed, that some efforts cost something, that some outcomes are genuinely at stake — which is to say, each is a way of keeping agency alive in an environment engineered to make it unnecessary.

The Applied Case for Practical Wisdom

This chapter closes Part II, and it is meant to. The disciplines that the preceding chapters set out — the wisdom that must govern power if it is not to become mere force, the Stoic tools for holding one’s reactions under one’s own authority, the folk maxims read as disciplines of attention — can look, in isolation, like a museum of self-improvement techniques. The ethics of affluence is what they are for. Practical wisdom is not an ornament of the good life; it is the operational capacity to wield affluence rather than dissolve in it. Sagency is precisely the faculty that chooses worthy struggles well rather than badly, that distinguishes a constraint worth authoring from a merely painful one, that keeps the reintroduction of friction from curdling into masochism or spectacle. Without that faculty, the injunction to choose struggle is dangerous advice. With it, the injunction becomes the through-line of a life.

And this is where Part II hands off to Part III. To choose one’s constraints deliberately is already to have crossed a threshold — from meaning received to meaning made. The person who authors the resistance against which their agency defines itself is doing, in the small, exactly what the rest of this volume will describe in the large: constructing meaning by an act of will rather than waiting to inherit or discover it. The chosen struggle is the first and most concrete instance of a much larger move — the affirmation that replaces nihilism with construction, the deliberate authorship of value in a universe that hands us none by default. Affluence removed the last excuse for believing that meaning arrives from outside. What is left is the work of making it, and the freedom — bought by all that hard-won prosperity — to make it well.

The lesson is not to flee affluence, and not to drown in it. It is to wield it: to turn the conquest of scarcity into the freedom to choose worthy struggles, and so to make abundance the ground of flourishing rather than the solvent that dissolves it.
