Integrating Meaningness

Participation and agency as one loop

This chapter is a review — it is readable but still changing.

Three thinkers, working in three incompatible vocabularies, keep drawing the same picture. Jordan Peterson speaks in myth and sees Order menaced by Chaos. David Chapman speaks in Buddhist phenomenology and sees Form dissolving into Nebulosity. I speak in information theory and see Coherence built from, and decaying back into, Chaos. Set the three maps side by side and the temptation is to referee — to decide which one is right and retire the others. That is the wrong move, and understanding why it is wrong is the whole point of this chapter.

What Synthesis Means

The word synthesis has a history, and the history matters, because most people use it to mean compromise — split the difference, keep a little of each. That is not what it has meant in philosophy since Fichte gave the dialectic its familiar shorthand: thesis, antithesis, synthesis, the interplay of assertion, negation, and resolution. Hegel, who is usually credited with the triad, rarely used those words; his dialectic ran deeper, tracing how a position generates its own contradiction and how the tension resolves into a richer unity that could not have been stated at the start. The resolution is not a blend. It is a new altitude from which the earlier opposition looks like two views of one thing.

Ken Wilber gave the move its most useful name: transcend and include. A genuine synthesis does not average its predecessors and it does not discard them. It preserves what each got right, drops what each got wrong, and reframes both inside a larger structure that does work neither could do alone. Differentiate, negate, integrate. The insight of the earlier stage survives; its pretension to be the whole story does not.

This is the method I am going to apply to Peterson, Chapman, and my own framework — and, one turn further in, to Chapman and me. The claim is not that all three are secretly saying the identical thing in different words. It is that the polarity each of them names is real, that each idiom captures something the others miss, and that there is a level at which the apparent rivalry dissolves. Hold that standard in mind. A synthesis that merely flatters everyone has failed. A synthesis that lets you see why the disagreement arose, and what it was a disagreement about, has succeeded.

Three Maps of One Polarity

Start with the shared substrate. Beneath the three vocabularies lies a single ancient tension: between the known and the unknown, the stable and the fluid, the determinate and the indeterminate. Pattern emerging from flux, structure dissolving back into potential. Every framework worth taking seriously has had to say something about this rhythm, and these three say it in idioms that never quite touch.

Peterson’s is a mythic idiom. His Order is the known world — the articulated territory of meaning, the moral scaffolding of civilization, the logos made flesh. His Chaos is the unknown that surrounds and threatens it: the abyss, the creative substrate, the dragon at the edge of the map. To live well is to balance on the line between them, refusing both tyrannical rigidity and dissolution. What Peterson’s language encodes is value and danger — the emotional charge of standing at the frontier of the known.

Chapman’s is a phenomenological idiom, drawn from Buddhist practice. His Form names the temporary crystallizations of structure within experience; his Nebulosity — emptiness — names the inherent indeterminacy and interdependence of all things. The crucial claim is that everything has both aspects at once: what appears solid is in truth nebulous, and the wise stance holds both without collapsing either. Where Peterson mythologizes, Chapman deconstructs. Peterson’s Chaos threatens; Chapman’s Nebulosity liberates. Peterson’s Order demands reverence; Chapman’s Form invites play. What Chapman’s language encodes is perception and stance — how to see clearly and how to hold what you see.

Mine borrows an information-theoretic idiom, and within it the polarity becomes two complementary phases of a dynamic. Chaos names a reservoir of insufficiently modeled possibility; coherence names patterns that remain mutually compatible or re-identifiable across relevant transformations. This is an analogy across physical entropy, cognition, and culture, not one measured quantity common to all three. Acts of cognition, creation, and evolution can be described as selecting and stabilizing patterns from a wider field. What this language encodes is a proposed mechanics of emergence and selection, without claiming that the mythic and phenomenological accounts reduce to physics.

Read across the rows and the family resemblance is visible. Read down the columns and you see what each idiom is for. Peterson dramatizes how the frontier feels, Chapman analyzes a way of perceiving and inhabiting it, and my framework proposes a structural description of pattern formation and dissolution. The similarities motivate comparison; they do not establish that all three have one literal referent.

Coherence does the unifying work here by directing attention to the persistence of pattern under specified change. Some forms of persistence are measurable; others remain model-dependent. Truth cannot be reduced to invariance alone, but invariance across relevant tests is evidence that a representation tracks more than one local presentation. Peterson’s confrontation and Chapman’s balance need not be secretly about one physical quantity for this comparison to illuminate their shared concern with structure and flux.

The Cycle of Emergence

The three maps converge most tightly on a single point: none of the poles is stable, and each degrades in its own characteristic way. Order hardens into tyranny. Form ossifies into delusion. Coherence decays into noise. In every case renewal requires fresh contact with the flux — Peterson’s dragon, Chapman’s nebulosity, my entropy reservoir. Structure that seals itself off from chaos does not stay pure; it dies. The cycle is a loop:

Chaos → Form → Coherence → Decay → Chaos.

Each pass refines what survives within the comparison. Undifferentiated possibility gets crystallized into provisional structure; structure that holds under relevant transformation counts as coherent; structures may decay when their sustaining conditions disappear. This cycle is a heuristic shared across psychological, cultural, and physical examples, not one demonstrated mechanism or conserved quantity. Myth can inspire an algorithm, stance can inform a model, and meaning can be represented through information without reducing any first term to the second.

This gives the ethical comparison its shape. For Peterson the virtue is courage; for Chapman it is flexibility; in my framework it is constructive agency — maintaining coherence adaptively, neither worshipping order nor fearing chaos. The three can overlap without being identical. They emphasize, respectively, the nerve to face the unknown, the suppleness to move with it, and the skill to build durable pattern from it. The constructor is my synthesis: an agent who rebuilds order while remembering that coherence is indexed to transformations and conditions, not a warrant for whatever happens to endure.

And here this chapter’s account of meaning comes into view. Meaning is not imposed from above or found as a finished cosmic message. It is generated in a relation between a modeling agent, its purposes, and a world that constrains which interpretations continue to work. This develops the account in The Boundary of Revelation: meaning surfaces at the interface where a modeling system meets more world than it can contain. Biological, cognitive, and social systems all stabilize patterns, but only agents with the relevant capacities interpret those patterns as meaningful. The analogy does not make every self-maintaining system conscious.

The Synthesis Proper

Now apply the method a second time, at higher resolution, to the two frameworks that survive the comparison as live theories of value rather than as myth: Chapman’s Meaningness and my own. On the surface they look like rivals. Meaningness emphasizes participation — the fluid attunement to unfolding patterns. My framework emphasizes agency — the structured dynamics by which a mind preserves coherence and projects itself through time. One is phenomenological and fluid, the other structural and architectural. It is tempting to see two competing theories.

They are not competing. They are two views of one process, and to see it you first have to accept the premise both of them share. There is no cosmic scoreboard. The universe does not tell us what matters; there is no hidden ledger of right and wrong written into the fabric of reality. Most people, hearing this, recoil in one of two directions — toward nihilism, nothing means anything, or toward eternalism, meaning must be fixed, objective, and universal. Both are evasions of the same fact, panic responses to the absence of the scoreboard. Nihilism concludes that without the ledger nothing counts; eternalism insists the ledger must exist and goes looking for it in metaphysics, in God, in the state, in whatever institution can be made to stand in for the missing cosmic authority. The two look like opposites and are secretly the same error: both assume that if meaning is not handed down from outside, it cannot be real. Strip that assumption and both collapse. The whole prior volume’s engagement with contingency — the refusal, in Beyond Clown World, to read the loss of absolutes as collapse — turns on refusing both. Meaning is neither fixed nor absent. It is enacted.

That is Chapman’s contribution, and it is a phenomenological one. Meaning arises in interaction — not in the world alone, not in the self alone, but in the interface. It is neither discovered nor invented but brought forth through participation. Freeze it into eternal categories and you generate confusion; deny its reality and you generate despair. Meaning is not a substance. It is a relation. Meaningness is superb at this: it describes how meaning surfaces as we engage with patterns beyond our control, it dismantles the false choice between fixed and nonexistent, and it diagnoses the stance errors — eternalism, nihilism, and the rest — that distort lived experience. It offers clarity about how confusion arises.

My framework begins somewhere else entirely. Its central primitive is the agent: an entity that maintains itself by modeling, choosing, and acting across time. An agent needs sufficient coherence to persist, and involuntary constraints can collapse its range of moves. The value volume adds an explicit normative commitment to protecting sapient agency, then defines harm and coercion under specified conditions. Those ethical verdicts do not fall from system dynamics alone; they combine facts about agency with value commitments this volume eventually states in the Credo. I use that conditional framework here rather than re-deriving it.

So: one framework phenomenological and fluid, one structural and architectural. The contrast is real — until you examine how either process actually functions, at which point it dissolves.

Agency requires participation. An agent cannot form predictions without coupling to an environment, cannot update beliefs without feedback, and cannot maintain a stable identity without continuing interaction. Agency is not a sealed monad choosing from the inside. It is an embedded control process sustained by what it encounters. Participation is therefore a condition of agency, not a competing definition of it.

Participation requires agency. The moment you describe attunement, responsiveness, improvisation, you have already presupposed selective attention, intentional arcs, sensitivity to some patterns rather than others. Participation is not passive absorption and it is not dissolution into the flux. It is a way of acting. Participation is exogenous agency.

Each supposedly opposed term turns out to contain the other. The mind is a single dynamical loop viewed from two angles. From the inside it appears as choosing, intending, maintaining coherence — agency. From the outside it appears as attunement, responsiveness, co-creation — participation. Not rival explanations. Dual aspects of one continuous process. This is the synthesis in the strict Wilberian sense: it transcends the opposition by relocating it as a difference of vantage on a single thing, and it includes both descriptions because a full account needs both.

Consider a jazz improviser. She listens to the ensemble — shifts in harmony, microtiming, dynamics — and responds to cues she does not control. That is participation: attunement to patterns beyond her. Simultaneously she selects motifs, shapes musical arcs, holds a coherent identity across the unfolding piece. That is agency: projecting viable futures and choosing among them. The music exists only because attunement and projection are not two operations taking turns but one loop, each feeding the other in real time. Pull them apart and there is no music — only noise, or a metronome.

Here is the proposed division of labor. Meaningness foregrounds the phenomenology of participation — how meaning feels, surfaces, and is lived. Axio foregrounds a structural model — how some agents preserve organization, project alternatives, and act. The model does not prove why suffering has one essence, why every coercive order must fail, or which kinds of mind the universe permits. The comparison is useful insofar as each account exposes omissions in the other.

Which yields the formulation the whole chapter has been building toward, and the one I will carry forward unchanged:

Meaning can emerge through participation between agents and world; value enters when valuers choose what patterns and relationships to sustain.

The universe contains no moral scoreboard. Minds evaluate, but not necessarily by maximizing coherence or propagation. My synthesis treats participation and agency as mutually informing aspects of meaning-making: reality answers back, while agents select and revise what matters to them. That is a proposed integration, not a result secured by system dynamics alone.

This is where the Chapman engagement, previewed as far back as The Secular Sacred, lands. Values and coherent patterns sometimes propagate across agents and time, but survival is evidence of transmission, not of authority. That distinction carries into the avowed meta-myth of The Great Unfolding. Meaning is made in the exchange between participating agents and a reality that constrains them; it is not discovered on a cosmic scoreboard.
