The Hidden Depths of Simple Advice

Folk maxims as disciplines of attention

This chapter is a draft — it is readable but still changing.

A mother says it to a child who is watching his own feet, or a passing dog, or nothing at all, while walking straight toward a lamppost: look where you’re going. It is the first piece of philosophy most of us receive, and we receive it before we can spell. We hear it as a correction about lampposts. It is that, but it is not only that. The terse formulas a culture hands down — the mother’s warning, the schoolyard’s mind your own business, the maxims the Greeks carved into stone at Delphi — are compressed on purpose. They survive because they are short enough to remember and deep enough to reward a lifetime of unpacking. Read carelessly they are nagging. Read carefully they are disciplines: of attention, of boundary, of restraint. This chapter reads them carefully.

The point is not that ancient wisdom secretly encodes my philosophy. The point is the reverse. When a maxim has been polished by ten thousand years of use until only the load-bearing words remain, and you follow those words down to the bottom, you tend to arrive at the same few structural facts about agency that any honest account arrives at. The maxims are terse because they have to be portable. They are deep because attention, boundary, and measure are deep.

Look Where You’re Going

Start at the surface and descend. Each layer is already contained in the sentence; we only have to stop treating it as advice about lampposts.

The literal layer is spatial: attend to your immediate environment so you do not walk into things. This is the cornerstone of ordinary competence, and it is not trivial — most physical harm to small children is a failure at exactly this level. Basic safety is the floor, not the ceiling.

The cognitive layer is about attention itself. Human perception is selective; what you notice is largely determined by what you have chosen to look toward. To look where you’re going is to direct attention deliberately rather than let it be captured. And because attention is the intake valve for cognition, steering it does not merely change what you see — it changes what you can think. The person who cannot govern where his gaze lands cannot govern his mind, because his mind is downstream of his gaze.

The trajectory layer turns spatial into metaphorical. Where the mental gaze lands, action follows. A goal is a place you keep looking toward until your movements start to arrange themselves around it. This is why clarity of intention is not a motivational nicety but a mechanical one: an undefined outcome cannot organize behavior, and a person who has never decided where he is going will be moved instead by whatever is loudest.

The temporal layer adds the future. To look where you are going is to look ahead — to anticipate the outcome of the present step before you take it, and adjust. Planning, strategy, and foresight are all this one habit extended in time: run the trajectory forward, see where it lands, correct now while correction is cheap.

The ethical layer widens the frame from your path to everyone standing on it. Your trajectory has consequences for others, and moral maturity is in large part the acquired capacity to see those consequences before they arrive rather than to discover them in the wreckage. To foresee the impact of where you are heading, and to let that foresight bend your course, is most of what compassion amounts to in practice.

The existential layer returns the whole thing to agency. The direction of your gaze is not given to you; you set it, and in setting it you declare what you value. A life is a long trajectory, and to choose where to look is to choose, cumulatively, what that life will have been about.

There is one more layer, and it is where this maxim quietly touches the rest of my project. If your vantage — your present standpoint and your intentional focus — genuinely shapes the trajectory you travel through the space of possible futures, then attention is not merely how you observe your path. It is, in part, how you lay it. That is the claim developed at length elsewhere in the Quantum Branching Universe and in conditionalist epistemology, and I will not re-derive it here. It is enough to notice that a mother chasing a toddler down a sidewalk has, without knowing it, handed him the whole of it: reality, in the sense that matters to a living agent, emerges from where he chooses to look.

Mind Your Own Business

The schoolyard version arrives with a sting on it, which is a shame, because it is built on exactly the same staircase — the difference is that where look where you’re going is a discipline of attention, mind your own business is a discipline of boundary. Run the same descent.

Literally it asks for respect of personal space: do not interfere in what is not yours to manage. This is the grease that lets people live at close quarters without constant friction, and civil coexistence is largely made of it.

Psychologically it is a prescription for sanity. Preoccupation with other people’s problems is a reliable engine of stress, anxiety, and resentment, and much of it is not even help — it is the mind refusing to stay in its own lane. Keeping your attention on what is actually yours to carry is not selfishness; it is the maintenance schedule for a functioning nervous system.

Socially it names why boundaries build rather than break relationships. Respecting another person’s autonomy is what earns their trust; overstepping it, however warm the motive, breeds the resentment that erodes the bond you were trying to strengthen. Restraint is not distance. It is the condition under which closeness stays healthy.

Economically it is comparative advantage in four words. Everyone prospers by concentrating on what they do best and leaving to others what others do better. A person or an institution that insists on minding everyone’s business does all of it worse, and starves the thing it was actually equipped to do well.

Ethically it defends autonomy as an intrinsic good. To respect another’s choices even when you would choose otherwise is to acknowledge their standing as an agent, not a project. This is the maxim’s sharpest edge, and it is aimed at paternalism: at coercion and unwanted interference dressed up as helpfulness. The fuller argument for why autonomy sits where it does belongs to the value theory developed earlier in the book; here the maxim carries the practical instruction, which is to keep your hands off lives that are not yours to run.

Existentially it locates the origin of meaning in the person whose life it is. Purpose is not a substance one person can install in another. Each life is its own to navigate, and the attempt to impose your answers on someone else’s existential questions is not generosity — it is a category error about where meaning comes from. Concentrate on authoring your own; leave others the same dignity.

And at the bottom, the same structural fact surfaces from the other side. If each agent’s experienced reality emerges from his own vantage — his own conditions, choices, and interpretations — then the place where you can actually move the world is your own life. Mind your own business is not a counsel of indifference. It is a recognition that your leverage on reality begins, and is largest, exactly where you stand. The two maxims turn out to be one instruction seen from two angles: govern your attention, and keep it where it belongs.

Apollo’s Inscriptions

The folk maxims descend from the everyday into the philosophical. The Greeks worked in the other direction: they took the philosophical and compressed it until it would fit on a temple wall. Three maxims were inscribed in the forecourt of the Temple of Apollo at Delphi, and between them they form a triad of restraint — each one a guardrail against a distinct human folly.

Γνῶθι σεαυτόν — know thyself. This is the demand for self-examination: honest recognition of your own nature, capacities, and limits. It comes first because it is the precondition for everything else. An agent ignorant of his own capabilities and desires is not free; he is enslaved by delusion, moved by forces he has not troubled to name. Socrates’ famous line — that the unexamined life is not worth living — is a later echo of this older, terser call to clarity. You cannot govern what you have not looked at, and the first thing to look at is yourself.

Μηδὲν ἄγαν — nothing in excess. This is the principle of moderation: the refusal of extremity in thought, in desire, in action. Excess corrupts even noble aims; the virtue pursued without measure curdles into its own vice. The Greek word for the balance being counseled is sophrosyne, temperance — and it is worth insisting that this is not weakness or timidity. It is the active art of holding competing drives in harmony, which takes more strength than surrendering to any one of them. Aristotle would later systematize the thought as the Golden Mean, but the Delphic formula anticipates him with greater austerity: all things must be bounded, lest they turn against the one who holds them.

Ἐγγύα πάρα δ’ Ἄτα — surety brings ruin. On its face this is mundane financial advice: do not stand as guarantor for another’s debt. But beneath the prudence about money lies a universal law of agency. To pledge beyond your power is to invite catastrophe. Ἄτα — Átē, ruin personified — visits those who overcommit, who bind their agency in obligations they cannot control. The overextension may be material, political, or moral; the mechanism is the same. Commit more than you command, and collapse is not a risk but a schedule.

Taken together the three form a single arc. Know thyself counsels an inward honesty; nothing in excess, a measured balance; surety brings ruin, a prudent vigilance about what you take on. Each guards a different door — against self-ignorance, against intemperance, against reckless overcommitment — and the three doors open onto the same room. Civilizations rise and fall, but the boundaries of reason do not move. These are the guardrails without which freedom collapses into chaos, which is the through-line of everything in this part of the book: agency without discipline is not more agency, it is less. This is the same recovery move I make against the Stoics — take the discipline, drop the resignation — and the same reason wisdom, not raw power, is what sagency names. The Delphic triad is that thesis, cut into stone twenty-four centuries before I got to it.

The Mantra

The maxims so far are correctives: warnings against a specific error, spoken when you are about to make it. But wisdom also needs a forward form — not just what to avoid but what to keep doing. A four-beat mantra will serve:

Wake Up Show Up Level Up Grow Up

Wake up is awareness: the shift from being run by impulse and inherited assumption to noticing that you are running at all. Show up is participation: reliable, accountable engagement with the tasks and people that are actually in front of you. Level up is competence: the deliberate accumulation of skill, the refusal to stay as capable as you were last year. Grow up is maturation: the emotional and moral deepening that turns capacity into wisdom, and that mostly consists of accepting complexity, trade-off, and your own limits without flinching.

What makes this more than a motivational slogan is that it is not a ladder you climb once. It is a cycle you re-enter at every stage of a life, and each element means something different depending on where you stand.

For a child, wake up is the first dawning of self-awareness, the discovery that one is a self at all; show up is learning to attend school and keep a promise; level up is language and number and the basic social skills; grow up is the first grasp of cause and effect and where one person ends and another begins.

For a young adult the same four words turn outward and sharpen. Wake up becomes the critical examination of inherited belief and the conscious choice of an identity — the questioning of authority that is the beginning of genuine selfhood. Show up becomes the weight of real responsibility: work, relationships, a stake in the common life. Level up becomes specialization and the pursuit of expertise. Grow up becomes the confrontation with real trade-offs and ethical complexity, the exchange of youthful absolutes for load-bearing judgment.

In middle age the cycle deepens again. Wake up is the midlife reckoning — revisiting the direction of a life, confronting stagnation, renewing purpose. Show up is commitment to the deep roles of family, community, and leadership, becoming one of the pillars others lean on. Level up is the mastery that lets experience be converted into mentorship. Grow up is the arrival of a genuine wisdom marked by humility and the acceptance of one’s own limits.

And in the last stage it turns toward completion. Wake up is the honest appraisal of a finite life and the meaning made within it; show up is presence and contribution carried on through decline, which often carries the most moral weight of all; level up is the cultivation of philosophical or spiritual depth; grow up is equanimity — reconciliation with complexity, and the acceptance of one’s own story’s ending with integrity and grace.

The same four words, revisited across a life, describe the work of continually re-authoring the self that the rest of this volume calls growing up in earnest. This is why the mantra is cyclical rather than linear: you never finish waking up, and the version of showing up that a child can manage is not the version a dying person is asked for. It is a structured framework that stays flexible precisely because its terms refill with new content each time you return to them.

Which returns us to where a mother started us, chasing a toddler down a sidewalk. The wisdom a culture compresses into its shortest sayings is not simple; it is compressed, which is the opposite. Look where you are going, and mind your own business, and know yourself, and want nothing to excess, and do not pledge past your strength — and wake, show, level, and grow, again and again, for as long as you last. None of it is metaphysics. All of it is the discipline of a finite agent learning to point his one available instrument, attention, at the things that are actually his to move. That the instructions fit on a temple wall, or into the mouth of a tired parent, is not a sign that they are shallow. It is a sign that they are true enough to have survived.
